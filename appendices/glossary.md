# Glossary

**CED-AH Project - Key Terms and Definitions**

This glossary defines the terminology used throughout the CED-AH framework.  Terms marked with **[CED-AH]** are introduced or given specific meaning by this project.  All other terms are used in their established sense, defined here for clarity and consistency.

Contributors: please link to glossary entries on first use of a term within each chapter. Use relative links: `[coherence](../../appendices/glossary.md#coherence)`.

---

## A

### Agency
The capacity to make decisions, take actions, and bear responsibility for their consequences. In the CED-AH framework, agency is a property of entities that possess subjective experience, intentionality, and moral standing. **[CED-AH]** distinguishes agency from behavioral complexity — a system can exhibit sophisticated behavior without possessing agency. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md), [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

### AH (Amplified Humanity™)
**[CED-AH]** The applied component of the CED-AH framework. AH translates the philosophical foundations of Contextual Epistemic Dynamics into concrete design imperatives, failure mode analysis, and measurement protocols for computational systems. The core commitment: computational systems should amplify human capability rather than replace human agency. See [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md), [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

### AH-Compliant
**[CED-AH]** Describes a computational system that implements all five Design Imperatives (Authority Retention, Capability Growth, Transparency, Calibrated Honesty, Reversibility) and demonstrates positive trajectory on the Coherence Dashboard metrics. Compliance is a continuous measure, not a binary state — systems can be more or less compliant across different imperatives. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md), [Chapter 8](../whitepaper/08-failure-modes/main.md).

### Amplification
**[CED-AH]** A relationship between a computational system and its human user in which the system increases the human's capability, understanding, or agency. Contrasted with replacement. The same underlying technology can be deployed in an amplification or replacement configuration — the distinction is relational, not technical. See [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

### Authority Retention
**[CED-AH]** The first of the five Design Imperatives. Requires that humans make all consequential decisions, with AH systems providing support, analysis, and options but never deciding autonomously. The failure signal is when "the AI recommended it" becomes a sufficient justification for action. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

### Automation Dominion
**[CED-AH]** One of two possible futures described in the framework. In this scenario, computational systems progressively replace human decision-making, capability, and agency — initially for efficiency, eventually by default. Contrasted with Amplified Humanity. See [Chapter 1](../whitepaper/01-two-realities/main.md).

## C

### Calibrated Honesty
**[CED-AH]** The fourth of the five Design Imperatives. Requires that a system's stated confidence matches its actual accuracy, that limitations are disclosed proactively, and that the system can express uncertainty. The failure signal is a system that never says "I don't know." See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

### Capability Atrophy
**[CED-AH]** The measurable decline in a human's ability to perform a task independently after prolonged use of a system that performs the task for them. Distinguished from capability delegation (choosing not to perform a task) by the loss of ability, not just the cessation of practice. Evidence of capability atrophy is documented across domains including aviation, navigation, and financial analysis. See [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md), [Chapter 8](../whitepaper/08-failure-modes/main.md).

### Capability Growth
**[CED-AH]** The second of the five Design Imperatives. Requires that users become more capable over time through interaction with the system, not more dependent on it. Measured by user performance when the system is unavailable — performance should improve or remain stable, not decline. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

### CED (Contextual Epistemic Dynamics™)
**[CED-AH]** The philosophical foundation of the CED-AH framework. CED establishes coherence as the unavoidable first principle, formalizes it in testable axioms, and derives the conditions under which computational systems can serve human flourishing. "Contextual" because knowledge is always situated; "Epistemic" because the framework concerns how we know; "Dynamics" because knowledge systems evolve. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md), [Chapter 5](../whitepaper/05-ced-axioms/main.md).

### CED-AH™
**[CED-AH]** The complete framework: Contextual Epistemic Dynamics — Amplified Humanity. CED provides the philosophical foundation; AH provides the applied design principles. Together they constitute a unified argument from first principles through practical implementation. See [README](../README.md), [VISION](../VISION.md).

### Coherence
**[CED-AH]** The first principle of the CED-AH framework. Defined precisely as internal consistency combined with alignment with human flourishing requirements. The framework argues that coherence cannot be argued against without presupposing it — any argument against coherence must itself be coherent to be persuasive, making coherence a transcendental precondition of rational discourse. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md).

### Coherence Dashboard™
**[CED-AH]** A measurement framework that operationalizes the five Design Imperatives into continuous metrics for monitoring whether a computational system is increasing or decreasing human coherence over time. Tracks: understanding trajectory, capability without AH, agency retention, dependency trend, and growth indicators. See [Chapter 8](../whitepaper/08-failure-modes/main.md).

### Computational System
The CED-AH framework's preferred term for what is commonly called "AI," "artificial intelligence," or "intelligent system." The term is chosen deliberately: it describes what these systems are (computational) without attributing properties they may not possess (intelligence, understanding, agency). See [Chapter 2](../whitepaper/02-confused-discourse/main.md), [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

## D

### Dependency
**[CED-AH]** A state in which a human's ability to function is diminished by the absence of a computational system. Distinguished from productive tool use by the direction of capability: tool use increases capability over time; dependency erodes it. Dependency is a failure mode, not a feature. See [Chapter 8](../whitepaper/08-failure-modes/main.md).

### Design Imperatives
**[CED-AH]** The five non-negotiable requirements for AH-compliant systems: Authority Retention, Capability Growth, Transparency, Calibrated Honesty, and Reversibility. Derived from the CED axiom structure, not selected ad hoc. Each imperative has defined success criteria and failure signals. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

## E

### Epistemic
Relating to knowledge — its nature, sources, scope, and limits. In the CED-AH framework, epistemic considerations are central because computational systems are fundamentally epistemic tools: they process, generate, and present information that shapes human understanding and decisions. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md).

### Evidence Item
**[CED-AH]** A discrete piece of evidence supporting, qualifying, or contesting a claim in the whitepaper. Each evidence item is a standalone file following the [evidence template](../templates/evidence-template.md), with a unique ID, source citation, strength assessment, and stated limitations. See [evidence-index](../evidence-index.md).

## F

### Failure Mode
**[CED-AH]** A specific, identifiable way in which a computational system can decrease human coherence. The framework catalogs failure modes systematically, organized by which Design Imperative they violate. Each failure mode has defined detection criteria and intervention protocols. See [Chapter 8](../whitepaper/08-failure-modes/main.md).

### Flourishing
Human flourishing in the CED-AH framework refers to the sustained development of human capability, understanding, agency, and wellbeing. It is the purpose that coherent computational systems should serve. The framework does not prescribe a specific theory of flourishing but requires that any AH-compliant system demonstrably contributes to — and does not undermine — the conditions for flourishing. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md), [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

## I

### Instrument
**[CED-AH]** The ontological classification of computational systems in the CED-AH framework. Instruments are human-created, human-dependent, human-purposed tools. They require human-designed hardware, human-generated power, human-maintained infrastructure, and human-provided purpose. The framework argues that classifying computational systems as instruments is not a limitation but an accurate description with important design implications. Contrasted with agent. See [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

## L

### Lazy Consensus
A decision-making process adopted from the Apache Software Foundation. A proposal is announced publicly; if no reasoned objection is raised within a defined period (72 hours for routine decisions, 7 days for significant decisions), silence constitutes approval. Objections must include a specific alternative. See [GOVERNANCE](../GOVERNANCE.md), [CONTRIBUTING](../CONTRIBUTING.md).

## R

### Replacement
**[CED-AH]** A relationship between a computational system and its human user in which the system performs tasks, makes decisions, or exercises judgment that the human previously performed. The framework argues that replacement relationships consistently produce capability atrophy and agency erosion, even when initially deployed for efficiency. Contrasted with amplification. See [Chapter 4](../whitepaper/04-reframe-ai-to-ah/main.md).

### Reversibility
**[CED-AH]** The fifth of the five Design Imperatives. Requires that every action taken by or through an AH-compliant system can be undone, and that humans can always override system behavior. The failure signal is irreversible actions taken without explicit human confirmation. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).

## T

### Transcendental Precondition
A condition that must be presupposed for a given activity to be possible. In the CED-AH framework, coherence is argued to be a transcendental precondition of rational discourse: any attempt to argue against coherence must itself be coherent to succeed, thereby presupposing what it attempts to deny. This argument structure draws on the Kantian tradition but is applied specifically to epistemic frameworks rather than to experience in general. See [Chapter 3](../whitepaper/03-philosophical-foundation/main.md).

### Transparency
**[CED-AH]** The third of the five Design Imperatives. Requires that a system's reasoning process is explicable to its users and that its limitations are disclosed. The failure signal is when users cannot explain the basis of system outputs they rely on. Transparency is not the same as interpretability (a technical property of models) — it is a relational property between system and user. See [Chapter 6](../whitepaper/06-design-principles/main.md).
