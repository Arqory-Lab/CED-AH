# Contributing to CED-AH

Thank you for your interest in contributing to CED-AH.  This project improves through structured, evidence based engagement from people across disciplines.  Philosophy, computer science, cognitive science, policy, mathematics, and beyond.  We designed the contribution process to be clear, asynchronous, and respectful of everyone's time.

## Before You Start

1. **Read the [README](README.md)** for an overview of the project and the framework.
2. **Review the [CLA](CLA.md)** - you'll be asked to sign our Contributor License Agreement on your first pull request.  It preserves your copyright while granting The Arqory Research Institute the rights needed to maintain and distribute the project.  You sign once; it covers all future contributions.
3. **Check existing issues and discussions** before opening a new one.  Your question or idea may already be in progress.

## How to Contribute

There are three primary ways to engage with this project.  Each has a dedicated template to keep contributions structured and reviewable.

### 1. Challenge a Claim

Every substantive claim in the CED-AH whitepaper is linked to supporting evidence and is open to formal challenge.  If you believe a claim is wrong, unsupported, or incomplete, we want to know.

**Use the [Challenge Evidence template](.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/challenge-evidence.md).**

A strong challenge includes:

- The specific claim being challenged (chapter and section reference)
- Counter evidence with citations (academic papers, empirical data, formal proofs)
- The scope of impact - does the counter evidence weaken the claim, invalidate it, or require a different formulation?

Successful challenges are among the most valuable contributions to this project.  They improve rigor.  The framework is designed to withstand scrutiny, not avoid it.

### 2. Add Evidence

The evidence base is intentionally incomplete.  We publish what we have and invite extension.  If you have evidence that supports, qualifies, or contextualizes a claim in the whitepaper, from any discipline, we want it.

**Use the [Add Evidence template](.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/add-evidence.md).**

Evidence submissions should include:

- The claim or chapter the evidence relates to
- Evidence type: philosophical, historical, mathematical, empirical, or technical
- Full citation and, where possible, a link to the source material
- A brief summary of why this evidence is relevant

Evidence is categorized by type and filed within the relevant chapter's `evidence/` directory.  See [evidence-index.md](evidence-index.md) for the current evidence base and organizational structure.

### 3. Provide Feedback

For everything else - unclear writing, missed implications, domain specific concerns, structural suggestions, questions about the framework, general feedback is welcome.

**Use the [Feedback template](.github/ISSUE_TEMPLATE/feedback.md).**

No specific format required.  Tell us what you're thinking.

## Pull Requests

If you want to contribute directly by fixing errors, improving clarity, adding evidence files, or extending documentation, pull requests are welcome.

### Process

1. **Fork the repository** and create a branch from `main`.
2. **Make your changes.**  Follow the style and structure of existing documents.  If you're adding a new evidence item, use the [evidence template](templates/evidence-template.md).
3. **Write a clear PR description.**  Explain what you changed and why.  Link to any related issues.
4. **Sign the CLA.**  Our CLA bot will prompt you on your first PR.  You authenticate with GitHub, review the agreement, and sign electronically.
5. **Submit.**  PRs are reviewed and merged by the project maintainer.

### What Makes a Good PR

- **One concern per PR.**  Don't combine an evidence addition with a structural reorganization. Smaller, focused PRs get reviewed faster.
- **Cite your sources.**  If you're adding or modifying claims, link to supporting evidence.
- **Respect existing structure.**  Each chapter has a consistent organization: `main.md` for the chapter text, `README.md` for navigation, and `evidence/` for supporting materials.  Work within this structure unless your PR is specifically proposing a structural change.
- **Don't modify auto generated files.**  `whitepaper-complete.md` and `evidence-index.md` are generated by CI workflows.  Change the source files; the generated files will update automatically.

## Style and Standards

### Writing

CED-AH is a philosophical and technical project . The writing should be precise, clear, and accessible to an educated reader who may not share your discipline's assumptions or vocabulary.

- Define terms before using them.  If a term is in the [glossary](appendices/glossary.md), link to it on first use within a chapter.
- Prefer concrete examples over abstract claims.
- State assumptions explicitly.  If an argument depends on a premise, say so.
- Distinguish between what the framework claims and what the evidence demonstrates.  Use language like "the evidence suggests" or "we argue that" rather than stating contested positions as fact.

### Evidence

Every evidence item follows a consistent structure (see [evidence template](templates/evidence-template.md)):

- **Evidence ID** - unique identifier within the chapter (e.g., `phil-003`, `emp-012`)
- **Title** - descriptive name
- **Type** - philosophical, historical, mathematical, empirical, or technical
- **Source** - full citation
- **Summary** - what the evidence shows
- **Relevance** - how it connects to the chapter's claims
- **Strength** - the contributor's assessment of evidential strength (strong, moderate, suggestive)
- **Limitations** - what the evidence does not show

### Formatting

- Markdown only.  No proprietary formats.
- One sentence per line in source files (makes diffs readable).
- Links should be relative within the repository where possible.
- Images, if used, go in an `assets/` directory within the relevant chapter.

## Issue Labels

We use a structured label system to keep issues organized and make it easy to find work that matches your interests.

**Area** - what part of the project:
- `area:safety-framework` - the five design imperatives, failure modes, measurement
- `area:philosophy` - coherence, epistemics, first principles
- `area:evidence` - the evidence base supporting claims
- `area:infrastructure` - repository tooling, CI, templates
- `area:documentation` - writing quality, structure, navigation

**Kind** - what type of work:
- `kind:challenge` - a formal claim challenge
- `kind:evidence` - new evidence submission
- `kind:bug` - something broken or incorrect
- `kind:enhancement` - improvement to existing content
- `kind:documentation` - clarification or expansion
- `kind:question` - resolved through GitHub Discussions, not Issues

**Priority:**
- `P0:critical` - factual errors, broken links, CI failures
- `P1:important` - substantive challenges, significant evidence gaps
- `P2:normal`- enhancements, additional evidence, clarity improvements
- `P3:low` - nice to haves, cosmetic changes

**Status:**
- `status:needs-triage` - new, not yet reviewed
- `status:accepted` - reviewed and approved for work
- `status:in-progress` - someone is actively working on it
- `status:blocked` - waiting on something external

**Contributor experience:**
- `good-first-issue` - well-scoped, self contained, good for newcomers
- `help-wanted` - we'd welcome a contribution here
- `mentor-available` - a maintainer will provide guidance

## Governance

### Current Model: BDFL

CED-AH is in its founding phase.  The project operates under a Benevolent Dictator for Life (BDFL) governance model.  Final decisions on framework direction, evidence inclusion, and contribution acceptance rest with the project founder.

This is standard for early stage open source projects (Linux, Python, Vue.js, and Clojure all started this way) and will evolve as the contributor community grows.

### Decision Making: Lazy Consensus

Day to day decisions follow the Apache Software Foundation's lazy consensus model:

1. **Propose.** State your intent publicly in an issue or PR.
2. **Wait 72 hours.** Silence is consent.
3. **Proceed** unless someone raises a reasoned objection with a specific alternative.
4. **If objections arise**, the maintainer decides after considering all perspectives.

This shifts the burden from "get permission" to "object if you disagree", which keeps the project moving without requiring active approval for every change.

### What Lazy Consensus Covers

- Evidence additions that follow the template
- Documentation improvements and error corrections
- New issue templates or workflow improvements
- Minor structural changes within existing chapters

### What Requires Explicit Approval

- New chapters or major structural reorganization
- Changes to the axiom structure or core philosophical claims
- Modifications to licensing, CLA, or governance
- Changes to the trademark policy

## Questions and Discussion

**Questions go to [GitHub Discussions](../../discussions), not Issues.**  Issues are for actionable work like bugs, evidence submissions, challenges, and tracked enhancements.  Discussions are for questions, brainstorming, and open ended conversation.

This distinction keeps the issue tracker clean and ensures actionable work doesn't get buried under conversation threads.

---

## Response Times

This project is maintained by a small team.  We review issues and PRs regularly, but response times depend on priority and complexity.

- **P0 (critical):** Within 48 hours.
- **P1 (important):** Within one week.
- **P2-P3:** Best effort, typically within two weeks.

Issues with no activity for 60 days will be marked `stale`.  Issues with no activity for 74 days after being marked stale will be closed. Priority labeled issues (`P0`, `P1`) are exempt from auto closing. Closed issues can be reopened if circumstances change.
