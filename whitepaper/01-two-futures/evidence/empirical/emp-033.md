## Summary

Thomas Davenport and Julia Kirby's *Only Humans Need Apply* is the closest existing business-strategy book to CED-AH's amplification thesis — and the most practical articulation of the augmentation paradigm from within the management literature. Davenport, one of the world's most cited business scholars, and Kirby, editor-at-large at *Harvard Business Review*, argue that the dominant corporate narrative about AI — automate everything, reduce headcount, maximize efficiency — is strategically self-defeating. Organizations that replace human workers with automation achieve short-term cost savings but lose the adaptive capacity, creative capability, and contextual judgment that human workers provide.

The book's central argument is captured in its title-echoing phrase: "Don't automate, augment." Davenport and Kirby propose that organizations should use AI to augment human workers rather than to replace them — making each worker more capable, more productive, and more valuable rather than making workers unnecessary.

The book provides a taxonomy of five augmentation strategies — five ways that individuals and organizations can position themselves in relation to increasingly capable AI:

1. **Step up.** Move to a higher level of cognitive work — big-picture thinking, strategic judgment, ethical decision-making — that AI cannot do. Use AI to handle the analytical work that supports these higher-level functions.

2. **Step aside.** Focus on capabilities AI lacks: interpersonal skills, emotional intelligence, physical dexterity, creative expression. Use AI for the routine cognitive components of the work while performing the irreducibly human components.

3. **Step in.** Understand AI's capabilities and limitations deeply enough to improve, monitor, and manage AI systems. Become the human-in-the-loop that makes AI systems effective.

4. **Step narrowly.** Find specific niches where human expertise is uniquely valuable and where automation is not cost-effective. Specialize in areas that require deep domain knowledge combined with contextual judgment.

5. **Step forward.** Build the next generation of AI systems — design, develop, and deploy the technology. This requires both technical and humanistic skills: understanding what technology can do and what humans need.

Davenport and Kirby provide case studies of organizations that have pursued augmentation strategies — law firms that use AI for document review while freeing lawyers for client interaction; financial advisory firms that use AI for portfolio analysis while advisors focus on client relationships; healthcare organizations that use AI for diagnostic support while physicians focus on patient care.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification thesis from within the business and management literature — the closest existing articulation. Davenport and Kirby's "augment, don't automate" is CED-AH's "amplify, don't replace" stated in business-strategy language. CED-AH provides the philosophical foundation (coherence as first principle) that Davenport and Kirby's practical framework lacks; Davenport and Kirby provide the business-strategy operationalization that CED-AH's abstract principles need.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth). Davenport and Kirby's five augmentation strategies are all strategies for human capability growth: step up (develop higher-level judgment), step aside (develop uniquely human skills), step in (develop AI management expertise), step narrowly (deepen domain expertise), step forward (develop design and development capability). Each strategy responds to AI by developing *more* human capability, not less — which is exactly what CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative requires.

**Provides the business case** for CED-AH's framework. CED-AH argues that amplification is the coherent design paradigm. Davenport and Kirby argue that augmentation is the strategically superior business approach. The convergence is significant: the normatively correct approach (CED-AH's coherence argument) is also the strategically effective approach (Davenport and Kirby's business case). This convergence strengthens both arguments.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention) in organizational context. Davenport and Kirby's augmentation strategies all preserve human authority over work outcomes — AI provides support, analysis, and capability, but humans retain decision-making authority. The "step up" strategy is particularly aligned with Authority Retention: use AI to handle lower-level analytical work while humans exercise higher-level judgment.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by documenting the practical challenges of augmentation. Davenport and Kirby acknowledge that augmentation is harder than automation: it requires redesigning work processes, retraining workers, managing human-AI collaboration, and resisting the cost-cutting temptation to simply replace workers. CED-AH's design imperatives must be practically implementable in organizational contexts where automation is the path of least resistance.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Davenport is one of the most cited management scholars in the world, and the book is grounded in extensive business research and case studies. The augmentation framework has been influential in the business literature on human-AI collaboration. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) the book is written for a business audience and does not meet academic standards of empirical rigor — the case studies are illustrative rather than systematic; (b) the five augmentation strategies are heuristic rather than analytically derived — there is no formal model for when each strategy is appropriate; (c) the book's optimistic tone may understate the difficulty of implementing augmentation in the face of competitive pressure to automate; and (d) the "augmentation" concept, while resonant with CED-AH's amplification thesis, is not philosophically grounded — Davenport and Kirby do not ask *why* augmentation is preferable, only that it is strategically advantageous.

---

## Limitations

- **Business-strategy genre.** The book is prescriptive rather than analytical — it tells organizations what to do, not why augmentation is normatively superior. CED-AH provides the normative argument.
- **Case studies are illustrative, not systematic.** The organizations profiled were selected because they exemplify augmentation strategies, not because they represent a systematic sample. Survivorship bias may overweight success stories.
- **The competitive pressure problem.** In markets where competitors automate, the cost pressure to follow may overwhelm the strategic argument for augmentation. Davenport and Kirby acknowledge this but do not fully resolve it. CED-AH's governance framework (Part 8) addresses this: if augmentation is normatively required, regulation can prevent the race-to-the-bottom dynamic.
- **Does not address the philosophical foundations.** Davenport and Kirby argue that augmentation is strategically superior but do not argue that it is morally required. CED-AH's contribution is the philosophical grounding.
- **Published before the LLM era.** The specific augmentation challenges of generative AI — which can perform creative, analytical, and communicative tasks — may require updating the five-strategy taxonomy.
