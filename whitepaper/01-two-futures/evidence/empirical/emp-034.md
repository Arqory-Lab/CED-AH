## Summary

Raisch and Krakowski's article in the *Academy of Management Review* provides the most rigorous academic analysis of the paradox at the heart of CED-AH's framework: that organizations pursuing AI simultaneously experience pressures toward automation (replacing human activity with machine activity) and augmentation (enhancing human activity with machine support) — and that these pressures are genuinely paradoxical, not simply competing options.

The authors define the automation-augmentation paradox as follows: automation and augmentation are not merely different strategies that organizations can choose between. They are interdependent, contradictory, and simultaneously present. Any attempt to augment human work with AI creates opportunities and pressures to automate; any attempt to automate creates new demands for human augmentation. The paradox cannot be resolved by choosing one side — it must be managed dynamically.

Raisch and Krakowski identify three specific paradoxical dynamics:

**The learning paradox.** AI systems learn from human activity — they require human-generated data, human-labeled examples, and human feedback to improve. But as AI systems become more capable (through learning from human activity), they increasingly replace the human activity from which they learn. Taken to its logical conclusion, this dynamic eliminates the source of the AI system's own improvement.

**The performing paradox.** Organizations that deploy AI for augmentation often find that augmented performance exceeds expectations, which creates pressure to let the AI perform more autonomously — shifting from augmentation toward automation. Conversely, organizations that deploy AI for automation often find that the automated system fails in unexpected ways, requiring human intervention — shifting from automation back toward augmentation.

**The organizing paradox.** Automation and augmentation require fundamentally different organizational structures. Automation requires standardized processes, clear task boundaries, and machine-readable workflows. Augmentation requires flexibility, contextual judgment, and human-AI collaboration that is difficult to standardize. Organizations pursuing both simultaneously face structural contradictions.

The article proposes that the automation-augmentation paradox is best managed through "paradoxical thinking" — the cognitive and organizational capacity to hold contradictory demands simultaneously rather than resolving them prematurely in one direction. This requires organizational leaders who understand both automation and augmentation, governance structures that can shift between modes, and a strategic tolerance for ambiguity that most organizations lack.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's central framing by validating the amplification/replacement distinction as the defining axis of AI deployment — and by showing that it is not simply a one-time design choice but an ongoing paradox that requires continuous management. CED-AH's design imperatives can be understood as the principles for managing this paradox: they ensure that the continuous pull toward automation does not erode the human capabilities that augmentation requires.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that the choice between amplification and replacement is not merely strategic but structural. Raisch and Krakowski show that automation and augmentation create different organizational structures, different learning dynamics, and different performance trajectories. The choice is not just about individual system design — it shapes the entire organizational context in which humans and AI interact.

**Provides the academic management foundation** for CED-AH's framework. While Davenport and Kirby (emp-033) make the popular case for augmentation, Raisch and Krakowski provide the rigorous academic analysis — published in the top management theory journal — that establishes the automation-augmentation tension as a fundamental paradox of AI deployment. For academic audiences, this is the strongest validation of CED-AH's central concern.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by demonstrating that the amplification/replacement distinction is not a simple binary but a dynamic paradox. CED-AH must acknowledge that the boundary between amplification and replacement is not fixed — it shifts over time as AI capabilities evolve, human capabilities develop or atrophy, and organizational contexts change. The design imperatives must be understood as ongoing commitments, not one-time design decisions.

**Extends** CED-AH's analysis through the learning paradox. Raisch and Krakowski's learning paradox — that AI learns from human activity but then replaces the human activity from which it learns — has profound implications for CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative. If AI systems are trained on expert human performance and then deployed to replace that performance, the system's own learning foundation erodes. This is a specific, formally identifiable coherence failure: a system whose long-term effectiveness depends on the very human capability it is designed to replace.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in the *Academy of Management Review* — one of the most prestigious management theory journals — this article has been cited over 400 times since publication and has become a foundational reference in the academic literature on AI and organizations. The paradox framework is rigorously developed through extensive theoretical analysis and is grounded in the well-established paradox theory tradition in management research. The article provides the strongest available academic validation for CED-AH's central concern — that the automation/augmentation choice is consequential, paradoxical, and requires principled management rather than naive optimization.

---

## Limitations

- **Theoretical rather than empirical.** The article develops a theoretical framework but does not test it empirically. The paradoxes it identifies are analytically derived from existing literature and case examples, not from systematic empirical observation. CED-AH should treat this as a theoretical foundation and seek empirical validation.
- **Management-focused.** The article addresses organizations and their strategic choices, not individual system design. CED-AH must translate the organizational-level paradoxes into design-level principles — which the five design imperatives attempt to do.
- **The "paradox management" prescription is vague.** Raisch and Krakowski argue that the paradox must be managed through "paradoxical thinking," but they do not provide specific management tools or decision criteria. CED-AH's design imperatives are more operationally specific.
- **Does not address the philosophical foundations.** The article frames the automation-augmentation tension as a strategic paradox, not as a normative question. CED-AH provides the normative framework (coherence) for resolving the paradox in favor of augmentation when the two pressures conflict.
- **The paradox framing may obscure the normative priority.** By presenting automation and augmentation as equally legitimate poles of a paradox, Raisch and Krakowski may understate CED-AH's argument that augmentation is normatively primary — not just one pole of a paradox to be managed, but the coherent default that should govern unless specific conditions warrant deviation.
