## Summary

Langdon Winner's *The Whale and the Reactor* is a landmark work in the philosophy of technology and science and technology studies (STS). The most famous chapter, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?", argues that technological artifacts and systems are not merely neutral tools awaiting human use but embody political choices, values, and power relationships in their very design.

Winner develops two senses in which artifacts have politics. The first is "inherently political technologies" — technologies whose adoption requires or is strongly compatible with particular kinds of social and political arrangements. Nuclear power, Winner argues, requires centralized authority, restricted access, and expert management. It is not merely that nuclear power *happens* to be managed centrally; its technical characteristics *require* centralization. Similarly, solar power is compatible with (though it does not require) decentralized, democratic arrangements.

The second sense is "politically valenced technologies" — artifacts designed (or that function) to settle a social or political question. Winner's most famous example is Robert Moses' bridges on Long Island, allegedly designed with low overpasses that prevented buses from reaching Jones Beach, thereby excluding lower-income (predominantly Black) New Yorkers who relied on public transit. Whether this specific historical claim is accurate has been debated, but the conceptual point stands: design choices can embed and enforce social arrangements, and they do so in ways that persist long after the original designers' intentions are forgotten.

Winner argues that our failure to recognize the politics of artifacts leads to a systematic blindness: we debate the uses of technology but not the values embedded in its design. This blindness serves the interests of those who control design — technology appears neutral and inevitable, when in fact it embodies specific choices about who benefits, who is excluded, and what kind of social order is perpetuated.

The broader argument of *The Whale and the Reactor* extends beyond artifacts to "technological somnambulism" — the tendency to adopt technologies without reflecting on their consequences for social, political, and moral life. Winner calls for a more deliberate, democratic engagement with technological choices, arguing that technology governance is too important to be left to technologists alone.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's foundational claim that technology design embeds values (Part 1). Winner's "do artifacts have politics?" thesis is the philosophical articulation of CED-AH's claim that "the design choice that matters is amplification vs. replacement." Both arguments insist that technology is not neutral — that the choice of how to design a computational system is a choice about what kind of social, political, and human order to promote. CED-AH's five design imperatives are precisely the kind of deliberate value-embedding Winner calls for.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that systems designed under the agent assumption erode human agency by design. Winner's analysis of how artifacts embed power relationships provides the theoretical framework for understanding *how* this erosion occurs: it is not through malice or conscious intent but through design choices that become normalized and invisible. When a system is designed to make decisions for users (rather than amplifying their decision-making capacity), it embeds a political arrangement — a power relationship between system and user — that CED-AH identifies as harmful.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's argument for the Transparency imperative (Design Imperative 3). Winner's critique of "technological somnambulism" — the unreflective adoption of technologies — parallels CED-AH's insistence that systems must make their reasoning explicable and their limitations disclosed. If users cannot understand what a system does and why, they cannot evaluate the political and ethical choices embedded in its design.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's Part 1 (Two Futures). Winner's work establishes that the choice between two futures — automation dominion vs. amplification — is a political choice embedded in design, not merely a philosophical abstraction. This grounds CED-AH's opening argument in the established tradition of philosophy of technology and STS.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" is one of the most cited and influential essays in science and technology studies, and *The Whale and the Reactor* is a canonical text in the philosophy of technology. Winner's argument that technology embeds political choices has been vindicated by decades of subsequent research in STS, design studies, and technology policy. The connection to CED-AH's core argument is direct: CED-AH's entire project presupposes that design choices matter — that the same technology, designed differently, produces opposite outcomes. Winner provides the philosophical foundation for this presupposition.

## Limitations

- **The Moses bridges example is historically contested.** Subsequent research has questioned whether Robert Moses actually designed his bridges to exclude buses, or whether the low overpasses had other explanations. If the most famous example is empirically dubious, the argument's rhetorical force is weakened — though the conceptual point (artifacts can embed political choices) does not depend on any single example.
- **The concept of "inherently political technologies" is strong.** Winner's claim that some technologies *require* particular political arrangements has been challenged by STS scholars who argue that no technology is deterministic — social and political choices always mediate between technical properties and social outcomes. If this is right, the politics of artifacts is always contingent, not inherent. CED-AH should be careful about claiming that computational systems *necessarily* erode agency when designed under the agent assumption, rather than claiming they tend to.
- **Winner does not address computational systems specifically.** Writing in 1986, Winner discusses bridges, nuclear power, and factory technologies. The application to AI, LLMs, and computational systems requires bridging. However, the conceptual framework (artifacts embed values) is directly applicable.
- **The prescriptive dimension is underdeveloped.** Winner calls for democratic engagement with technological design but does not provide the specific design principles CED-AH offers. His work is primarily diagnostic (here is the problem) rather than constructive (here is what to do about it).
- **Technological determinism concerns.** Winner's argument treads close to technological determinism — the view that technology shapes society independently of human choice. CED-AH should draw on Winner's insight (design embeds values) while maintaining its own position (the choice between amplification and replacement is a human choice, not a technological inevitability).
