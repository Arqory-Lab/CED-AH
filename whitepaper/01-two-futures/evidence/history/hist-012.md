## Summary

David Mindell's *Our Robots, Ourselves* is the most comprehensive historical demolition of the "myth of autonomy" — the belief that the trajectory of robotics and automation leads inevitably toward fully autonomous systems that operate independently of human involvement. Mindell, who holds joint appointments in engineering and the history of technology at MIT, brings both technical expertise and historical depth to a sustained argument that this narrative is empirically false, conceptually confused, and practically dangerous.

The book's argument is built on detailed case studies from three domains where autonomous systems have been most aggressively developed and where the stakes of failure are highest: undersea robotics, unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), and human spaceflight.

**Undersea robotics.** Mindell traces the history of remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) and autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), from early tethered systems through the exploration of the Titanic wreck and deep-sea scientific research. The central finding is that even the most sophisticated autonomous underwater vehicles require extensive human involvement — in mission planning, in real-time monitoring, in exception handling, and in data interpretation. The systems that succeed are not the most autonomous but the most effectively *collaborative*: they distribute tasks between human and machine based on each one's capabilities.

**Unmanned aerial vehicles.** Mindell examines the history of military drones, from World War I-era radio-controlled aircraft through Predator and Reaper systems. The popular image of "unmanned" aircraft is radically misleading: a single Predator drone mission requires approximately 170 human operators across piloting, sensor operation, intelligence analysis, maintenance, and command functions. "Unmanned" does not mean "unhuman" — it means the humans have been moved from inside the aircraft to ground control stations.

**Human spaceflight.** Mindell's analysis of the Apollo program provides the book's most compelling case study. The Apollo guidance computer was one of the first digital computers used in a safety-critical system, and its design involved intense debates about the appropriate relationship between human astronauts and automated systems. The resulting system was neither fully automated nor fully manual: it was a carefully designed hybrid in which the computer performed navigation calculations and trajectory management while the astronauts retained authority over all critical decisions, with the ability to override at every stage. During the Apollo 11 landing, Neil Armstrong took manual control to avoid a boulder field that the automated system was targeting — a decision that could not have been made without both the computer's navigational precision and the human's contextual judgment.

Mindell's conclusion is unequivocal: "Full autonomy is a myth. It has always been a myth" (p. 10). In every domain he examines, the most effective, safest, and most reliable systems are not the most autonomous but the most thoughtfully designed collaborations between human capability and machine capability.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's instrument thesis with the strongest available historical evidence. CED-AH's foundational claim — that computational systems are instruments, not agents — finds in Mindell the most extensive empirical validation across multiple high-stakes domains. Mindell demonstrates historically, not merely philosophically, that the most aggressively developed autonomous systems in the world — military drones, deep-sea robots, spacecraft — are not and have never been truly autonomous. They are instruments operated by human teams.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification paradigm through historical precedent. Mindell's case studies are, in CED-AH's terms, case studies in amplification. The Apollo guidance system amplified human capability: it performed calculations no human could perform in real time, while the human astronaut provided contextual judgment no computer could match. In every case, the systems that work best are systems that amplify human capability rather than attempting to replace it.

**Provides the empirical counter-evidence** to the autonomous-agent framing that CED-AH critiques (Part 2). The AI safety discourse typically assumes that AI systems will become increasingly autonomous. Mindell's historical evidence demonstrates that this trajectory has been assumed before, in multiple domains, and has never materialized.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention) with the Apollo precedent. The Apollo astronauts' insistence on override capability — and the demonstration during Apollo 11 that this capability was operationally essential — is the historical template for Authority Retention.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by documenting the practical complexity of human-machine collaboration. Mindell does not argue that humans should do everything — he argues that the division of labor between human and machine must be designed carefully, domain by domain, based on the specific capabilities and limitations of each.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Mindell holds dual professorships at MIT in both engineering and the history of technology — a unique combination that gives him both technical credibility and historical depth. The book draws on extensive primary research, including interviews with astronauts, drone operators, undersea robotics engineers, and system designers, as well as archival sources from NASA, the Department of Defense, and the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution. For CED-AH's purposes, Mindell provides the strongest available historical evidence for the instrument thesis: the demonstration, across multiple domains with the highest stakes, that "full autonomy" is a myth and that the most effective systems are human-machine partnerships.

---

## Limitations

- **Historical domains may not map directly to AI.** Mindell's case studies involve physical systems (robots, aircraft, spacecraft) operating in physical environments. AI systems operating in informational, cognitive, and social environments face different constraints and may present different opportunities for autonomy.
- **The book predates the LLM revolution.** Published in 2015, *Our Robots, Ourselves* does not address the specific capabilities of large language models, which can perform cognitive tasks (writing, analysis, coding, reasoning) that earlier automation could not.
- **Mindell's argument may be historically contingent.** The fact that full autonomy has not materialized in any domain *so far* does not prove it is impossible in principle.
- **Military applications raise distinct ethical questions.** Several of Mindell's case studies (Predator drones, military robotics) involve weapons systems where the ethical questions about human authority are qualitatively different from the questions CED-AH primarily addresses.
- **The "myth" framing may be too strong.** Some systems — industrial robots performing repetitive manufacturing tasks, autopilot systems in routine cruise flight — do operate autonomously for extended periods within well-defined parameters.
