## Summary

Parasuraman and Riley provide the definitive taxonomy of human-automation interaction failures — a classification system that has shaped how the field thinks about what goes wrong when humans and automated systems work together. The taxonomy identifies four categories of failure:

**Use** is the baseline: the operator engages with the automation as intended by the designer. This is not a failure mode but the reference condition against which failures are defined.

**Misuse** occurs when operators over-rely on automation — trusting it beyond its actual capability, failing to monitor it adequately, or accepting its outputs without critical evaluation. Misuse is the behavioral consequence of over-trust (emp-007, emp-008). Examples include pilots who fail to cross-check automated navigation against their own understanding, or clinicians who accept a clinical decision support recommendation without considering the patient's specific circumstances.

**Disuse** occurs when operators under-rely on automation — rejecting its recommendations even when they are accurate, failing to engage with its capabilities, or abandoning it after a single failure. Disuse is the behavioral consequence of under-trust. Examples include clinicians who ignore a validated alert system because of "alert fatigue," or operators who manually override a reliable automated system because they "don't trust computers."

**Abuse** occurs when designers or organizations deploy automation without adequate consideration of the human-automation interaction. This is a systems-level failure, not an individual operator failure. Abuse includes: automating tasks without considering the impact on operator situational awareness, deploying automation without adequate training, designing systems that obscure their state or reasoning, and implementing automation to reduce labor costs without considering safety implications.

The taxonomy's most important contribution is the concept of *abuse* — the recognition that human-automation interaction failures are not just operator problems (misuse, disuse) but also design and organizational problems. When operators misuse or disuse automation, the root cause is often a system that was designed without attention to the human-automation relationship. The operators are responding rationally to a poorly designed system.

Parasuraman and Riley document these failure modes across aviation, medicine, manufacturing, and military domains, showing that the patterns are domain-general rather than domain-specific.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's failure mode analysis. Parasuraman and Riley's taxonomy maps directly onto CED-AH's failure modes: misuse corresponds to authority erosion (the human defers to the system inappropriately); disuse corresponds to a different form of coherence failure (the system's capabilities go unused, reducing overall effectiveness); abuse corresponds to the systemic design failures that CED-AH's five imperatives are designed to prevent.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that failure is a design problem, not a user problem. The concept of "abuse" is perhaps the most important contribution for CED-AH's purposes. It locates the root cause of human-automation interaction failures in the design of the system and the organization, not in the behavior of the operator. This is precisely CED-AH's position: systems that fail to maintain human coherence are poorly designed, regardless of how "capable" they are by their own internal metrics. The five design imperatives address abuse at the architectural level.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Misuse and disuse are both consequences of trust miscalibration. A system that implements Calibrated Honesty — stating its confidence accurately, disclosing its limitations, saying "I don't know" when appropriate — reduces both failure modes. Misuse decreases because the system does not invite over-trust; disuse decreases because the system builds warranted trust through transparency about its actual capabilities.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's organizational guidance (Part 8). Parasuraman and Riley's "abuse" category demonstrates that the human-automation relationship is shaped not just by system design but by organizational decisions about deployment, training, and accountability. CED-AH's stakeholder guidance must address the organizational level — not just how to design systems but how to deploy them responsibly.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in the flagship human factors journal, cited over 2,000 times, and widely adopted as the standard taxonomy for human-automation interaction failures. The four-category framework is well-supported by evidence from multiple domains and has been validated by subsequent research. The inclusion of "abuse" as a category — locating responsibility with designers and organizations rather than solely with operators — is particularly valuable for CED-AH's design-level approach. The taxonomy provides the empirical classification system that CED-AH's formal failure mode analysis builds upon.

## Limitations

- **Taxonomy, not theory.** Parasuraman and Riley classify failures but do not provide a unified theory of *why* they occur. CED-AH's coherence framework provides the theoretical explanation: all four failure modes represent different forms of coherence breakdown in the human-system relationship.
- **Traditional automation focus.** The taxonomy was developed for physical and procedural automation. The extension to AI systems — where "misuse" might mean accepting a hallucinated LLM output and "abuse" might mean deploying a chatbot without guardrails — requires careful adaptation.
- **Individual-level framing.** Despite the inclusion of "abuse" as a systems-level category, the taxonomy is primarily framed around individual operator behavior. CED-AH's societal-level concerns (epistemic pollution, collective capability degradation) are not fully addressed.
- **Does not address amplification.** The taxonomy describes failure modes of the replacement paradigm. If systems are designed for amplification rather than replacement, the failure modes may differ. CED-AH should consider whether its amplification approach introduces novel failure modes that Parasuraman and Riley's taxonomy does not capture.
- **No measurement framework.** The taxonomy identifies categories but does not provide metrics for detecting or measuring the severity of each failure mode. CED-AH's Coherence Dashboard addresses this gap.
