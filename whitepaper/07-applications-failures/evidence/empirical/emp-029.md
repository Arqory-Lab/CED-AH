## Summary

Richard Susskind's *Tomorrow's Lawyers* is the most influential work on the transformation of legal practice through technology. Susskind, an advisor to the Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales and a professor at Oxford, argues that the legal profession is on the brink of fundamental transformation: technology will automate much of what lawyers currently do, and the profession must adapt or face irrelevance.

Susskind describes a trajectory from traditional "bespoke" legal work (each matter handled individually by a human lawyer) through standardization (routine matters handled using templates and procedures) to automation (technology performing tasks previously done by lawyers) and eventually to commoditization and open access (legal services available at low cost to a much wider population).

Susskind identifies several technologies driving this transformation: document automation, legal research AI, online dispute resolution, predictive analytics for case outcomes, and contract analysis systems. He argues that many tasks currently performed by junior lawyers — document review, legal research, due diligence, contract drafting — will be largely automated within a generation.

Crucially for CED-AH, Susskind does not treat this as an unambiguously positive development. He identifies a tension between efficiency and expertise: automation makes legal services cheaper and more accessible, but it also disrupts the apprenticeship model through which junior lawyers develop professional judgment. If routine tasks are automated, the question becomes: how do young lawyers develop the expertise currently gained through years of performing those routine tasks?

Susskind's answer is largely pragmatic: the profession must adapt its training models, develop new forms of expertise (legal technology management, data analysis, process design), and accept that some traditional legal roles will disappear. He frames this as inevitable and largely positive, though he acknowledges the disruption it entails.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification/replacement distinction in the legal domain. Susskind's analysis implicitly distinguishes between two approaches to legal technology: tools that replace lawyer judgment (automated contract review that decides whether a clause is acceptable) and tools that amplify lawyer judgment (research systems that surface relevant precedents while leaving the legal reasoning to the lawyer). CED-AH provides the philosophical framework that Susskind's analysis implies but does not articulate.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by documenting the genuine benefits of legal automation. Susskind shows that legal technology can make legal services more accessible, more affordable, and more consistent — serving populations that currently cannot afford legal representation. CED-AH must acknowledge this: not all replacement is bad if the alternative is no service at all. The coherence analysis must account for the counterfactual: is the relevant comparison "automated legal service vs. expert lawyer" or "automated legal service vs. no legal service"?

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth) through the apprenticeship problem. Susskind's concern about the loss of the apprenticeship model — how do young lawyers develop expertise if routine tasks are automated? — is a Capability Growth concern. If legal technology replaces the training ground on which legal expertise develops, the profession's long-term capability atrophies even as short-term efficiency increases. This is Bainbridge's irony (emp-001) in the legal domain.

**Provides domain-specific evidence** for CED-AH's claim that the amplification/replacement choice has long-term consequences for professional capability. Susskind's trajectory — from bespoke to standardized to automated to commoditized — describes a profession progressively ceding capability to technology. CED-AH's amplification alternative would look different: technology that makes each lawyer more capable, not technology that makes lawyers less necessary.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Susskind is the most influential commentator on legal technology, with decades of advisory and academic work in the field. *Tomorrow's Lawyers* is published by Oxford University Press and is widely used in law schools. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) Susskind is primarily an advocate for legal technology transformation, and his evidence selection may reflect this advocacy; (b) the book is more predictive than empirical — many of Susskind's claims about the future of legal practice are extrapolations that have not yet been validated; (c) the book does not provide a systematic analysis of when automation helps and when it harms, which is the distinction CED-AH needs; and (d) Susskind does not engage with the human factors or philosophical literature that grounds CED-AH's analysis.

---

## Limitations

- **Predictive rather than empirical.** Much of Susskind's analysis is about what *will* happen, not what *has* happened. The predictions are informed by trends but have not been fully validated.
- **Pro-transformation advocacy.** Susskind's default stance is that technological transformation of law is inevitable and largely beneficial. Countervailing evidence — cases where legal automation produced worse outcomes — receives less attention.
- **The apprenticeship problem is identified but not solved.** Susskind acknowledges the training challenge but does not provide a satisfactory answer. CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative provides the design principle; the implementation in legal technology remains to be developed.
- **Does not address access-to-justice complexity.** Susskind frames automation as improving access to legal services, but the quality of automated legal services relative to human legal representation is a complex empirical question that the book does not thoroughly address.
- **UK-centric in many examples.** While the general principles apply globally, Susskind's specific examples and regulatory context are primarily British.
