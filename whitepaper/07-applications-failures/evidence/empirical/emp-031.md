## Summary

Neil Selwyn's *Should Robots Replace Teachers?* is the most critically rigorous assessment of AI in education — a deliberate counterweight to the techno-optimism that dominates educational technology discourse. Selwyn, a professor of education at Monash University and one of the leading critical scholars of educational technology, argues that the dominant narrative about AI in education — that it will personalize learning, free teachers from drudgery, and close achievement gaps — systematically ignores the social, political, and pedagogical complexities of education.

Selwyn's critique operates at several levels:

**The "personalization" myth.** AI-driven personalized learning, Selwyn argues, typically means personalized *content delivery* — the system adjusts what information the student receives based on their performance data. But education is not primarily about information delivery. It is about developing understanding, cultivating judgment, building social skills, learning to navigate ambiguity, and forming identity. "Personalized" AI addresses only the most superficial dimension of education while claiming to transform the whole enterprise.

**The deskilling of teaching.** If AI takes over individualized instruction, assessment, and content delivery, what remains for the teacher? Selwyn argues that the likely outcome is not teachers freed for higher-order work but teachers reduced to classroom managers, behavior monitors, and system administrators. This is Bainbridge's irony (emp-001) in educational form: the automation of "routine" teaching tasks degrades the teacher's role rather than enriching it.

**The surveillance dimension.** AI in education requires continuous data collection about student behavior, performance, attention, and engagement. Selwyn argues that this surveillance apparatus — regardless of its educational justification — fundamentally changes the nature of the learning environment. Students who are constantly monitored, scored, and classified by AI systems learn in an environment of surveillance rather than an environment of trust, exploration, and intellectual risk-taking.

**The values embedded in "efficiency."** Selwyn argues that AI in education is driven by an efficiency paradigm — faster learning, more content covered, better test scores — that may be antithetical to the values of education itself: slow thinking, deep understanding, intellectual struggle, creative exploration, and the development of wisdom. CED-AH's coherence criterion would ask: is the educational system internally consistent? A system that claims to serve education while optimizing for metrics that are not education may be incoherent.

**The equity question.** Selwyn challenges the claim that AI will close achievement gaps. He argues that AI is more likely to amplify existing inequalities: well-resourced schools will use AI as a complement to excellent teaching; under-resourced schools will use AI as a substitute for absent teaching. The same technology, deployed differently, will widen the gap it claims to close.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's caution about replacement in the education domain. Selwyn's analysis demonstrates that the replacement paradigm in education — AI replacing teacher functions — is not merely educationally suboptimal but actively harmful: it deskills teachers, surveils students, reduces education to information delivery, and amplifies inequality. CED-AH's amplification alternative — AI as classroom assistant (emp-030), not as teacher replacement — is the pedagogically and ethically appropriate approach.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's coherence criterion through the education-values analysis. Selwyn's argument that AI-driven education is optimizing for efficiency while claiming to serve educational values is a coherence failure: the system's actual optimization target (speed, coverage, test scores) is inconsistent with its stated purpose (understanding, capability, wisdom). CED-AH's coherence criterion — internal consistency + alignment with human flourishing — detects exactly this kind of misalignment.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth) through the deskilling argument. If educational AI deskills teachers (reducing them from educators to system managers) and deskills students (reducing them from learners to content consumers), then the system is producing capability atrophy in both user populations — the opposite of what CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative requires.

**Provides the critical counterbalance** to du Boulay (emp-030). Where du Boulay shows that AI can be an effective classroom assistant, Selwyn shows that this is not the default outcome — it requires deliberate design choices that resist the efficiency-maximizing default. CED-AH's design imperatives provide the framework for making these choices.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim about the equity dimension of amplification vs. replacement. Selwyn's argument that the same technology amplifies inequality depending on deployment context is a specific instance of CED-AH's general claim: the design relationship (amplification vs. replacement) determines the outcome, and replacement disproportionately harms those with the least power and the fewest alternatives.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Selwyn is a respected critical scholar of educational technology with extensive publication and research experience. The book is published by Polity Press (a reputable academic publisher) and provides a rigorous critical analysis that is sorely needed in a field dominated by techno-optimism. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) Selwyn's analysis is primarily critical rather than constructive — he is better at identifying problems than proposing solutions; (b) the book does not engage with the empirical evidence on AI tutoring effectiveness that du Boulay (emp-030) cites; (c) some of Selwyn's arguments may overstate the negative case, particularly regarding surveillance, where the comparison should be with existing educational surveillance (testing, grading, disciplinary records) rather than with an idealized surveillance-free classroom; and (d) the book does not provide a systematic framework for distinguishing beneficial from harmful educational AI — which is the gap CED-AH's design imperatives fill.

---

## Limitations

- **Critical but not constructive.** Selwyn identifies problems with AI in education but does not propose a detailed alternative. CED-AH's amplification framework and design imperatives provide the constructive response that Selwyn's critique demands.
- **May overstate the negative case.** Selwyn's critical posture may lead him to underweight genuine benefits of educational AI (improved access, individualized practice, rapid feedback) that du Boulay documents.
- **Does not engage with the intelligent tutoring systems literature.** Selwyn's critique addresses the policy discourse about AI in education more than the technical and empirical literature. The evidence that well-designed tutoring systems can improve learning outcomes is not fully addressed.
- **The surveillance concern is real but not unique to AI.** Schools already conduct extensive surveillance through testing, grading, discipline records, and administrative data. The question is whether AI surveillance is qualitatively different — a question Selwyn raises but does not fully answer.
- **Pre-LLM.** The specific challenges of LLM-based educational tools (students using ChatGPT for homework, AI writing assistants, interactive tutoring) are not addressed. These may fundamentally change the educational AI landscape.
