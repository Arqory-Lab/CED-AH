## Summary

Philip Tetlock's *Expert Political Judgment* is one of the most important — and most sobering — studies of expert performance ever conducted. Over a twenty-year period (1984-2003), Tetlock collected over 28,000 predictions from 284 recognized experts (political scientists, economists, intelligence analysts, journalists) about political and economic events. The results were devastating: on average, expert predictions performed only slightly better than random chance and were consistently outperformed by simple statistical algorithms.

More specifically: (1) Experts were systematically overconfident — their stated confidence exceeded their actual accuracy. (2) Experts with the highest public profiles (the "hedgehogs" — those with a single big idea they applied to everything) performed worst. (3) Experts who were more eclectic, self-critical, and willing to update their views (the "foxes") performed better, though still modestly. (4) Experts were poor at recognizing their own failures — they engaged in extensive post-hoc rationalization to explain away incorrect predictions.

Tetlock's fox/hedgehog distinction (borrowed from Isaiah Berlin) is particularly significant. Hedgehogs are ideologically committed, confident, and resistant to disconfirming evidence. Foxes are pragmatic, uncertain, and willing to integrate information from multiple sources. The superior performance of foxes suggests that cognitive style — particularly openness to revision and tolerance of uncertainty — is more important for judgment quality than domain expertise alone.

The study has been cited as evidence that human judgment should be replaced by algorithms. But Tetlock himself drew a more nuanced conclusion: the problem is not expertise per se but the conditions under which expertise is exercised. In domains with feedback and accountability, experts can improve. In domains without feedback (like long-term political forecasting), expertise stagnates.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Qualifies** CED-AH's claim that human expertise is a capability to be amplified. Tetlock demonstrates that in some domains, expert judgment is remarkably poor — and that experts are often unaware of their limitations. CED-AH cannot cite Klein (emp-019) and Ericsson (emp-014) on the power of expertise without also acknowledging Tetlock's evidence that expertise can fail dramatically, particularly in domains without clear feedback.

**However**, Tetlock's findings support amplification rather than replacement. The foxes outperformed the hedgehogs — and the fox cognitive style (integrating diverse information, updating views, tolerating uncertainty) is precisely what amplification-oriented AI systems should support. A system that helps a hedgehog think like a fox — providing diverse perspectives, flagging disconfirming evidence, calibrating confidence — is an amplification system.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Tetlock's finding that experts are systematically overconfident is the political judgment equivalent of Kahneman's overconfidence research (emp-017). Calibrated Honesty — stating confidence that matches actual accuracy — directly addresses Tetlock's core finding. An AI system that says "based on historical accuracy of similar predictions, this class of forecast is correct approximately 60% of the time" would be implementing Calibrated Honesty in a way that directly counters the overconfidence Tetlock documented.

**Complicates** CED-AH's position by providing ammunition for the replacement paradigm. If experts perform at or near chance levels, why retain human authority? CED-AH must respond to this challenge — perhaps by noting that (a) Tetlock's findings are specific to long-term political forecasting, not all domains; (b) even in domains where algorithms outperform, the algorithm cannot explain its reasoning, adapt to novel situations, or be held accountable; and (c) Tetlock's own follow-up work (emp-022) shows that trained human forecasters can dramatically improve.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** One of the most rigorous and extensive studies of expert judgment ever conducted, spanning 20 years with over 28,000 predictions from 284 experts. Published by Princeton University Press, extensively peer-reviewed, and widely cited across political science, psychology, and decision science. The findings are robust and have withstood extensive scrutiny. For CED-AH, this is the most important adversarial source in the cognitive science batch — it challenges naive optimism about expertise while ultimately supporting amplification through its fox/hedgehog distinction.

## Limitations

- **Domain-specific findings.** Long-term political and economic forecasting is an unusually difficult domain — low predictability, delayed feedback, high noise. The failures Tetlock documents may not generalize to domains with clearer feedback and higher regularity (medicine, engineering, aviation).
- **The study ended before the AI era.** Whether modern AI systems would outperform Tetlock's experts at political forecasting is an empirical question. Tetlock's simple algorithms were not AI systems — they were basic statistical models.
- **The fox/hedgehog distinction may be too simple.** Real experts do not fall neatly into two categories. The distinction is useful as a heuristic but may oversimplify the relationship between cognitive style and judgment quality.
- **Experts may have been making calibrated "hedging" predictions.** Some experts may have been assigning moderate probabilities to multiple outcomes — which would look like poor performance by Tetlock's metrics even if the probabilities were well-calibrated. The scoring methodology has been debated.
- **Public predictions may differ from private judgments.** Experts making public predictions face incentives (media attention, reputation management) that may distort their judgments. Expert judgment in non-public, high-stakes contexts may be more accurate.
