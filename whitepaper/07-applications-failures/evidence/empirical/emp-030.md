## Summary

Benedict du Boulay's article in *IEEE Intelligent Systems* provides a measured, evidence-based assessment of how artificial intelligence can function as an effective classroom assistant — not a replacement for teachers but a complement that enhances the learning environment. Du Boulay, a professor of artificial intelligence at the University of Sussex with decades of research in intelligent tutoring systems, draws on the accumulated evidence from educational AI research to identify what works, what doesn't, and why the "classroom assistant" metaphor is more productive than the "automated teacher" metaphor.

Du Boulay identifies several functions where AI has demonstrated effectiveness as a classroom complement:

**Individualized practice.** AI tutoring systems can provide students with practice opportunities tailored to their current understanding — adjusting difficulty, providing hints, and offering explanations at a pace the individual student needs. This is work that a human teacher cannot practically do for 30 students simultaneously. The AI handles the drill-and-practice component; the teacher handles the conceptual instruction, motivation, and social learning.

**Formative assessment.** AI can provide rapid, detailed feedback on student work — identifying specific misconceptions, errors, and learning gaps. This feedback complements (rather than replaces) teacher assessment by providing more frequent and detailed information about student learning.

**Metacognitive scaffolding.** Some AI systems help students develop metacognitive skills — self-monitoring, self-regulation, planning, and reflection. These systems prompt students to think about their own thinking, which develops the learning skills that transfer across domains.

Du Boulay is explicit that these functions are *assistant* functions, not *teacher* functions. The teacher remains essential for: establishing learning goals, providing motivation and social support, creating meaningful learning contexts, handling the emotional and relational dimensions of education, and making holistic judgments about student development that no AI system can make. The AI handles the scalable, repetitive, individualized components; the teacher handles the irreducibly human components.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification thesis in the education domain. Du Boulay's "classroom assistant" model is amplification by another name: the AI enhances the teacher's capability (by handling individualized practice and formative assessment at scale) while the teacher retains authority over the learning environment. The result is that both the teacher and the students are better off — the teacher can focus on high-value instruction, and students receive both expert teaching and individualized support.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth) in a distinctive way. In education, the "user" is the student, and the goal is the student's learning and development. An AI that replaces the teacher removes the human relationship that motivates learning and contextualizes knowledge. An AI that assists the teacher enhances both the teacher's effectiveness and the student's learning — a double amplification that exemplifies Capability Growth.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention). Du Boulay's model keeps the teacher in authority over the learning process. The AI provides information and support; the teacher makes pedagogical decisions. This is the education-specific instantiation of Authority Retention: the professional (teacher) retains decision-making authority while the system (AI assistant) provides capabilities the professional can leverage.

**Provides the positive educational countercase.** Where Selwyn (emp-031) provides the critical assessment of educational AI, du Boulay provides the evidence that AI can genuinely improve education when deployed as amplification. Together, the two sources provide the complete picture: amplification works; replacement doesn't.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Du Boulay is a respected researcher in educational AI with decades of experience. The article is published in a peer-reviewed IEEE journal and synthesizes evidence from the intelligent tutoring systems literature. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) the article is a brief review (six pages) rather than a systematic meta-analysis; (b) the evidence for AI tutoring effectiveness varies substantially across domains and implementations — what works for mathematics may not work for humanities; (c) the "classroom assistant" framing, while appealing, may understate the complexity of integrating AI into actual classroom practice; and (d) the article predates the LLM era and does not address the specific challenges of generative AI in education.

---

## Limitations

- **Brief review format.** The article cannot provide detailed evidence for each claim. CED-AH should treat this as a summary and seek additional sources for domain-specific effectiveness evidence.
- **Effectiveness varies by domain and implementation.** Intelligent tutoring systems work well for structured, procedural domains (mathematics, programming) but less well for open-ended, interpretive domains (literature, ethics, creative writing). CED-AH should note this variation.
- **Implementation challenges are underemphasized.** Integrating AI assistants into real classrooms involves teacher training, infrastructure, curriculum alignment, and institutional support that du Boulay does not fully address.
- **Pre-LLM.** The specific challenges and opportunities of LLM-based educational tools (ChatGPT as a tutor, writing assistants, interactive explanations) are not addressed. These may fundamentally change the amplification/replacement calculus in education.
- **The "assistant" metaphor may obscure power dynamics.** Calling AI a "classroom assistant" implies a benign supporting role, but AI systems in education can also surveil students, standardize curricula, and reshape learning in ways that serve institutional rather than student interests.
