**Access:** SAGE via DOI. Available through institutional library access. Published in *Human Factors*, the flagship journal of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society.

## Summary

Endsley and Kiris's 1995 paper provides the key experimental evidence for the "out-of-the-loop performance problem" — the finding that human operators who are removed from active control of a system and placed in a monitoring role experience measurable degradation in both their situational awareness and their ability to intervene effectively when the automated system fails. This paper transforms Bainbridge's conceptual analysis (emp-001) into experimentally validated claims.

The study used a simulated dynamic task environment in which participants either controlled the system directly, monitored a fully automated system, or worked with an intermediate level of automation. The critical finding was systematic: participants in the fully automated condition had significantly poorer situational awareness — they were less able to accurately describe the current state of the system, predict its near-future trajectory, or detect when anomalies occurred. When automation failed and manual intervention was required, participants in the fully automated condition performed significantly worse than those who had been actively controlling the system.

Crucially, the study found that the *level* of automation mattered. Participants working with intermediate automation — where the system assisted but did not replace their decision-making — maintained better situational awareness than those with full automation, and performed better when manual takeover was required. This is one of the earliest experimental demonstrations that the design choice (how much the system replaces vs. augments) determines the human performance outcome.

The study also documented that participants in the fully automated condition were slower to detect automation failures — a finding consistent with the "complacency" literature. When you are not actively engaged with a task, you are less likely to notice when the system doing the task on your behalf begins to malfunction. The combination of degraded awareness and delayed detection creates a window of vulnerability that increases with automation reliability: the better the automation usually works, the less practiced the human is at detecting its rare failures.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's identification of the out-of-the-loop problem as a central coherence violation. This is the experimental evidence for what CED-AH formalizes philosophically. Endsley and Kiris demonstrate that when humans are placed in a monitoring role by automation, their performance degrades across multiple dimensions: situational awareness, failure detection, and manual intervention capability. This is not a matter of individual inattention — it is a structural consequence of the monitoring role itself.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification/replacement distinction with experimental data. The finding that intermediate automation preserved human performance while full automation degraded it is a direct experimental demonstration of CED-AH's core claim: same technology, different relationship, opposite outcomes. The critical variable was not the presence or capability of the automation but the nature of the human-system relationship — exactly what CED-AH argues.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth). The experimental data show that full automation leads to capability atrophy (degraded situational awareness, slower failure detection, worse manual performance) while intermediate automation preserves or enhances capability. CED-AH's requirement that systems support increasing capability over time is empirically validated by this study's finding that the human-system relationship, not just the system's capability, determines the human outcome.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's measurement framework. Endsley and Kiris's dependent variables — situational awareness accuracy, failure detection time, manual takeover performance — are empirical operationalizations of dimensions CED-AH's Coherence Dashboard tracks. The study demonstrates that these dimensions are measurable, that they differentiate between design approaches, and that they track meaningful differences in human capability.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in the flagship human factors journal, this study provides rigorous experimental evidence for claims that Bainbridge (emp-001) argued conceptually and that Carr (emp-003) documented journalistically. The experimental design is appropriate, the findings are consistent with the broader literature, and the results have been replicated and extended in subsequent studies across multiple domains. The direct comparison between full automation and intermediate automation is particularly valuable for CED-AH: it provides experimental evidence that the amplification/replacement distinction produces measurably different human outcomes.

## Limitations

- **Laboratory simulation.** The study used a simulated task environment, not a real operational setting. While simulation provides experimental control, ecological validity is always a concern — real operators in real systems may behave differently due to training, experience, motivation, and organizational context.
- **Short-duration exposure.** The study measured performance over a single experimental session. CED-AH's concern with capability trajectory over time — whether the user becomes *more* capable with extended use — requires longitudinal data this study does not provide.
- **Procedural task domain.** The simulated environment involved monitoring and controlling a dynamic system. The extension to cognitive tasks (writing, diagnosis, strategic judgment) — the domains where AI systems are increasingly deployed — requires additional evidence. Situational awareness may operate differently in knowledge-work contexts.
- **Binary level-of-automation comparison.** The study compared full automation, intermediate automation, and manual control. CED-AH's amplification framework envisions a richer design space (augmenting understanding, scaffolding skill development, calibrating confidence) that goes beyond simply adjusting the "level" of automation.
- **Does not address trust dynamics.** The study focuses on performance and awareness but does not systematically examine how trust in automation develops, calibrates, or miscalibrates over time. Lee and See (emp-008) and Hoffman et al. (emp-007) address this gap.
