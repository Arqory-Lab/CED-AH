## Summary

Cabitza, Rasoini, and Gensini's brief but influential JAMA commentary identifies a set of unintended consequences that arise when machine learning systems are deployed in clinical medicine. Published in the world's most prestigious medical journal, the commentary draws on the broader automation and human factors literature (including Bainbridge, emp-001) and applies it specifically to clinical AI.

The authors identify five categories of unintended consequence:

1. **Automation bias.** Clinicians tend to accept machine-generated recommendations uncritically, even when those recommendations conflict with their own clinical judgment. This effect is well-documented in aviation and process control (emp-001, emp-009); Cabitza et al. demonstrate its presence in clinical settings. When a diagnostic AI suggests a diagnosis, clinicians are more likely to accept it than to question it — even when clinical evidence points elsewhere.

2. **Deskilling.** Consistent with Bainbridge's ironies (emp-001) and Endsley's lessons (emp-004), clinical ML systems that replace diagnostic reasoning can degrade the clinician's own diagnostic capability over time. A radiologist who relies on AI-flagged abnormalities may lose the perceptual skill to detect unflagged abnormalities; a physician who follows algorithmic treatment recommendations may lose the clinical reasoning ability to manage cases the algorithm has not encountered.

3. **Automation complacency.** Distinct from automation bias, complacency refers to reduced vigilance in monitoring system outputs. When an ML system performs well most of the time, clinicians stop checking its work — and miss the cases where it fails. This is Bainbridge's monitoring irony in clinical form.

4. **Anchoring and premature closure.** ML-generated recommendations can anchor clinicians' reasoning, causing them to close the diagnostic search prematurely. If the AI suggests pneumonia, the clinician may stop considering other diagnoses — even if the patient's presentation is atypical.

5. **Opacity and trust miscalibration.** Clinicians cannot evaluate recommendations they do not understand. When an ML system produces a recommendation through opaque neural network processing, the clinician cannot assess whether the recommendation is well-founded — and may either over-trust (accepting uncritically) or under-trust (rejecting reflexively), depending on their dispositional attitude toward the technology.

The commentary concludes by calling for clinical AI to be designed as a support tool rather than a replacement for clinical judgment — a recommendation that maps directly onto CED-AH's amplification paradigm.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's failure mode analysis in the healthcare domain. Cabitza et al.'s five unintended consequences map directly onto CED-AH's failure modes: automation bias violates Authority Retention (the clinician abdicates judgment); deskilling violates Capability Growth (the clinician becomes less capable); automation complacency reflects the monitoring failure Bainbridge identified; anchoring and premature closure are specific cognitive mechanisms through which replacement degrades clinical reasoning; and opacity violates Transparency.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that the same technology produces opposite outcomes depending on the design relationship. The commentary's conclusion — that clinical AI should be designed as support rather than replacement — is CED-AH's amplification thesis stated in clinical terms. Cabitza et al. do not provide a philosophical framework for this distinction (that is CED-AH's contribution), but they identify the empirical pattern: replacement degrades clinical capability; support preserves it.

**Provides domain-specific evidence** for the coherence failure pattern. Each of Cabitza et al.'s unintended consequences is a coherence failure: the system claims to improve clinical care while actually degrading the clinical capabilities that make care possible. The system is internally inconsistent (designed to help but structurally harmful) and misaligned with human flourishing (the patient's interest requires a competent clinician, not a dependent one).

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in JAMA — one of the world's highest-impact medical journals — this commentary has been cited over 700 times and has shaped the discourse on clinical AI deployment. The authors are credentialed medical researchers, and the commentary draws on the well-established human factors literature. For CED-AH's healthcare failure mode analysis, this is the most direct and authoritative clinical source. The brevity of the commentary is a limitation (it is a viewpoint piece, not an empirical study), but the findings it synthesizes are well-supported by the broader literature.

---

## Limitations

- **Commentary, not original research.** Cabitza et al.'s piece is an analytical commentary drawing on existing literature, not a report of new empirical findings. The unintended consequences they describe are well-documented elsewhere, but this specific source does not provide new data.
- **Brief format.** At two pages, the commentary cannot provide detailed evidence for each claim. CED-AH should cite this as a synthesis and refer to the underlying studies (Bainbridge, Endsley, Parasuraman) for detailed evidence.
- **The five consequences are not unique to ML.** Automation bias, deskilling, and complacency occur with any clinical automation, not just ML. The specific ML-related mechanisms (opacity, learned pattern reliance) require additional analysis beyond what this commentary provides.
- **Does not quantify the effects.** The commentary identifies the categories of unintended consequence but does not quantify their frequency, severity, or clinical impact. CED-AH needs quantitative evidence (from sources like Topol, emp-026, and Wachter, emp-027) to complement this categorical analysis.
- **Does not provide a design framework.** The commentary calls for "support" rather than "replacement" but does not specify what this means in design terms. CED-AH's five design imperatives provide the missing specification.
