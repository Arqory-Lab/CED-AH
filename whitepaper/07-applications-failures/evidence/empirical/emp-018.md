## Summary

*Noise* extends Kahneman's research program (emp-017) into a dimension of judgment error that has received far less attention than bias: noise — the unwanted variability in judgments that should be identical. Where bias is systematic error (everyone errs in the same direction), noise is random error (different people, or the same person at different times, reach different conclusions about the same case).

The book documents noise across consequential domains: criminal sentencing (judges give wildly different sentences for identical cases), insurance underwriting (adjusters vary dramatically in their assessments), medical diagnosis (doctors disagree about diagnoses and treatments for the same patients), forensic science (fingerprint examiners reach different conclusions about the same prints), and personnel decisions (interviewers evaluate identical candidates differently).

Kahneman, Sibony, and Sunstein argue that noise is at least as damaging as bias — and often more so — because it is invisible. Bias can be detected by comparing judgments to correct answers. Noise can only be detected by comparing judgments to each other. Organizations that never conduct "noise audits" — comparing how different judges handle identical cases — remain unaware of the variability in their decision-making.

The book's prescriptive chapters argue for "decision hygiene" — structured procedures that reduce noise without requiring an understanding of its specific causes. These include: independent judgments before group discussion, structured decision protocols, ranking rather than rating, and — importantly — the use of algorithms and statistical models to replace or discipline human judgment in domains where noise is high.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by documenting a genuine problem with human judgment that amplification must address. Noise is not bias — it cannot be corrected by providing better information or countering specific heuristics. It is variability in judgment that arises from factors irrelevant to the decision (mood, fatigue, order effects, individual differences). CED-AH must acknowledge that noise is a real phenomenon and that its amplification framework must address it.

**However**, CED-AH can argue that the appropriate response to noise is not replacement but structured amplification. Kahneman et al.'s "decision hygiene" recommendations are themselves amplification strategies: they structure human judgment without replacing it. Algorithms that provide reference points, structured protocols that ensure consistency, and feedback systems that help judges calibrate — all of these are tools that amplify human judgment rather than substituting for it.

**Complicates** CED-AH's authority retention imperative. The book's most challenging implication for CED-AH is its evidence that human judgment in some domains is so noisy that algorithmic replacement would produce more consistent (and arguably fairer) outcomes. Criminal sentencing is the paradigm case: if two judges give dramatically different sentences for identical cases, the injustice is in the noise, and an algorithm that produces consistent sentences might serve justice better. CED-AH must address this challenge — perhaps by arguing that the appropriate response is structured human judgment (sentencing guidelines, calibration tools) rather than algorithmic replacement.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Noise is, in part, a calibration failure — judges are confident in their judgments despite massive disagreement with equally confident colleagues. A system implementing Calibrated Honesty would surface this disagreement, helping judges understand that their confidence exceeds the reliability of their judgment.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The book is authored by a Nobel laureate and two prominent legal scholars, and it documents noise with extensive evidence from multiple domains. The rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) the book's prescriptive conclusions (favoring algorithmic replacement in some domains) are more contested than its descriptive findings, (b) the evidence for noise is primarily from organizational decision-making contexts that may not generalize to all domains CED-AH addresses, and (c) the book does not engage with the philosophical questions CED-AH raises about human authority and coherence.

---

## Limitations

- **Prescriptive conclusions favor replacement.** Kahneman et al. sometimes recommend replacing human judgment with algorithms — a conclusion CED-AH challenges. CED-AH should engage with the data while critiquing the prescriptive leap from "noise exists" to "algorithms should decide."
- **Noise may be partly functional.** In some domains, variability in judgment may reflect legitimate differences in values, priorities, or contextual factors. Reducing noise to zero could eliminate important contextual sensitivity along with unwanted randomness.
- **The organizational focus is specific.** Noise is best documented in institutional decision-making (sentencing, underwriting, hiring). Whether the same levels of noise exist in the individual-level, technology-mediated decisions CED-AH primarily addresses is an empirical question.
- **Does not address the authority question.** The book asks "how can we reduce noise?" without asking "who should have authority?" CED-AH's insistence on human authority is a normative commitment that noise evidence alone cannot override.
