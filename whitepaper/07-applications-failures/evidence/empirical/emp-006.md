## Summary

Peter Hancock — one of the most prominent human factors researchers of the past four decades — provides a critical analysis of the promises and assumptions underlying the autonomous vehicle (AV) industry. Hancock argues that the AV discourse is pervaded by unjustified optimism, rooted in misunderstandings of human factors research and a systematic failure to learn from the history of automation in other domains.

Hancock identifies several specific "pitfalls" in the AV discourse:

**The perfection assumption.** AV advocates often argue that autonomous vehicles will eliminate human error, which causes the vast majority of traffic accidents. Hancock counters that this assumes AV systems will not introduce their *own* error modes — an assumption contradicted by every previous domain of automation. New systems do not eliminate error; they transform it. The errors of automated systems may be less frequent but more catastrophic, harder to predict, and more difficult to recover from.

**The monitoring fallacy.** The "Level 3" autonomous vehicle concept requires the human driver to monitor the system and be ready to take over when it reaches its operational limits. Hancock argues — drawing directly on the Bainbridge/Endsley research tradition — that this is precisely the wrong task to assign to a human. Humans are poor passive monitors; the expectation that a driver who has been disengaged from driving for twenty minutes can suddenly take over in an emergency is not supported by any human factors evidence. This is Bainbridge's irony, instantiated in a new domain.

**The human-as-liability framing.** Hancock challenges the pervasive assumption that the human driver is a "problem to be solved." While human error is real, humans also bring adaptive capability, contextual judgment, and creative problem-solving that automated systems lack. The appropriate response is not to remove the human but to design systems that complement human capabilities — amplification rather than replacement.

**The transition problem.** Even if fully autonomous vehicles eventually become safe, the transition period — during which human-driven and autonomous vehicles share roads, and individual vehicles may switch between autonomous and human-controlled modes — presents severe safety challenges that the industry has not adequately addressed.

Hancock's analysis is notable for its combination of empirical rigor and philosophical clarity. He does not merely identify technical problems; he diagnoses the *conceptual errors* in the AV discourse — the unexamined assumptions that lead designers and policymakers astray.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's failure mode analysis through domain-specific evidence. Hancock's pitfalls are domain-specific instantiations of CED-AH's general failure modes. The perfection assumption reflects the measurement gap (evaluating system performance without evaluating human outcomes). The monitoring fallacy is the out-of-the-loop problem. The human-as-liability framing is the replacement paradigm. The transition problem is a reversibility failure. CED-AH's framework organizes and generalizes what Hancock documents in a specific domain.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that the replacement paradigm generates predictable, preventable failures. Hancock shows that the AV industry is repeating errors that human factors research identified and documented decades ago (Bainbridge 1983, Billings 1997). This is not a novel observation — it is a failure to learn. CED-AH's argument that the field needs philosophical foundations (not just more engineering) is supported by this evidence: the engineering knowledge exists but is ignored because there is no principled framework to compel attention to it.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's critique of the AI safety discourse more broadly. The AV industry's "pitfalls" — unjustified optimism, the perfection assumption, the human-as-liability framing — are structurally identical to the pitfalls CED-AH identifies in the broader AI discourse. If the AV case demonstrates that the replacement paradigm fails even with massive investment and decades of engineering, this strengthens CED-AH's claim that the paradigm itself is the problem, not the implementation.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by identifying domain-specific complexities. Hancock's analysis reveals that the amplification/replacement distinction is cleaner in some domains (decision support, knowledge work) than in others (driving, which is a continuous perceptual-motor task with limited opportunity for "amplification" as CED-AH conceives it). CED-AH must address how its framework applies to domains where the nature of the task makes augmentation architecturally challenging.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Hancock is one of the most cited and respected researchers in human factors, and the paper is published in a leading ergonomics journal. The analysis is rigorous and draws on a deep knowledge of the human factors literature. The rating is Moderate rather than Strong because the paper is primarily critical analysis rather than original empirical data — it synthesizes existing knowledge and applies it to the AV domain rather than reporting new experimental findings. Its value for CED-AH is as a domain-specific illustration of general principles rather than as novel evidence.

---

## Limitations

- **Critical analysis, not experimental data.** Hancock identifies problems but does not report experiments or observational studies. The pitfalls are argued from human factors principles, not demonstrated through AV-specific empirical research.
- **Autonomous vehicles are a specific domain.** The extension from AV failures to AI system failures in general requires argument. Driving is a continuous perceptual-motor task; many AI applications involve discrete decisions, language processing, or information synthesis. The failure modes may differ.
- **The technology is still evolving.** Hancock's critique was published in 2019. AV technology has continued to develop. While the human factors challenges he identifies remain relevant, some may be more tractable than his analysis suggests.
- **Does not engage with philosophical foundations.** Hancock's analysis is empirical and design-focused. He does not ask *why* the replacement paradigm generates these pitfalls at a philosophical level — which is CED-AH's unique contribution.
- **May underweight genuine safety gains.** Autonomous features (automatic emergency braking, lane-keeping assistance) have demonstrably reduced certain types of accidents. Hancock's focus on the pitfalls may underrepresent the benefits of well-designed driver assistance (which is closer to amplification than replacement).
