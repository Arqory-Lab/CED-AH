## Summary

Tim Wu provides the definitive historical account of what he calls **"the Cycle"**: each new information technology (telephone, radio, film, television, internet) begins in an open, decentralized phase before being captured by monopolists or cartels who centralize control. Wu traces this pattern from Bell's telephone through AT&T's monopoly, from amateur radio through NBC's dominance, from independent film through the Hollywood studio system, from open internet through platform consolidation. His "Separations Principle" — that content creators, network owners, and access providers must be kept structurally independent — is the governance-level analog of CED-AH's system-level authority retention principle.

Wu's central observation: *every development in the history of the American information industry once existed in an open and chaotic marketplace before centralization*. The pattern is structural, not incidental.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the entire historical scaffolding** for CED-AH's accountability argument (Paper 5). Wu's "Cycle" is the phenomenon; CED-AH's contribution is identifying *accountability severance* as its mechanism and the Two Commitments as its remedy.

**Supports the interlock argument through historical evidence.** In every cycle Wu documents, the technology was initially used as an instrument for human empowerment (consistent with Commitment 1). The failure occurred when no accountability structure prevented the transition from empowerment to exploitation (Commitment 2 absent). The historical pattern demonstrates empirically what the Two Commitments framework argues philosophically: correct classification without enforcement fails.

**Wu's Separations Principle parallels CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative.** Both argue that structural independence between the system and its controlling entity is necessary for the technology to serve rather than exploit its users.

## Limitations

- **U.S.-centric.** The Cycle is documented primarily through American information industries. The pattern may not hold in all jurisdictions and economic systems.
- **Pre-AI.** Published in 2010, before the current AI moment. Wu addresses the internet and platforms but not LLMs, foundation models, or generative AI. The Cycle's applicability to AI requires extension by CED-AH.
- **Descriptive, not prescriptive.** Wu documents the pattern and proposes the Separations Principle but does not derive a comprehensive governance framework. CED-AH provides the framework; Wu provides the historical evidence.
