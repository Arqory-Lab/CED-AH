## Source

**Access:** Available via academic libraries and commercial booksellers. ISBN 978-1595588678.

---

## Summary

McChesney argues that the internet's democratic promise has been captured by capitalist interests — that the same dynamics of media consolidation, advertising-driven content, and monopoly power that shaped 20th-century media have reshaped the internet. He provides a political-economic analysis of how ISPs, platform companies, and digital advertising networks have concentrated control over online information flows, undermining the decentralized, democratic potential Benkler documented.

McChesney's analysis is more pessimistic than Benkler's and more structurally focused than Wu's: he argues that capitalist capture of information systems is not cyclical but inherent in the logic of capital accumulation applied to information goods.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the structural-economic explanation** for the Cycle. Where Wu documents the pattern historically and Zittrain explains it mechanistically, McChesney argues that capitalist logic makes centralization not just likely but inevitable absent countervailing governance structures. This strengthens CED-AH's argument that accountability must be structurally embedded (the Two Commitments) rather than left to market self-regulation.

**Supports the accountability principle's necessity.** If McChesney is right that market dynamics inherently drive toward centralization and accountability evasion, then voluntary accountability frameworks will systematically fail. The accountability principle must be structural — built into governance, not dependent on corporate goodwill.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** McChesney is a prominent media studies scholar. The book provides a useful structural-economic complement to Wu's historical and Zittrain's architectural analyses. Its political-economic framing is controversial but empirically grounded in media consolidation data.

---

## Limitations

- **Ideological framing.** McChesney writes from a democratic-socialist perspective. His analysis of capitalist dynamics is rigorous but his prescriptions (public investment in media, regulation of monopoly) are politically contested. CED-AH should draw on the structural analysis without adopting the ideological framing.
- **Media-focused.** Primarily concerned with media and communications, not AI systems specifically. The extension to AI governance requires bridging work.
