## Summary

Diane Vaughan's *The Challenger Launch Decision* is one of the most important works of organizational sociology written in the twentieth century — a painstaking, decade-long investigation into how NASA came to launch the Space Shuttle Challenger on January 28, 1986, despite the fact that its engineers knew the O-ring seals on the solid rocket boosters were vulnerable to failure at low temperatures. Seven crew members died when the O-rings failed 73 seconds after launch.

Vaughan's central contribution is the concept of the **normalization of deviance** — the process by which an organization gradually comes to accept as normal a practice or condition that was originally recognized as deviant (risky, anomalous, or in violation of design requirements). The normalization of deviance is not a single decision or a moment of conscious risk acceptance. It is an incremental, socially constructed process:

1. **An anomaly is observed.** Engineers observe O-ring erosion during rocket firing.
2. **The anomaly is assessed.** Engineers determine erosion falls within what they judge to be an acceptable range.
3. **The anomaly recurs without catastrophe.** Because no catastrophe results, the assessment of acceptability is reinforced.
4. **The baseline shifts.** The definition of "acceptable" expands to include conditions originally considered unacceptable.
5. **Dissent is foreclosed.** Engineers who continue to express concern find their concerns met with accumulated evidence of prior flights without failure.

Vaughan demonstrates that this process was not the result of managerial malfeasance, cost-cutting, or deliberate risk acceptance. The engineers and managers involved were acting in good faith, following their organization's procedures. The normalization of deviance was a *structural* phenomenon — an emergent property of how the organization processed information, assessed risk, and made decisions.

The book's most devastating argument is that the Challenger disaster was not an anomaly in NASA's decision-making culture — it was the *product* of that culture. The same processes that allowed NASA to achieve extraordinary engineering feats also produced the conditions that made the Challenger disaster possible.

Vaughan's concept has been applied far beyond the Challenger case — to healthcare errors, financial risk management, police misconduct, regulatory capture, and organizational ethics generally.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's analysis of how organizations lose coherence — the single most important historical and sociological source for understanding institutional coherence failure. Vaughan's normalization of deviance is, in CED-AH's terms, the mechanism by which an organization's practices drift out of alignment with its stated principles. The organization maintains the *language* of safety while the substance has been hollowed out.

CED-AH's coherence criterion — internal consistency + alignment with flourishing requirements — captures exactly what the normalization of deviance erodes. This is the organizational analog of what CED-AH predicts will happen with computational systems designed for replacement: the gradual, incremental acceptance of reduced human capability, diminished authority, and increased dependency — each step individually defensible, the trajectory collectively devastating.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's argument that coherence failures are systemic, not individual. Vaughan demonstrates that the people involved were competent, well-intentioned, and following their organization's procedures. The failure was structural. CED-AH makes the same structural argument about computational systems.

**Provides the theoretical framework** applied in the CAIB Columbia Report (hist-011). The CAIB explicitly cited Vaughan's normalization of deviance to explain how NASA repeated, seventeen years later, the same institutional patterns. Together, Vaughan's analysis (Challenger) and the CAIB Report (Columbia) constitute a two-case longitudinal study of institutional coherence failure.

**Extends** CED-AH's analysis to the temporal dimension of coherence. Vaughan demonstrates that coherence is not a static property but a dynamic one — it must be actively maintained over time through thousands of small decisions.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by demonstrating that the same organizational properties that produce success can also produce failure. CED-AH must acknowledge that a culture of coherence that is too rigid may prevent innovation; too flexible may normalize deviance.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** *The Challenger Launch Decision* is one of the most cited works in organizational sociology, science and technology studies, and risk management — with thousands of academic citations. Vaughan's research is based on ten years of archival investigation, including previously unreleased NASA documents. The normalization of deviance concept has been validated through application across dozens of organizational contexts. For CED-AH's purposes, this is the strongest available source on how organizations lose coherence incrementally.

## Limitations

- **Specific to a single organization.** Vaughan's analysis is based on one organization (NASA) and one decision (the Challenger launch). Application to technology companies and other contexts is an extension.
- **Retrospective analysis with hindsight advantage.** Decisions that appear clearly wrong in retrospect may have appeared reasonable in real time.
- **The normalization of deviance may be universal.** If all organizations normalize some deviations as practical necessity, the concept may describe a universal dynamic rather than a specific failure mode.
- **Does not directly address computational systems.** The extension to computational systems — where "deviations" may be harder to detect — requires additional argumentation.
- **Structural explanation may underweight individual responsibility.** CED-AH's emphasis on human authority implies that individuals bear some responsibility for exercising judgment even within flawed institutional structures.
