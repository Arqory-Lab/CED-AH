## Source

**Access:** Available via academic libraries and commercial booksellers. ISBN 978-0300173130.

## Summary

Gillespie documents how social media platforms make consequential editorial decisions — what content to promote, demote, remove, or amplify — while publicly denying that they are editors. They position themselves as neutral platforms (which provides legal protection under Section 230) while functioning as curators (which involves normative judgments about content value, safety, and engagement). This "double identity" — platform in public, editor in practice — is the operational mechanism of the accountability gap. The decisions that shape public discourse are made by identifiable entities (platform companies) through identifiable mechanisms (recommendation algorithms, content policies, moderation practices) — but no accountability structure connects those decisions to their consequences.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's compound failure analysis of social media. Gillespie documents both commitment failures: (1) **Instrument thesis failure** — platforms transitioned from instruments users wielded (posting, sharing, connecting) to autonomous curatorial systems that wielded users (algorithmic feeds, engagement optimization, attention capture); (2) **Accountability principle failure** — the platform-editor duality allowed companies to disclaim accountability for editorial decisions they were actively making.

**Provides the empirical evidence** for CED-AH's claim that social media's failure is structural, not incidental. Gillespie shows that content moderation decisions are necessarily editorial — they involve normative judgments about value, harm, and relevance. Denying this editorial role while exercising it is precisely the accountability severance CED-AH identifies.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Gillespie is a leading scholar of platform governance. The book is based on extensive research including interviews with platform employees and analysis of moderation policies. Published by Yale University Press, it is the authoritative treatment of how platforms exercise editorial power while denying editorial accountability.

---

## Limitations

- **Descriptive, not prescriptive.** Gillespie documents what platforms do but does not propose a governance framework. CED-AH provides the framework; Gillespie provides the evidence.
- **Pre-generative-AI.** Written before large language models became consumer-facing. The content moderation dynamics Gillespie describes are evolving rapidly with AI-generated content.
