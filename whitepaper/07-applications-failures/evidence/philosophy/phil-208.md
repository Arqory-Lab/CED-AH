## Source

**Access:** Available via academic libraries; SSRN / Yale Journal on Regulation.

---

## Summary

Rozenshtein demonstrates that Section 230 is "deeply ambiguous" — its grants of immunity can be read either broadly (platforms are immune for virtually all content decisions) or narrowly (platforms are immune only for good-faith editorial choices). He argues that content recommendation algorithms pose a particularly difficult case because prioritizing content involves normative value judgments that go beyond passive hosting. His proposal: courts should look to Congress for clarification rather than continue expanding immunity through judicial interpretation. The paper illustrates the accountability vacuum: nearly 30 years after Section 230's enactment, neither courts nor Congress have resolved the fundamental question of who bears responsibility for algorithmic curation at scale.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the most current legal scholarship** on the unresolved accountability gap in platform governance. Rozenshtein's analysis demonstrates that the accountability vacuum CED-AH identifies is not a historical artifact but a live, unresolved problem as of 2024 — courts are still expanding immunity, Congress has not acted, and algorithmic content recommendation operates in a governance void.

**Supports the argument that philosophical commitment must precede legal implementation.** The Section 230 impasse exists because there is no consensus on the underlying accountability principle. CED-AH's contribution is providing that principle: every deploying entity bears accountability for its system's effects. Legal implementation can follow; but without the philosophical commitment, legal frameworks remain incoherent.

## Limitations

- **Legal analysis, not philosophical argument.** Valuable as evidence but does not independently support the accountability principle.
- **U.S.-specific.** Section 230 is American law; other jurisdictions have different frameworks. The accountability gap is universal but its legal manifestation is jurisdiction-specific.
