## Source

**Citation:**
Citron, D. K., & Wittes, B. (2017). The internet will not break: Denying bad Samaritans § 230 immunity. *Fordham Law Review*, 86(2), 401–423.

**Access:** Available via academic libraries; HeinOnline / SSRN.

---

## Summary

Citron and Wittes argue that Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act has been interpreted far more broadly than Congress intended, creating immunity for platforms that actively facilitate harmful conduct — "bad Samaritans" who profit from harm while claiming the protection intended for good-faith moderators. Their analysis traces how judicial interpretation expanded a narrow liability shield (designed to protect nascent internet services that made good-faith efforts at content moderation) into a comprehensive accountability vacuum (protecting platforms that make no effort at moderation, or whose moderation actively amplifies harmful content). They propose distinguishing between good-faith intermediaries (who deserve protection) and bad Samaritans (who do not).

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's analysis of social media as an accountability severance. Section 230's expansion from good-faith protection to comprehensive immunity is the legal mechanism through which the accountability principle was violated in the social media case. Citron and Wittes provide the legal scholarship documenting precisely how this happened — judicial interpretation, case-by-case expansion, legislative inaction.

**Illustrates the accountability gap as a systemic, not incidental, failure.** The expansion of Section 230 immunity was not accidental — it followed the logic of legal precedent in the absence of legislative correction. The accountability vacuum created is structural, exactly as CED-AH's analysis claims.

**Supports the argument that accountability requires proactive structural commitment.** The Section 230 experience demonstrates that accountability, once severed, is extremely difficult to restore through judicial or legislative processes. CED-AH's accountability principle must be built into systems and governance structures from the outset, not retrofitted after harm has occurred.

## Limitations

- **U.S.-specific.** Section 230 is a U.S. statute. The accountability dynamics differ in jurisdictions with different platform liability regimes (EU's Digital Services Act, UK's Online Safety Act). CED-AH's accountability principle must be jurisdiction-agnostic.
- **Legal, not philosophical.** The analysis is legal scholarship, not philosophical argument. It documents what happened but does not derive a general accountability principle from first principles. CED-AH provides the philosophical grounding; Citron and Wittes provide the empirical case.
