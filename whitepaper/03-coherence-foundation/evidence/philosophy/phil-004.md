## Summary

Catherine Z. Elgin's *True Enough* argues that truth, understood as strict literal correspondence to fact, is neither necessary nor sufficient for epistemic acceptability. Scientific models, thought experiments, idealizations, and approximations are epistemically valuable not because they are literally true but because they are *true enough* — they capture the relevant features of a domain in ways that advance understanding.

Elgin proposes "reflective equilibrium" (adapted from Goodman and Rawls) as the standard for epistemic acceptability: a system of commitments is epistemically acceptable when its components are mutually supportive and jointly tenable. This is an explicitly coherentist criterion, but Elgin's coherentism is broader than traditional epistemic coherentism. It encompasses not only beliefs but also models, exemplifications, categories, and values. Understanding, not truth, is the primary epistemic achievement.

A key innovation is Elgin's concept of "felicitous falsehoods" — representations that are literally false but epistemically productive. Frictionless planes in physics, ideal rational agents in economics, and simplified models in biology are all false, but they advance understanding by isolating relevant features. Elgin argues that a theory of knowledge restricted to literal truths cannot account for the epistemic value of these practices.

Elgin's framework is holistic: individual representations gain their epistemic standing from their role within a larger system of mutually supportive commitments. No representation is epistemically acceptable in isolation; acceptability is a property of systems.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's coherentist epistemology and its move from coherence as logical consistency to coherence as a richer, domain-responsive criterion. Elgin's "reflective equilibrium" — mutual support and joint tenability among diverse commitments — closely parallels CED-AH's multi-dimensional coherence (internal consistency + flourishing alignment). Both frameworks reject the idea that isolated components can be evaluated independently of their systemic context.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's own epistemic status. The CED-AH framework includes idealized models (the Coherence Dashboard, the four-tier formal architecture, the measurement framework). Elgin's argument that idealizations are epistemically productive — that being "true enough" is sufficient when the purpose is advancing understanding — provides philosophical warrant for CED-AH's use of simplified models and formalized structures that may not capture every nuance of real-world human-computation interaction.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's reliance on truth as the standard for evidence. If Elgin is right that understanding rather than truth is the primary epistemic achievement, CED-AH's evidence framework may need to evaluate sources not just by whether they are "true" (Strong/Moderate/Suggestive) but by whether they advance understanding of the relevant domain. This is a refinement, not a contradiction, but it enriches how CED-AH should think about evidence quality.

Elgin's holism — the claim that epistemic acceptability is a property of systems, not individual beliefs — directly reinforces CED-AH's emphasis on systemic coherence over isolated claims.

## Limitations

- **Scope is philosophy of science, not ethics or technology.** Elgin's arguments about models, idealizations, and understanding are developed primarily in the context of natural science. Extending them to normative evaluation of computational systems is plausible but not established by Elgin.
- **"True enough" may cut both ways.** If epistemic standards are relaxed from strict truth to "true enough," this could be used to defend CED-AH's idealizations — but also to defend competing frameworks that are "true enough" for different purposes. The criterion does not uniquely favor CED-AH.
- **No engagement with coherence as first principle.** Elgin takes a coherentist stance but does not argue that coherence is the *unavoidable* first principle in CED-AH's transcendental sense. Her coherentism is a methodological commitment, not a transcendental necessity.
- **Reflective equilibrium is contested.** Critics (notably Stich, 1990; Weinberg et al., 2001) argue that reflective equilibrium codifies contingent intuitions rather than tracking epistemic facts. If reflective equilibrium is unreliable, it weakens the case for Elgin's coherentism and, derivatively, for CED-AH's.
