## Summary

"Two Dogmas of Empiricism" is among the most influential essays in twentieth-century analytic philosophy. Quine attacks two interrelated assumptions of logical empiricism: (1) that there is a sharp analytic-synthetic distinction — a clear line between truths that hold by virtue of meaning (analytic) and truths that hold by virtue of fact (synthetic); and (2) that individual statements can be confirmed or disconfirmed in isolation (reductionism).

Against the first dogma, Quine argues that every attempt to define "analyticity" presupposes the very concept it is trying to define. Synonymy, definition, semantic rules — all the candidate explanations of analyticity — turn out to be circular. There is no clear boundary between truths of meaning and truths of fact.

Against the second dogma, Quine proposes what has become known as "confirmation holism" or the "web of belief" thesis. Statements do not face the tribunal of experience individually; they face it as a corporate body. When experience conflicts with our beliefs, we have a choice about which beliefs to revise — including beliefs we previously considered definitional or analytic. Even the laws of logic are, in principle, revisable in the face of sufficiently recalcitrant experience.

The positive upshot is a thoroughgoing holism: our beliefs form an interconnected web, with sensory experience impinging only at the edges. No belief is immune to revision, and no belief is confirmed in isolation. Justification is a property of the web as a whole, not of individual strands.

Quine's naturalism — his insistence that philosophy is continuous with natural science, not prior to it — follows from this holism. If no beliefs are analytic (true by meaning alone), then philosophical claims are as revisable as scientific ones.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's coherentist epistemology and the rejection of foundationalism. Quine's demolition of the analytic-synthetic distinction is, in effect, an argument that no beliefs are epistemically privileged — precisely CED-AH's coherentist starting point. If there are no truths immune to revision, then the structure of justification must be holistic (coherentist), not linear (foundationalist).

The "web of belief" metaphor maps directly onto CED-AH's coherence framework. CED-AH's four-tier architecture (T1, S1–S6, B1–B4, Design Imperatives) is itself a web: each element is connected to others, and the strength of the whole depends on the coherence of the connections. CED-AH explicitly allows that Tier 2 commitments are "genuinely contestable" and could be revised — a Quinean posture.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's claim that coherence is the evaluative criterion for computational systems. If even logical truths are revisable within the web, then the web's coherence is the only stable criterion. This generalizes from epistemology to evaluation: if no evaluative principle is immune to revision, then the criterion for choosing among principles must be coherence.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's normative claims. Quine's naturalism is in tension with CED-AH's normative dimension (S1: coherence = flourishing alignment). Quine dissolves the fact-value distinction along with the analytic-synthetic distinction, but he does so in favor of a naturalized epistemology that is descriptive, not prescriptive. CED-AH's move from "coherence is the structure of justification" to "coherence should include flourishing alignment" is a normative step that Quine's framework does not straightforwardly support.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** "Two Dogmas" is one of the most consequential philosophical essays of the twentieth century and has shaped epistemological debate for seven decades. Its central arguments — against the analytic-synthetic distinction and for confirmation holism — have been widely accepted (though not universally) and directly support CED-AH's coherentist foundations. The connection is not analogical but structural: Quine's holism is a direct ancestor of the coherentist epistemology CED-AH adopts.

## Limitations

- **Quine's naturalism is in tension with CED-AH's normativity.** Quine's project is to naturalize epistemology — to make it continuous with empirical science. CED-AH's project is to establish normative principles for evaluating computational systems. These are different enterprises. CED-AH can draw on Quine's holism without endorsing his naturalism, but it should be transparent about where it diverges.
- **The web of belief is a metaphor, not a formal model.** Quine does not provide a formal account of how beliefs are connected, how coherence is measured, or how revision should proceed. CED-AH's formal architecture (Tiers 1–4) goes beyond anything Quine provides, and the connection between Quine's metaphor and CED-AH's formalism requires bridging.
- **Quine's holism may be too permissive.** If any belief can be revised in light of experience (including logic), then the web of belief can accommodate almost anything by making adjustments elsewhere. This raises the same "too-much-coherence" concern that Haack identifies: coherence without constraint is vacuous.
- **No ethical or political dimension.** Quine's essay is in philosophy of language and epistemology. It says nothing about human flourishing, computational systems, or design imperatives. The extension is CED-AH's own.
- **The analytic-synthetic debate continues.** While Quine's critique was devastating, defenders of analyticity (notably Grice and Strawson, 1956; Boghossian, 1996; Williamson, 2007) have offered responses. The matter is not definitively settled.
