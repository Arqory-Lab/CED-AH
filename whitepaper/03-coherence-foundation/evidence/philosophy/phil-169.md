## Summary

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) is the most influential English-language philosopher of the 19th century and the thinker who gave utilitarianism — the moral theory that the right action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number — its most sophisticated and enduring formulation. For CED-AH, Mill presents a **dual challenge**: *Utilitarianism* offers the strongest competing moral framework (one that could justify replacing human judgment with more accurate algorithmic decisions if doing so increases aggregate welfare), while *On Liberty* provides powerful arguments for individual autonomy and intellectual freedom that support CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative and open challenge process. Mill is both adversary and ally — and CED-AH must engage with both dimensions.

**Utilitarianism, Chapter II: The Greatest Happiness Principle.** Mill defines the utilitarian standard as follows: "The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals, Utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. By happiness is intended pleasure, and the absence of pain; by unhappiness, pain, and the privation of pleasure" (Utilitarianism, Ch. II).

Mill famously modifies Bentham's crude quantitative hedonism by introducing qualitative distinctions among pleasures: "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied" (Ch. II). Some pleasures (intellectual, aesthetic, moral) are qualitatively superior to others (bodily, sensory), and the judgment of those who have experienced both is the criterion for distinguishing them. This qualitative dimension makes Mill's utilitarianism more sophisticated than simple aggregate welfare maximization, but it also introduces a tension: who decides which pleasures are "higher"?

The utilitarian framework presents the most direct competing theory to CED-AH's coherence principle. CED-AH holds that coherence — internal consistency + alignment with human flourishing requirements — is the first principle, and that human decision-making authority is non-negotiable. Utilitarianism holds that the right action is the one that maximizes aggregate happiness. These two principles can diverge: a utilitarian could argue that replacing human decision-making with more accurate algorithmic decisions *increases* aggregate happiness (fewer errors, better outcomes, more efficient resource allocation) and is therefore morally required — even if it diminishes individual agency. If an AI system makes better medical diagnoses than human doctors, a utilitarian might argue that patients' welfare requires replacing human diagnostic judgment with algorithmic diagnosis. CED-AH would reject this: even if the algorithmic system produces better outcomes on average, the replacement of human judgment violates the Authority Retention imperative and produces dependency that undermines long-term human capability.

The disagreement is fundamental. CED-AH holds that some values (human agency, coherence, the capacity for growth) are non-negotiable — they cannot be traded off against aggregate welfare. Utilitarianism holds that everything is commensurable — any value can be traded off against any other if the overall utility calculus favors the trade. CED-AH must articulate why certain values should be protected from utilitarian calculus — why agency cannot be sacrificed for efficiency, even when efficiency improves aggregate outcomes.

**On Liberty, Chapters I–III: The Harm Principle and Individuality.** *On Liberty* presents Mill's argument for the broadest possible freedom of thought, expression, and individual action. The "harm principle" — "the only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others" (On Liberty, Ch. I) — sets the boundary of legitimate interference: individuals are sovereign over their own lives, bodies, and minds, and interference is justified only to prevent harm to others.

Mill's argument for freedom of thought and discussion (Chapter II) is directly relevant to CED-AH's open challenge process. Mill argues that suppressing any opinion is wrong, regardless of whether the opinion is true or false: if the opinion is true, suppressing it prevents the discovery of truth; if it is false, suppressing it prevents the truth from being tested, strengthened, and fully understood through confrontation with error. "The peculiar evil of silencing the expression of an opinion is, that it is robbing the human race" (Ch. II). This argument provides powerful philosophical support for CED-AH's evidence challenge system: every challenge, even an unsuccessful one, strengthens the framework by forcing its defenders to articulate and defend their positions.

Mill's argument for individuality (Chapter III) directly supports CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative. Mill argues that individuality — the exercise of one's own judgment, the development of one's own character, the pursuit of one's own plan of life — is intrinsically valuable, not merely instrumentally useful. A person who follows custom, convention, or algorithmic recommendation without exercising their own judgment is, in Mill's terms, failing to develop their individuality — they are using their faculties as a "machine" rather than as a "human being" (Ch. III). Mill writes: "He who lets the world, or his own portion of it, choose his plan of life for him, has no need of any other faculty than the ape-like one of imitation. He who chooses his plan for himself, employs all his faculties." This passage could serve as a manifesto for CED-AH's amplification principle: systems that support the exercise of individual judgment amplify humanity; systems that substitute for individual judgment reduce it.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the strongest competing moral framework** that could justify replacement over amplification. Utilitarianism is the philosophical position most likely to be invoked against CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative: if replacing human judgment with algorithmic judgment increases aggregate welfare, utilitarianism provides the moral argument for doing so. CED-AH must engage with this argument directly and demonstrate why its coherence principle — which protects human agency as non-negotiable — is more defensible than the utilitarian alternative. The strongest CED-AH response may be that utilitarian calculation itself requires human agents capable of evaluating outcomes, and that systematically replacing human judgment undermines the very capacity for evaluation that utilitarianism presupposes. A society that has delegated all judgment to algorithms cannot evaluate whether the algorithms are producing good outcomes, because the humans have lost the capability to judge.

**Partially adversarial, partially supportive.** Mill's *Utilitarianism* is adversarial to CED-AH; Mill's *On Liberty* is supportive. This dual relationship must be made explicit. CED-AH can draw on Mill's arguments for individuality and intellectual freedom while articulating its disagreement with Mill's utilitarian foundation. The strongest articulation of the disagreement: CED-AH holds that certain values (agency, coherence, the capacity for growth) are constitutive of human flourishing and therefore cannot be traded off against aggregate happiness. Mill's utilitarianism, even in its qualitative version, treats all values as commensurable with happiness — a position that CED-AH rejects as failing to protect the conditions for human flourishing.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's open challenge process through the argument for freedom of discussion. Mill's Chapter II of *On Liberty* provides the single most powerful philosophical argument for intellectual openness: every opinion should be heard, every claim should be challengeable, because the suppression of any opinion — true or false — impoverishes the intellectual community. CED-AH's evidence challenge system is a direct application of this Millian principle: the framework publishes its claims, invites challenges, and treats successful challenges as contributions rather than threats.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative through the argument for individuality. Mill's Chapter III of *On Liberty* argues that the exercise of individual judgment is intrinsically valuable — a person who merely follows external direction without engaging their own faculties has failed to develop as a human being. This provides direct philosophical support for CED-AH's claim that human decision-making authority is non-negotiable: even when an algorithm could make a "better" decision (by some external metric), the exercise of human judgment has intrinsic value that the algorithmic decision lacks. The value is not merely in the outcome but in the process — in the human engagement with the problem, the exercise of faculties, the development of capability that comes from making one's own decisions.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by raising the utilitarian challenge to non-negotiable principles. CED-AH claims that human authority is "non-negotiable" — but a utilitarian would ask: non-negotiable under what conditions? If replacing human judgment with algorithmic judgment in a specific domain (e.g., air traffic control) would save thousands of lives, is CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative still binding? CED-AH must address this edge case: does "non-negotiable" mean absolute (authority retention applies regardless of consequences) or lexically prior (authority retention applies unless the consequences of maintaining it are catastrophic)? The utilitarian challenge forces CED-AH to specify the limits and conditions of its non-negotiable principles.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Mill is one of the most important moral and political philosophers in the Western tradition, and his works present both the strongest competing framework to CED-AH (utilitarianism) and powerful support for key CED-AH commitments (intellectual freedom, individuality, Authority Retention). The dual adversarial/supportive relationship makes this a particularly rich and complex piece of evidence that forces CED-AH to articulate its positions with precision. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) the adversarial dimension (utilitarianism vs. coherence) is genuine and the outcome of the confrontation is not predetermined — CED-AH must make its case, not merely assert its superiority; (2) Mill's qualitative utilitarianism may be closer to CED-AH's position than the simple utilitarian caricature suggests — the "higher pleasures" doctrine introduces non-quantitative values that may overlap with coherence; (3) *On Liberty* was written for a political context (individual liberty vs. state power) and its extension to human-AI interaction requires interpretive work; and (4) the utilitarian tradition has been extensively refined since Mill (Sidgwick, Hare, Parfit, Singer) in ways that may be more challenging to CED-AH than Mill's original formulation.

## Limitations

- **This is partially adversarial evidence.** Mill's utilitarianism provides the most serious competing framework to CED-AH's coherence principle. The evidence item should be flagged as adversarial on the utilitarian dimension: it articulates a challenge that CED-AH must meet, not a source to be cited in unqualified support.
- **Qualitative utilitarianism complicates the adversarial frame.** Mill's distinction between higher and lower pleasures introduces qualitative, non-quantitative values into the utilitarian framework. If the "higher pleasures" include the exercise of individual judgment, the development of capability, and intellectual growth — which Mill's text suggests — then qualitative utilitarianism may be closer to CED-AH's position than the adversarial framing implies. CED-AH should engage with Mill's qualitative utilitarianism specifically, not merely with the crude version.
- **The harm principle's scope is contested.** Mill's harm principle — only harm to others justifies interference — has been debated for over 160 years. What counts as "harm"? Does the atrophy of human capability through over-reliance on algorithms constitute "harm" in Mill's sense? CED-AH might argue that it does (capability atrophy harms the individual and, through dependency, harms society), but this extension of the harm principle requires argument.
- **Historical context of exclusion.** Mill was a progressive for his time — he advocated for women's suffrage (*The Subjection of Women*, 1869) and against colonialism. But his framework emerged from the British liberal tradition that coexisted with empire, and his concept of "civilized community" (in the harm principle) implicitly excludes those he considered "uncivilized." CED-AH should acknowledge this context when drawing on Mill's universalist language.
- **The post-Mill utilitarian tradition.** Utilitarianism has been significantly refined since Mill. Preference utilitarianism (Hare, Singer), rule utilitarianism (Hooker), and consequentialism more broadly (Parfit) present more sophisticated challenges to CED-AH than Mill's original formulation. CED-AH should engage not only with Mill but with the strongest contemporary versions of the utilitarian challenge.
- **No engagement with technology.** Mill wrote in the mid-19th century — before electricity, let alone computation. His arguments about individuality and intellectual freedom were directed at social conformity, state censorship, and public opinion. The application to algorithmic recommendation systems, AI decision-making, and computational governance is entirely a modern extension.
