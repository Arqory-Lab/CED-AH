## Summary

In this landmark essay, Donald Davidson argues that coherence among beliefs is not merely a test for truth but is constitutively connected to truth and knowledge. His argument proceeds through several distinctive moves.

First, Davidson observes that beliefs are inherently propositional and interconnected — you cannot have a single belief in isolation because having a belief requires understanding its content, which requires understanding its relations to other beliefs. The "web of belief" is not a metaphor but a structural requirement.

Second, Davidson deploys the "principle of charity": to interpret another speaker at all, we must assume that most of their beliefs are true. Radical interpretation (understanding a language from scratch) requires assuming coherence and truth in the speaker's overall belief system. This is not generosity but necessity — without the assumption, interpretation collapses.

Third, and most crucially for CED-AH, Davidson argues that the very concept of belief presupposes a background of largely true, coherent beliefs. A being whose beliefs were mostly false or incoherent would not, by Davidson's lights, count as having beliefs at all. Coherence is therefore not one desirable property of a belief system among others — it is a *precondition* for anything counting as a belief system.

Davidson explicitly rejects the "scheme-content" dualism — the idea that we have a conceptual scheme applied to raw experiential content. There is no uninterpreted "given" that beliefs must correspond to. All we have are beliefs, and their justification is their coherence with other beliefs.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's transcendental argument for coherence (T1). Davidson's claim that coherence is a precondition for having beliefs at all is structurally identical to CED-AH's claim that coherence is presupposed by all evaluation. Both arguments establish coherence not as one criterion among many but as the criterion without which other criteria cannot function.

Davidson's principle of charity — the claim that interpretation requires assuming coherence — directly parallels CED-AH's performative contradiction argument. Just as CED-AH argues you cannot argue against coherence without presupposing it, Davidson argues you cannot interpret a speaker without presupposing that their beliefs are largely coherent and true.

Davidson's rejection of scheme-content dualism reinforces CED-AH's coherentist stance against foundationalism. If there is no uninterpreted "given," then beliefs cannot be justified by correspondence to raw experience — they can only be justified by coherence with other beliefs. This supports CED-AH's claim that no beliefs are "basic."

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's claim that coherence applies to evaluative systems generally, not just to individual belief systems. If coherence is a precondition of interpretation between speakers, it is plausibly a precondition of evaluation between frameworks.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Davidson is one of the most important analytic philosophers of the twentieth century. This essay has been debated, criticized, and refined for four decades and remains a touchstone of the coherentism literature. The argument that coherence is constitutive of (not merely a test for) belief and truth is logically rigorous and directly addresses CED-AH's foundational claim. The connection is not analogical but structural: Davidson's transcendental argument about belief parallels CED-AH's transcendental argument about evaluation.

---

## Limitations

- **Davidson's argument is about belief systems, not evaluative frameworks.** The extension from "coherence is necessary for beliefs" to "coherence is necessary for evaluating computational systems" requires additional argumentation. Davidson establishes that belief requires coherence; CED-AH needs that evaluation requires coherence. The gap is plausible to bridge but is not bridged by Davidson himself.
- **The principle of charity is contested.** Critics (notably Fodor and Lepore, 1992) argue that charity is too strong — we can interpret speakers with significant numbers of false beliefs. If charity is weakened, Davidson's constitutive claim about coherence is weakened correspondingly.
- **No physical grounding.** Davidson's argument is purely conceptual and linguistic. He does not connect coherence to thermodynamics, biology, or physical systems. CED-AH's physical grounding claim must be defended independently.
- **Truth and coherence may come apart.** Davidson's critics have argued that even within his framework, coherence is necessary but not sufficient for truth. A system of beliefs could be maximally coherent but systematically false (the "alternative coherent systems" problem). CED-AH's domain commitment (S1: coherence = flourishing alignment) may address this, but that is a separate argument.
