## Summary

Keith Lehrer's *Theory of Knowledge* develops a distinctive version of coherentism that centers on the notion of "personal justification" — justification relative to the acceptance system of an individual epistemic agent. For Lehrer, a belief is justified if it coheres with the agent's "acceptance system": the set of things the agent accepts for the purpose of attaining truth and avoiding error.

Lehrer's key innovation is the concept of "beating and neutralizing competitors." A belief p is personally justified for subject S if and only if p beats or neutralizes every competitor — every proposition that, if accepted, would lower the reasonableness of accepting p. This is evaluated within the subject's acceptance system, making Lehrer's account internalist (justification depends on the subject's own cognitive resources) and coherentist (justification depends on relations among accepted propositions, not on foundational beliefs).

Lehrer distinguishes between personal justification (coherence within the individual's acceptance system) and "undefeated justification" (personal justification that is not undermined by truths the subject doesn't know about). Full knowledge requires both: the belief must cohere with what the subject accepts *and* must not be defeated by external facts.

Lehrer's treatment of the Gettier problem (cases where justified true belief fails to constitute knowledge) through his "defeasibility" condition — knowledge requires that justification not be defeated by truths — is influential and connects coherentist epistemology to broader debates about the nature of knowledge.

The book also provides a valuable critique of foundationalism, particularly the "myth of the given" (following Sellars), arguing that no sensory state can serve as a non-conceptual epistemic foundation.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's coherentist orientation. Lehrer's account, like BonJour's and Davidson's, denies that there are epistemically basic beliefs and holds that justification is a holistic property of belief systems. His "beating and neutralizing competitors" framework formalizes what coherence requires: a coherent system is not merely internally consistent but actively resistant to challenge. This maps onto CED-AH's evidence framework, which invites formal challenges and requires that every claim survive scrutiny to remain in the evidence base.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's structured approach to evidence and challenge. Lehrer's competitive model — where beliefs must beat or neutralize competitors to be justified — parallels CED-AH's open challenge structure (the GitHub issue templates for challenging evidence). In both systems, justification is not a static property but an ongoing process of surviving challenge.

**Provides necessary context** for understanding the coherentist tradition CED-AH draws on. Lehrer represents a third independent development of coherentism (alongside BonJour and Davidson), showing that the position is not idiosyncratic but has been arrived at through multiple lines of argumentation.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's reliance on coherence alone. Lehrer's distinction between personal justification (coherence-based) and undefeated justification (requiring truth-tracking) suggests that coherence may be necessary but not sufficient for knowledge. CED-AH's claims may be personally justified (coherent within the framework) but could be defeated by external facts the framework does not account for.

## Limitations

- **Internalist framework.** Lehrer's coherentism is internalist: justification depends on the subject's own acceptance system. This creates challenges for CED-AH's institutional-level analysis. Whether an organization's deployment of computational systems increases or decreases coherence is not well-captured by an individual-level acceptance-system model.
- **The acceptance system may be idiosyncratic.** If justification is relative to what the subject happens to accept, then two subjects with different acceptance systems could be equally justified in contradictory beliefs. CED-AH needs a more objective standard of coherence than Lehrer's personal justification provides.
- **Undefeated justification requires truth access.** Lehrer's condition for full knowledge — that justification not be defeated by unknown truths — is epistemically demanding and raises questions about how CED-AH's coherence measurements can account for facts not yet known to the evaluators.
- **Does not address physical grounding.** Like BonJour and Davidson, Lehrer's argument is purely within analytic epistemology and does not connect coherence to thermodynamics or biology.
- **Second edition (2000) context.** While updated from the 1990 first edition, the text predates the AI era and does not address computational epistemology or human-machine knowledge systems.
