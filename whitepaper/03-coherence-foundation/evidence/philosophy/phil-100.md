## Summary

The *Nicomachean Ethics* is Aristotle's most sustained and systematic inquiry into the nature of the human good — the question of what constitutes a well-lived human life. It is the foundational text of virtue ethics and the single most influential work in the Western ethical tradition on the concept of human flourishing.

Aristotle's inquiry begins with a teleological observation: every art, inquiry, action, and pursuit aims at some good (1094a1–3). But goods are nested hierarchically — the good of bridle-making serves the good of horsemanship, which serves the good of military strategy, which serves the good of the political community. There must therefore be some highest good, some ultimate end for the sake of which everything else is pursued but which is not itself pursued for the sake of anything further. This highest good, Aristotle argues, is **eudaimonia** — a term variously translated as "happiness," "flourishing," "well-being," or "living well and doing well" (1095a18–20).

Crucially, Aristotle distinguishes eudaimonia from several things it is commonly confused with. It is not pleasure, though the flourishing life is pleasant. It is not wealth, for wealth is always instrumental — pursued for the sake of something else. It is not honor, for honor depends on the judgment of others and can be bestowed on those who do not deserve it. Eudaimonia is **self-sufficient** (autarkes) — it is the kind of good that, when possessed, makes life lacking in nothing (1097b14–16). And it is **activity** (energeia), not a passive state: eudaimonia is not something that happens to a person but something a person does. Specifically, it is activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, over a complete life (1098a16–18).

The **function argument** (ergon argument) at Book I, Chapter 7 provides the logical structure. If every craftsman, every organ, and every living thing has a function — the function of a flautist is to play the flute, the function of an eye is to see — then a human being as such must have a function. That function is the activity that is distinctive to humans: not mere life (shared with plants), not mere perception (shared with animals), but **rational activity** — the active exercise of the soul's rational capacities, in accordance with virtue, over a complete life. The good life, then, is not a state of feeling but a *pattern of activity* characterized by the excellent use of distinctively human capacities.

In Books II through V, Aristotle develops his account of the moral virtues as stable dispositions to act, feel, and choose in accordance with a mean relative to the agent — neither excess nor deficiency. Courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity is the mean between profligacy and miserliness. These virtues are developed through habituation: one becomes courageous by performing courageous acts, just as one becomes a builder by building. Virtue is not innate but acquired through practice within a community that cultivates it.

Book VI introduces **phronesis** — practical wisdom — as the intellectual virtue essential to the moral virtues. Phronesis is the capacity to deliberate well about what is good and beneficial for oneself and for human beings in general (1140a25–28). It is not theoretical knowledge (episteme) or technical skill (techne) but a distinctive capacity to perceive the morally salient features of particular situations and to act rightly in response. Phronesis cannot be reduced to rules or algorithms; it requires the judgment that comes from experience, maturity, and habituated virtue. The person of practical wisdom *sees* what the situation requires — an act of perception, not calculation.

Book X completes the argument by identifying **contemplation** (theoria) as the highest human activity — the activity of the rational soul's highest capacity (nous) directed at the most worthy objects. Whether Aristotle means that the contemplative life is the only truly flourishing life or that contemplation is one (the highest) component of a broader flourishing life that includes moral virtue and social engagement is one of the most debated questions in Aristotelian scholarship. The inclusive interpretation (Cooper, 1975; Broadie, 1991) reads Aristotle as holding that flourishing includes both contemplative and practical virtues; the dominant or intellectualist interpretation (Kraut, 1989) reads him as holding that contemplation alone constitutes perfect eudaimonia.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the foundational concept** for CED-AH's definition of coherence. CED-AH defines coherence as "internal consistency + alignment with human flourishing requirements." The second element — "alignment with human flourishing requirements" — depends on having a philosophically defensible account of what flourishing is. Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* is the originating and still most influential account. His identification of flourishing as *activity of the soul in accordance with virtue over a complete life* provides CED-AH with a substantive criterion: coherent systems are those that support the active, excellent use of distinctively human capacities. Incoherent systems are those that atrophy, bypass, or replace those capacities.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that flourishing is activity, not a passive state — and therefore that systems which render humans passive undermine flourishing by definition. Aristotle's insistence that eudaimonia is energeia (activity) rather than a feeling or condition means that human well-being cannot be achieved by systems that *do things for* humans. It can only be achieved by systems that enable humans to *do things better*. This is the philosophical foundation of the amplification imperative: CED-AH-compliant systems amplify human activity rather than replacing it because flourishing *just is* activity — not the output of activity, but the activity itself.

**Provides the foundational concept** for CED-AH's account of what systems should amplify. Aristotle's concept of phronesis — practical wisdom as the capacity for context-sensitive moral judgment that cannot be reduced to rules — maps directly onto what CED-AH identifies as the irreplaceable human contribution. Computational systems can process information, identify patterns, and apply rules, but they cannot exercise phronesis because phronesis is not algorithmic. It is the perception of particulars in light of a life shaped by virtue, experience, and habituation. CED-AH's "Authority Retention" imperative (humans make all decisions) is grounded in this Aristotelian insight: the decisions that matter most require phronesis, and phronesis is a human capacity that cannot be externalized.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's "Capability Growth" imperative. Aristotle's account of virtue acquisition through habituation — one becomes virtuous by practicing virtue, and this requires a community that cultivates virtuous practice — parallels CED-AH's requirement that users become more capable over time, not more dependent. Just as Aristotle holds that virtue is developed through practice (not outsourced to tutors or guardians), CED-AH holds that competence must be developed through engagement (not outsourced to computational systems). The failure mode CED-AH identifies — declining performance when the system is unavailable — is precisely the failure of habituation: a user who has not been practicing the relevant capacities cannot exercise them when needed.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by raising the question of whether "flourishing" can bear the weight CED-AH places on it. Aristotle's account of eudaimonia is thick — it involves specific virtues, specific institutions (the polis), specific social relationships (friendship, family, political community), and specific metaphysical commitments (the function argument, teleology). CED-AH's use of "flourishing" is thinner — it functions as a general criterion ("alignment with human flourishing requirements") without fully specifying what flourishing demands. Whether this thinning is a legitimate abstraction or a loss of philosophical substance is a question CED-AH must address. The risk is that "flourishing" becomes so general as to be vacuous — a word that confers philosophical authority without providing philosophical content.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Nicomachean Ethics* is the most influential single text in the Western ethical tradition on the concept of human flourishing, and Aristotle's account has survived 2,300 years of philosophical scrutiny, challenge, and development. The philosophical substance is beyond question. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because the connection to CED-AH requires interpretive work across a vast historical and conceptual distance. Aristotle wrote for the Greek polis, not for the age of artificial intelligence. Applying his concepts to computational systems requires interpretive translation that introduces unavoidable anachronism risks. The evidence establishes the foundational concept but does not directly demonstrate CED-AH's specific claims about technology.

## Limitations

- **Historical distance.** The *Nicomachean Ethics* was composed circa 340 BCE for an audience of free male citizens of the Greek polis. Its conceptual framework presupposes social, political, and metaphysical conditions radically different from those of the 21st century. Applying Aristotelian concepts to computational systems requires interpretive translation that introduces anachronism risks at every step.
- **The function argument is contested.** Aristotle's identification of the human function (ergon) as rational activity has been challenged on multiple grounds: that humans have no single function (Wilkes, 1978), that the function argument commits a compositional fallacy (Whiting, 1988), and that teleological reasoning about human nature is incompatible with post-Darwinian biology (Foot, 2001, offers a qualified defense; Thompson, 2008, offers a more robust one). CED-AH's reliance on "flourishing" must reckon with these challenges.
- **Translation and interpretation.** "Eudaimonia" does not straightforwardly mean "happiness" or "flourishing" — it is a technical term in Aristotle's philosophy whose precise content is disputed even among Aristotle scholars. CED-AH's use of "flourishing" draws on the Aristotelian concept but may not track all of its commitments (e.g., the role of external goods, the primacy of contemplation, the necessity of the polis).
- **Exclusionary context.** Aristotle's account of flourishing was explicitly restricted to free adult male citizens; women, slaves, and non-Greeks were excluded from the possibility of full eudaimonia. CED-AH's universalist commitment to "human flourishing" implicitly corrects this exclusion but should acknowledge it. The correction is philosophically warranted (Nussbaum, 1988, 1990, 2000) but should be made explicit.
- **Phronesis is not fully defined.** Aristotle's account of practical wisdom is notoriously difficult to pin down — he characterizes it through examples and negation (it is not episteme, not techne, not cleverness) rather than through a precise definition. Whether CED-AH's reliance on an irreducibly human judgment capacity maps onto Aristotle's phronesis or is doing different philosophical work requires further analysis.
- **The contemplation problem.** If Aristotle's highest form of flourishing is contemplation (theoria), and if contemplation is an activity that could in principle be better performed by computational systems (which can process information without fatigue, distraction, or error), then Aristotle's framework might paradoxically support rather than undermine the replacement of human activity. CED-AH must distinguish its account of flourishing from interpretations of Aristotle that would license this conclusion.
