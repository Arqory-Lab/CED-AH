## Summary

Ernest Sosa's *A Virtue Epistemology* represents a major attempt to transcend the foundationalism-coherentism debate by reframing epistemology around the concept of intellectual virtue. Sosa argues that the traditional debate is misdirected: the fundamental question is not about the *structure* of justification (linear vs. holistic) but about the *qualities of the knower* that make knowledge possible.

Sosa introduces a three-level analysis of belief quality. An "accurate" belief is simply true. An "adroit" belief is skillfully formed — it results from the exercise of a competence or intellectual virtue. An "apt" belief is accurate *because* adroit — it is true because of the knower's competence, not by luck. Knowledge, on Sosa's account, is apt belief.

Sosa further distinguishes between "animal knowledge" (apt belief, where the competence operates reliably) and "reflective knowledge" (apt belief that the agent can reflectively endorse — the agent knows that their belief is apt). Reflective knowledge requires a kind of coherence: the agent must have a coherent understanding of why their cognitive processes are reliable. This introduces a coherentist element at the reflective level while allowing for a quasi-foundationalist structure at the animal level.

Sosa's framework is ecumenical: it accommodates foundationalist insights (some cognitive processes are basic and reliable) and coherentist insights (reflective endorsement requires coherence) within a unified virtue-theoretic framework. He argues this resolves the impasse between the two traditions.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Qualifies** CED-AH's pure coherentist position while providing a constructive alternative framing. Sosa's virtue epistemology suggests that the foundationalism-coherentism debate — which CED-AH resolves in favor of coherentism — may be less fundamental than CED-AH assumes. If what matters is intellectual virtue (competence, apt performance), then the structural question (coherentist vs. foundationalist) is secondary.

However, Sosa's reflective level — where agents must coherently understand why their cognitive processes are reliable — converges with CED-AH's position. CED-AH could be reframed in virtue-theoretic terms: a coherent computational system is one that amplifies the intellectual virtues of its users (competence, understanding, reflective capacity) rather than substituting for them. This maps onto CED-AH's S4 (amplification vs. substitution) from an epistemological direction.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's commitment S2 (flourishing requires agency, capability, understanding, meaning). Sosa's intellectual virtues — competences that produce apt belief — are a subset of the capabilities CED-AH identifies as constitutive of flourishing. If computational systems degrade intellectual virtue (by substituting for human cognitive competence), they reduce the conditions for reflective knowledge, which is a form of coherence degradation.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's epistemological positioning. Sosa represents one of the most sophisticated contemporary responses to the foundationalism-coherentism impasse. CED-AH should engage with virtue epistemology rather than treating the debate as settled in coherentism's favor.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Sosa's work is among the most important contributions to contemporary epistemology and provides a sophisticated alternative to the foundationalism-coherentism framing that CED-AH adopts. The connection to CED-AH's concerns (particularly S2 and S4) is genuine but indirect: Sosa is concerned with the structure of individual knowledge, not with evaluating computational systems. The virtue-theoretic reframing of CED-AH's commitments is suggestive but has not been developed in the literature.

## Limitations

- **Virtue epistemology is not coherentism.** Sosa explicitly positions his account as transcending the foundationalism-coherentism debate. CED-AH's adoption of coherentism as its epistemic framework may be premature if Sosa's ecumenical approach is superior. However, this is a qualification rather than a refutation — Sosa's reflective level is coherentist in structure.
- **Individual-level analysis.** Sosa's account is about individual knowers and their intellectual virtues. CED-AH operates at the institutional and systemic level (how computational systems affect populations). The extension requires significant bridging.
- **No engagement with technology.** Sosa does not address how computational tools affect intellectual virtue. The question of whether a calculator degrades mathematical competence (substitution) or enhances mathematical capability (amplification) is the kind of question CED-AH addresses but Sosa does not.
- **"Apt belief" may not capture CED-AH's concerns.** CED-AH is concerned with coherence broadly construed (including flourishing alignment), not just with apt belief. Sosa's framework may illuminate the epistemic dimension of CED-AH but does not address the ethical and political dimensions.
- **Volume I of a multi-volume project.** Sosa's full account extends through *Reflective Knowledge* (2009) and *Judgment and Agency* (2015). Drawing solely on Volume I provides an incomplete picture of his mature position.
