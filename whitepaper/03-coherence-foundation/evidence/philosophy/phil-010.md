## Summary

Wilfrid Sellars' "Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind" is one of the most important and difficult essays in twentieth-century epistemology. Its central achievement is the critique of what Sellars calls "the Myth of the Given" — the foundationalist assumption that there exists a level of non-conceptual, pre-theoretical awareness (the "given") that serves as the epistemic foundation for all knowledge.

Sellars argues that what appears to be immediately "given" in experience — raw sensory data, sense impressions, the "look" of things — is already conceptually structured. To report that something "looks red" is to place that experience within a conceptual framework that includes the concept of redness, the distinction between appearance and reality, and background knowledge about normal observing conditions. There is no epistemic stratum below the conceptual.

The implications are devastating for foundationalism. If the "given" is a myth — if there is no non-conceptual epistemic foundation — then the foundationalist picture of knowledge (justified ultimately by basic, non-inferential, non-conceptual beliefs) collapses. Sellars proposes instead that knowledge is essentially social and inferential: to know something is to be able to place it within the "space of reasons," the network of justificatory relationships that constitutes rational discourse.

Sellars distinguishes between the "manifest image" (the common-sense framework through which we understand ourselves as persons in a world of persons) and the "scientific image" (the framework of theoretical science). He argues that both are conceptual achievements, neither is simply "given," and the relationship between them is a central philosophical problem.

The essay also develops the concept of "epistemic authority" — the idea that claims to knowledge carry normative force and that attributing knowledge is attributing a normative status within the space of reasons.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's rejection of foundationalism and its coherentist orientation. Sellars' critique of the Myth of the Given is the single most important argument against epistemic foundationalism in twentieth-century philosophy. If there is no non-conceptual "given," then beliefs cannot be justified by correspondence to raw experience — they can only be justified by their place in the space of reasons, which is a coherentist picture.

CED-AH's T1 — "any evaluation of claims presupposes that coherent claims are preferred over incoherent ones" — is a claim about the space of reasons: evaluation presupposes a framework of rational relationships. Sellars provides the deep philosophical grounding for why this must be so: there is no escape from the conceptual, and therefore no escape from the inferential and coherentist structure of justification.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's claim about human authority over computational systems. Sellars' concept of "epistemic authority" — the idea that knowledge claims carry normative force and that authority is a status within the space of reasons — has implications for CED-AH's Imperative 1 (Authority Retention). If epistemic authority is normative and social, then transferring "authority" to computational systems (which are not participants in the space of reasons) is a category error. This reinforces CED-AH's S3 (computational systems are instruments, not agents) from an epistemological direction.

**Provides necessary context** for the coherentist tradition that flows from Sellars through Davidson, Brandom, and into contemporary epistemology. Without understanding the Myth of the Given, CED-AH's coherentist position appears as one option among several; with Sellars, it appears as the consequence of the collapse of the foundationalist alternative.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Sellars' essay is a watershed in twentieth-century epistemology, and the critique of the Myth of the Given has been enormously influential (developed further by Davidson, Rorty, McDowell, and Brandom). The argument directly undermines foundationalism — the primary competitor to CED-AH's coherentist position. The connection is structural, not merely analogical: Sellars demonstrates that the space of reasons (the domain in which CED-AH's coherence operates) is conceptual all the way down.

## Limitations

- **Sellars' positive proposal is contested.** While the critique of the Given is widely accepted, Sellars' positive account of knowledge as placement in the space of reasons has been interpreted in divergent ways (scientific realist by Sellars himself, pragmatist by Rorty, inferentialist by Brandom). CED-AH's use of Sellars must specify which interpretation it relies on.
- **The "space of reasons" may not extend to system evaluation.** Sellars develops the space of reasons for propositional knowledge. Whether it applies to the evaluative practices CED-AH concerns itself with (assessing whether computational systems increase or decrease coherence) requires argumentative extension.
- **Difficulty and interpretive controversy.** The essay is notoriously dense, and scholarly interpretation varies widely. Different readings of Sellars support different epistemological conclusions. CED-AH should be explicit about which reading it adopts.
- **No engagement with physical grounding.** Sellars' critique is conceptual and linguistic. He does not connect the space of reasons to thermodynamics or biological coherence. CED-AH's physical grounding argument operates on a different register.
- **Historical context.** Written in 1956, the essay does not address computational systems, artificial intelligence, or human-machine interaction. The application to CED-AH's concerns is plausible but anachronistic — Sellars could not have anticipated the specific questions CED-AH raises.
