## Summary

The *Summa Theologica* is Thomas Aquinas's (1225–1274) most comprehensive and systematic philosophical-theological work — an encyclopedic synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy, Christian theology, Neoplatonism, and Islamic philosophy that remains the foundational text of the Thomistic intellectual tradition. It is structured as a series of "questions," each subdivided into "articles" that follow a rigorous dialectical format: objections to a thesis are stated, a counter-position is given ("On the contrary..."), and then Aquinas provides his own determination ("I answer that...") followed by replies to each objection. This structure — which anticipates CED-AH's commitment to formally contestable claims — reflects Aquinas's conviction that truth emerges through the rigorous consideration of opposing positions, not through the suppression of dissent.

The passages relevant to CED-AH fall primarily in two areas: the account of intellect (Ia, Q.79) and the treatise on law (Ia-IIae, QQ.90–97), particularly the doctrine of natural law (Q.94).

**On intellect (Ia, Q.79):** Aquinas distinguishes between the **agent intellect** (intellectus agens) and the **possible intellect** (intellectus possibilis). The agent intellect is the active power that abstracts universal concepts (intelligible forms) from the data of sense experience — it "illuminates" the phantasmata (sensory images) to reveal their intelligible content. The possible intellect is the receptive capacity that receives and retains these abstracted forms. This is not merely a psychological theory but a metaphysical one: understanding, for Aquinas, is an active process of rational engagement with reality — the mind does not passively receive information but actively constitutes knowledge through the exercise of its own rational powers. The intellect is not a mechanism that processes inputs; it is a capacity that *understands* — and understanding is an irreducibly active, rational achievement.

Aquinas explicitly addresses the question of whether the intellect operates mechanically. He argues that it does not: the intellect is a power of the rational soul that operates through judgment (iudicium), not through automatic response. The intellect evaluates, compares, and assesses — it does not merely register. This distinction between mechanical response and rational judgment foreshadows the modern question of whether computational information processing constitutes understanding (Searle's Chinese Room) and is directly relevant to CED-AH's instrument thesis.

**On law (Ia-IIae, QQ.90–97):** Aquinas develops a four-tier theory of law that is one of the most influential systematizations in the history of legal and political philosophy:

1. **Eternal Law** (lex aeterna): The rational plan of God governing the entire universe — the ultimate ordering principle of all reality. Everything that exists participates in the eternal law by being governed according to its nature.

2. **Natural Law** (lex naturalis): The participation of rational creatures in the eternal law through the exercise of reason. Humans, unlike stones or animals, participate in divine governance *actively* — through rational apprehension of the principles that govern human flourishing. Natural law is not a set of arbitrary divine commands but the rational structure of human good that reason can discover because it is built into the nature of reality itself.

3. **Human Law** (lex humana): Positive law enacted by human communities — legitimate when derived from natural law through rational determination, illegitimate when it contradicts natural law.

4. **Divine Law** (lex divina): Revealed law (Scripture) — necessary because human reason is fallible and because the supernatural end of human life (beatitude) exceeds what reason alone can discover.

The natural law doctrine (Q.94) is the passage most directly relevant to CED-AH. Aquinas argues that the first precept of natural law is that "good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided" (Q.94, a.2). This is not a mere tautology — it is Aquinas's claim that practical reason has a built-in orientation toward the good, just as theoretical reason has a built-in orientation toward truth. The principles of natural law are discoverable by reason because they are *instantiated in the structure of human nature and the nature of reality itself*. They are not conventions, preferences, or cultural constructs — they are objective features of the rational order.

Aquinas further argues that natural law is universal (applicable to all human beings), immutable in its first principles (though its secondary applications may vary with circumstances), and knowable through reason alone (not requiring revelation for its basic content). The rational structure of human good — that life, knowledge, sociality, practical reasonableness, and other basic goods are intrinsically worthwhile — is accessible to any reasoning being because it is built into the nature of things.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Supports by structural analogy** CED-AH's claim that coherence is physically grounded and rationally discoverable, not merely posited. Aquinas's natural law theory holds that moral principles are not imposed on reality from outside but are *discoverable within reality through the exercise of reason* — they are built into the structure of human nature and the nature of things. CED-AH makes a structurally parallel claim about coherence: coherence is not a value that CED-AH chooses or posits but a feature of reality that is physically instantiated and rationally unavoidable. Just as Aquinas argues that the first precept of practical reason ("good is to be done and pursued") is self-evident because to deny it is to presuppose it, CED-AH argues that coherence is the unavoidable first principle because any argument against coherence must presuppose coherence to be intelligible. The structural parallel — a first principle that is self-grounding because its denial requires its affirmation — is precise, even though the content differs (Aquinas's "good" is theologically grounded; CED-AH's "coherence" is physically grounded).

**Directly supports** CED-AH's tiered axiom structure through structural precedent. Aquinas's four-tier hierarchy of law (eternal → natural → human → divine) provides a historical precedent for CED-AH's own tiered structure (T1 → S1-S6 → B1-B4 → Design Imperatives). In both systems, the highest tier establishes the foundational principle from which lower tiers derive their authority and content. In both systems, lower tiers apply the foundational principle to increasingly specific domains. In both systems, the legitimacy of any lower-tier principle depends on its coherence with the higher tiers — human law that contradicts natural law is illegitimate in Aquinas's framework; a design imperative that contradicts T1 would be illegitimate in CED-AH's framework. The parallel is structural, not substantive: CED-AH does not adopt Aquinas's theology, but it inherits the architectural insight that a comprehensive normative framework requires a tiered structure with a self-grounding first principle.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's claim that the intellect operates through judgment, not mechanism. Aquinas's distinction between the agent intellect (active rational abstraction) and mechanical response anticipates the modern question of whether computational systems "understand" or merely process information. For Aquinas, understanding is an irreducibly rational activity of a rational soul — it is not mere pattern matching, data processing, or algorithmic transformation. A system that processes symbols according to rules is not understanding those symbols, however sophisticated its output. This Thomistic position aligns with CED-AH's instrument thesis: computational systems process information but do not understand it, because understanding requires the kind of active rational engagement that Aquinas attributes to the intellect — a power of the rational soul that no mechanism, however complex, possesses.

**Provides historical depth** for CED-AH's contestability architecture. The *Summa Theologica*'s dialectical structure — objections, counter-positions, determinations, and replies — is itself a model of the kind of structured intellectual engagement that CED-AH aims to instantiate in its evidence system. Aquinas does not merely assert his positions; he articulates the strongest objections, takes them seriously, and responds to them systematically. CED-AH's framework, in which "every claim can be traced to its supporting evidence, and every axiom can be formally contested" (README), echoes this Thomistic commitment to intellectual rigor through structured adversarial engagement.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by demonstrating the theological dependence of the original natural law framework. Aquinas's natural law derives its authority ultimately from the eternal law — from God's rational governance of the universe. Remove the theological foundation, and the question arises: what grounds the natural law? Can "natural law" be secularized without losing its normative force? CED-AH faces a structurally similar challenge: coherence is claimed to be physically grounded, but whether physical grounding provides sufficient normative authority — whether "is" can ground "ought" — is precisely the question that the naturalistic fallacy (Moore, 1903) and Hume's guillotine challenge. CED-AH must demonstrate that its physical grounding of coherence avoids the problems that have plagued secularized natural law theories since the Enlightenment.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Suggestive

**Justification:** The *Summa Theologica* is one of the most influential systematic philosophical works in the Western tradition, and Aquinas's natural law theory remains a live philosophical position with sophisticated modern defenders (Finnis, 1980; Grisez, 1983; Murphy, 2001). The structural parallels between Aquinas's tiered law framework and CED-AH's tiered axiom structure, and between Aquinas's self-grounding first precept and CED-AH's self-grounding coherence principle, are genuine and illuminating. The rating is "Suggestive" rather than "Moderate" because: (1) the framework is fundamentally theological, and the secularization required to apply it to CED-AH removes the very foundation that gives Aquinas's system its authority; (2) the historical distance (750 years) is vast; (3) the parallel is structural rather than substantive — CED-AH does not adopt Thomistic metaphysics, natural law theory, or theology; and (4) the connection to computational systems specifically requires interpretive bridging that Aquinas's text does not and could not provide.

## Limitations

- **Theological foundation.** Aquinas's entire natural law framework depends on the existence of God as the author of the eternal law. The "rational structure of reality" that natural law discloses is, for Aquinas, a rational structure that exists because God created it rationally. CED-AH is a secular framework. Whether the structural parallel survives the removal of the theological ground is an open question — one that the history of secularized natural law theory (from Grotius through contemporary natural law theorists) suggests is difficult but not impossible.
- **Historical distance.** Aquinas wrote in the 13th century for a Christian intellectual community that shared his metaphysical, theological, and cosmological assumptions. Applying his concepts to 21st-century computational systems requires interpretive translation across an enormous philosophical and cultural gap.
- **The is-ought problem.** Aquinas's natural law appears to derive normative conclusions ("good is to be done") from factual premises about human nature. Whether this move is legitimate — whether you can derive "ought" from "is" — has been debated since Hume and remains contested. CED-AH's physical grounding of coherence faces the same challenge. The parallel is instructive but not reassuring: it shows that CED-AH is attempting something that has faced persistent philosophical resistance for centuries.
- **Contested modern reception.** Natural law theory is a live but contested philosophical position. Critics (e.g., Hare, 1952; Mackie, 1977) argue that natural law commits the naturalistic fallacy, presupposes metaphysical commitments that modernity has abandoned, or smuggles theological premises into ostensibly rational arguments. CED-AH should not present the Thomistic parallel as uncontroversial philosophical support.
- **The intellect argument presupposes hylomorphism.** Aquinas's account of the agent intellect depends on his Aristotelian hylomorphic metaphysics — the doctrine that substances are composites of form and matter. Whether the argument that understanding is irreducibly rational (and therefore non-mechanical) survives outside this metaphysical framework requires independent philosophical argument.
- **No direct engagement with technology.** Aquinas did not contemplate anything resembling computational systems. All applications of his framework to AI and technology are modern extensions that cannot be attributed to Aquinas himself.
