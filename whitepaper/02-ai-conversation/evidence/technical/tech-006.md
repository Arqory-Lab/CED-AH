## Summary

Iain Gabriel's 2020 paper provides the most systematic philosophical analysis of the "value alignment" problem in AI — the challenge of building AI systems whose behavior reflects human values. Gabriel argues that before we can align AI with human values, we must answer a prior question that the technical literature largely ignores: *whose* values, and *which* values?

Gabriel distinguishes several possible alignment targets: (1) instructions — align the system with what the user literally says; (2) expressed preferences — align with what users say they want; (3) revealed preferences — align with what users' behavior reveals they want; (4) informed preferences — align with what users *would* want if they had full information; (5) interest — align with what is objectively good for users; and (6) values — align with what users' deep moral commitments require.

Each target has distinct advantages and problems. Instructions are clear but often incomplete or contradictory. Expressed preferences are accessible but may be biased, ill-informed, or manipulated. Revealed preferences have the advantage of being observable but face the problem of distinguishing genuine preference from habit, addiction, or manipulation. Informed preferences are theoretically attractive but practically inaccessible (we cannot know what someone would prefer under idealized conditions). Interest-based alignment requires a theory of objective well-being that is philosophically contested. Values-based alignment requires knowing which values to prioritize when values conflict.

Gabriel argues that no single alignment target is adequate, and that the choice of target is itself a value-laden decision with profound political implications. "Aligning AI with human values" sounds neutral but conceals deep disagreements about whose values count, how to handle value pluralism, and who has the authority to make these choices.

The paper is philosophically sophisticated and written by a researcher embedded in the AI safety community (Gabriel is a research scientist at DeepMind), giving it both philosophical rigor and insider credibility.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's diagnosis of definitional incoherence in the alignment discourse. Gabriel's analysis demonstrates precisely the problem CED-AH identifies: "alignment" lacks a clear, shared definition, and the choice of alignment target is itself a contested value judgment. The alignment field pursues a goal it cannot define — which is, by CED-AH's standards, a coherence failure.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that coherence provides the criterion the alignment field lacks. Gabriel's six alignment targets can be evaluated by CED-AH's coherence criterion: which target most consistently increases human flourishing (understanding, capability, agency, meaning) over time? CED-AH's S1 (coherence = internal consistency + flourishing alignment) provides exactly the adjudicating principle Gabriel finds missing. This does not eliminate value pluralism, but it provides a principled basis for evaluating alignment targets — a basis the alignment field currently lacks.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by raising a challenge the framework must address. If the coherence criterion is to resolve the value alignment problem, CED-AH must show that "human flourishing" is not itself a contested concept vulnerable to the same pluralism Gabriel identifies. Gabriel's analysis suggests that any criterion — including coherence — must address the "whose flourishing?" question. CED-AH's S2 (flourishing requires agency, capability, understanding, meaning) provides a partial answer, but CED-AH should engage with Gabriel's challenge explicitly.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's insistence on human authority. Gabriel's analysis reveals that value alignment decisions are inherently political — they determine whose values shape powerful systems. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative ensures these decisions remain with humans rather than being encoded (perhaps inadvertently) by system designers. Gabriel provides the political philosophy justification for why authority retention matters.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Gabriel's paper is philosophically rigorous, published in a leading journal, and directly addresses the foundational question CED-AH claims the alignment field has not answered. The analysis is valuable both as a diagnosis of the alignment field's conceptual confusion and as a challenge to CED-AH's own coherence criterion. The paper's position at the intersection of philosophy and AI safety research gives it authority in both domains.

---

## Limitations

- **Gabriel does not offer a resolution.** The paper identifies the problem (which values?) but does not solve it. CED-AH's coherence criterion is a proposed solution, but whether it actually resolves Gabriel's challenge is an open question.
- **The analysis presupposes the agency assumption.** Gabriel asks which values to align an agent with, presupposing that the system is an agent that can be aligned. CED-AH would reframe: if systems are instruments, the question is not "whose values should the system pursue?" but "whose authority should the system amplify?"
- **Political implications are underexplored.** Gabriel raises the political dimension of alignment but does not fully develop it. The question of who decides alignment targets — and the power dynamics involved — deserves more sustained treatment than the paper provides.
- **No engagement with coherence as a criterion.** Gabriel does not consider the possibility that coherence (or any specific philosophical first principle) could adjudicate among alignment targets. CED-AH must make this case independently.
- **The six targets may not be exhaustive.** CED-AH's approach — designing systems that amplify human capability rather than aligning system behavior with human values — represents an alternative that does not fit neatly into Gabriel's taxonomy.
