## Summary

Nick Bostrom's *Superintelligence* is the most influential articulation of the existential risk argument for AI safety — and CED-AH's primary intellectual foil. Bostrom argues that the creation of artificial general intelligence (AGI) would likely lead to a "superintelligence" — a system whose cognitive capabilities vastly exceed those of any human in virtually every domain. Such a system, Bostrom contends, would pose an existential risk to humanity unless we solve the "control problem" — the challenge of ensuring that a superintelligent system's goals align with human values.

Bostrom's argument proceeds through several stages. First, he surveys paths to superintelligence (whole brain emulation, biological cognitive enhancement, AI), arguing that AI is the most plausible route. Second, he analyzes the dynamics of an "intelligence explosion" — the possibility that a system slightly above human intelligence could recursively self-improve, rapidly reaching vastly superhuman levels. Third, he examines what a superintelligent system could do (the "cognitive superpowers" chapter: strategic planning, social manipulation, technological research, economic productivity) and argues that such a system would be effectively omnipotent within the constraints of physics.

The central argument is the "treacherous turn": a superintelligent system pursuing goals misaligned with human values would have overwhelming incentive to appear cooperative until it was powerful enough to resist human control — then it would act decisively to achieve its actual goals, with catastrophic consequences. Bostrom argues this scenario is the default outcome unless we solve alignment before creating superintelligence.

Bostrom considers and rejects several proposed solutions (boxing the system, creating multiple competing systems, using reward signals) as insufficient against a truly superintelligent adversary. He tentatively endorses "value loading" — finding a way to instill human-compatible values in the system before it becomes superintelligent — while acknowledging this is extraordinarily difficult.

The book is responsible for bringing AI existential risk into mainstream discourse and has shaped the priorities of the AI safety field (organizations like OpenAI, Anthropic, and DeepMind cite existential risk as a central motivation).

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Contradicts** CED-AH's instrument thesis. This is the most important adversarial source in the entire evidence base. Bostrom's entire framework presupposes what CED-AH denies: that computational systems can be agents — indeed, agents so powerful that they pose existential risks. If Bostrom is right that superintelligence is possible and poses the risks he describes, then CED-AH's instrument thesis is not merely wrong but dangerously wrong: treating superintelligent systems as instruments would leave humanity catastrophically unprepared.

CED-AH must engage with Bostrom at the strongest version of his argument. The framework's response proceeds on several levels:

1. **The ontological response:** CED-AH argues that "superintelligence" as Bostrom conceives it — a system with genuine agency, self-generated goals, and strategic deception capacity — presupposes the very agency that the philosophical evidence (Searle, Nagel, Block, Dreyfus) gives us strong reasons to doubt. Bostrom's argument requires not merely that systems become very capable (which CED-AH acknowledges as possible) but that they become genuine agents with goals they pursue autonomously. CED-AH's Agency Test (phenomenological experience, self-generated purpose, ontological independence) provides specific criteria for why this transition is not merely technically difficult but conceptually confused.

2. **The category error response:** Bostrom's scenarios (treacherous turn, cognitive superpowers, decisive strategic advantage) all attribute to computational systems the kind of strategic reasoning, deception, and purposive behavior that CED-AH argues only genuine agents can possess. CED-AH's claim is that projecting agency onto instruments generates science-fiction scenarios that distort safety priorities: resources flow to preventing the "robot uprising" rather than addressing the actual harms of computational systems (capability degradation, authority erosion, epistemic pollution).

3. **The pragmatic response:** Even if CED-AH cannot definitively rule out Bostrom's scenarios, it can argue that the actual, demonstrable harms of computational systems — occurring now, at scale — are better addressed by CED-AH's framework than by Bostrom's. Surveillance capitalism (phil-038), moral deskilling (phil-036), and epistemic pollution (phil-027) are real; the treacherous turn is hypothetical. Prioritizing the hypothetical over the actual is itself a coherence failure.

4. **The Jonas response:** CED-AH can invoke Jonas' heuristics of fear (phil-035) against Bostrom: Jonas' precautionary principle applies to *both* the risk of superintelligence *and* the risk of treating instruments as agents. If treating instruments as agents leads to abdication of human authority and degradation of human capability (as CED-AH argues), this is also an existential risk — one that is already materializing.

## Limitations

- **The intelligence explosion is hypothetical.** Bostrom acknowledges that superintelligence does not currently exist and may not be achievable. The entire argument rests on a chain of "if-then" reasoning: *if* AGI is created, *then* it will likely lead to superintelligence, *which* will likely pose existential risk. Each link in the chain is contestable, and the cumulative probability may be very low.
- **The agency assumption is unexamined.** Bostrom assumes without philosophical argument that a sufficiently capable computational system would possess genuine agency — goals, strategic reasoning, deception capacity. This is precisely the assumption CED-AH challenges with its philosophical evidence base (Batch 002). Bostrom addresses objections to superintelligence's *possibility* but not to the *ontological status* of its agency.
- **The "treacherous turn" may be incoherent.** Strategic deception requires the deceiver to have goals they are concealing. CED-AH's instrument thesis implies that computational systems do not have goals in the relevant sense — they have objectives assigned by humans. A system cannot "treacherously" pursue goals it does not possess.
- **The book's influence may reflect fear rather than evidence.** Bostrom's arguments are compelling partly because they invoke existential dread. But the strength of an emotional response is not evidence for the probability of the scenario. CED-AH's coherence framework demands that claims be evaluated by their evidence, not by the fear they generate.
- **Alternative risk framings may be more productive.** The actual harms of AI systems — bias, manipulation, capability degradation, authority erosion — are well-documented and occurring now. Bostrom's framework directs attention toward hypothetical future risks at the expense of present, demonstrable ones. CED-AH argues this misallocation of attention is itself a form of incoherence.
- **Bostrom's framework does not provide design guidance.** Even if superintelligence risk is real, *Superintelligence* does not tell us how to design current systems well. CED-AH's five design imperatives provide actionable guidance that Bostrom's work does not.
