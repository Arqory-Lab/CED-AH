## Summary

Luciano Floridi and Massimiliano Chiriatti's 2020 analysis of GPT-3 provides one of the earliest rigorous philosophical assessments of large language models by a major philosopher of information. Written shortly after GPT-3's release, the paper addresses the central question that LLMs raised with new urgency: does a system that produces human-quality text understand what it is producing?

Floridi and Chiriatti argue emphatically that it does not. Their central claim is that GPT-3 is "the wrong kind of AI" — not because it is technically unsophisticated (they acknowledge its impressive performance) but because the appearance of understanding is generated by statistical pattern matching over text, not by any form of semantic comprehension. They describe GPT-3 as an "agentless" system: it has no model of the world, no understanding of the concepts it manipulates, no intentions or beliefs. It produces text by predicting the statistically most likely next token given a prompt, and the resulting text is coherent because human language has statistical regularities that can be exploited without comprehension.

Floridi and Chiriatti develop the analogy of a "Clever Hans" — the famous horse that appeared to perform arithmetic by reading subtle cues from its handler. GPT-3, they argue, is a vastly more sophisticated Clever Hans: it produces outputs that observers interpret as evidence of understanding, but the mechanism behind those outputs is fundamentally different from human understanding. The appearance of comprehension is in the eye of the beholder, not in the system.

The paper also addresses consequences: Floridi and Chiriatti warn that GPT-3's capacity to produce plausible but semantically empty text poses risks for disinformation, epistemic pollution, and the erosion of trust in written communication. They argue that the real danger of LLMs is not that they will become conscious or rebellious but that their outputs will be treated as meaningful when they are merely statistically well-formed.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's instrument thesis with contemporary, LLM-specific analysis. Floridi and Chiriatti's characterization of GPT-3 as "agentless" — producing text without understanding, intention, or belief — directly instantiates CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments, not agents. Their analysis demonstrates that the instrument thesis is not a holdover from the symbolic AI era but applies with full force to the most capable systems of the present.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Category Error argument. Floridi and Chiriatti's Clever Hans analogy is a precise illustration of the category error CED-AH diagnoses: observers interpret functional outputs as evidence of understanding because humans are disposed to attribute understanding to systems that produce contextually appropriate language. The error is not in the system but in the interpretation — exactly CED-AH's point.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's concerns about coherence degradation. Floridi and Chiriatti's warning about "epistemic pollution" — the flooding of the information environment with plausible but semantically empty text — connects to CED-AH's claim that systems designed under the agent assumption erode human coherence. If LLMs degrade the epistemic environment by producing text that looks like knowledge but isn't, they decrease the conditions for human understanding and agency — precisely the coherence damage CED-AH's measurement framework aims to track.

**Provides necessary context** as contemporary philosophical analysis by a leading figure in information philosophy. Floridi's engagement with AI is extensive and philosophically sophisticated; his assessment carries significant authority in the field.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** This paper is among the first philosophically rigorous assessments of LLMs by a major philosopher and has been widely cited in subsequent literature. Floridi is one of the most influential philosophers of information and technology, and Chiriatti brings technical expertise. The analysis directly addresses the system type (LLMs) most relevant to CED-AH's contemporary claims, making the connection immediate rather than requiring historical bridging. The "agentless" characterization aligns precisely with CED-AH's instrument thesis.

## Limitations

- **Based on GPT-3, not later models.** The analysis was published shortly after GPT-3's release. Subsequent models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, etc.) exhibit capabilities that may challenge some of Floridi and Chiriatti's specific claims, though not necessarily their philosophical conclusions. Whether the arguments scale to more capable systems is an open question.
- **The "no understanding" claim is asserted more than demonstrated.** Floridi and Chiriatti argue that LLMs do not understand, but demonstrating this with rigor requires specifying what "understanding" requires — a notoriously difficult philosophical task. Their analysis relies on the intuition that statistical pattern matching is not understanding, but functionalists (and Dennett, phil-023) would challenge this intuition.
- **Floridi's own framework complicates the picture.** In earlier work (phil-034), Floridi and Sanders argued that artificial agents can be moral agents at a certain level of abstraction. The relationship between Floridi's "levels of abstraction" framework and his GPT-3 analysis requires careful navigation. CED-AH should note this internal tension in Floridi's work.
- **The Clever Hans analogy has limits.** Clever Hans responded to specific cues from a single handler. LLMs respond to arbitrarily novel prompts from arbitrary users. The analogy captures the mechanism (cue-response without comprehension) but may understate the generality and flexibility of LLM capabilities.
- **Short paper format.** At 14 pages, the analysis is necessarily compressed. More sustained philosophical engagement with the nature of LLMs has since been developed by Shanahan (phil-032) and others.
