## Summary

Daniel Dennett's *The Intentional Stance* develops one of the most influential and provocative positions in philosophy of mind: the view that attributing beliefs, desires, and intentions to a system is a predictive strategy rather than a discovery about the system's inner nature. Dennett argues that we adopt the "intentional stance" toward any system whose behavior is best predicted by treating it *as if* it had beliefs, desires, and rationality. We do this with people, animals, thermostats, and chess-playing computers — and in each case, the attribution is justified by its predictive success, not by metaphysical claims about what is "really" going on inside.

Dennett distinguishes three stances: the physical stance (predicting behavior from physical properties), the design stance (predicting behavior from functional design), and the intentional stance (predicting behavior from attributed beliefs and desires). The intentional stance is the most powerful for complex systems because it abstracts away from physical and design details, capturing the patterns that matter for prediction.

Crucially, Dennett argues there is no deeper fact about whether a system "really" has beliefs — if the intentional stance works, the system is an intentional system. There are no "original" intentional states (possessed by humans) versus "derived" intentional states (merely attributed to machines). Intentionality, for Dennett, is not a substance or a property hidden inside the system; it is a pattern visible from the outside. This is a deflationary account: it lowers the bar for what counts as a "believer" by making belief-attribution a matter of predictive utility rather than ontological discovery.

Dennett applies this framework to argue that the question "Does this computer really think?" is malformed. The right question is: "Is the intentional stance the best predictive strategy for this system?" For sufficiently complex computational systems, the answer is yes — and that is all "thinking" amounts to.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Complicates** CED-AH's instrument thesis and Agency Test. This is the most important adversarial source in this batch. Dennett's intentional stance directly challenges CED-AH's central ontological claim — that computational systems are instruments, not agents — by arguing that the instrument/agent distinction is not a deep metaphysical fact but a matter of which predictive strategy works best. If Dennett is right, then CED-AH's claim that computational systems lack "genuine" agency, beliefs, or understanding rests on a distinction (real vs. attributed intentionality) that Dennett has argued is incoherent.

Specifically, CED-AH's Agency Test requires phenomenological experience, self-generated purpose, and ontological independence. Dennett would reject all three as requirements for agency: phenomenological experience is either A-consciousness (which computational systems can have) or an incoherent concept; "self-generated purpose" is a romantic notion that applies no more to humans than to thermostats (both have purposes attributed by the intentional stance); and "ontological independence" is irrelevant since agency is a pattern, not a substance.

**However**, CED-AH has resources to respond. Dennett's position has a practical implication that CED-AH can exploit: even if we *can* usefully adopt the intentional stance toward computational systems, the question remains whether we *should* organize our institutions, policies, and design practices around that attribution. CED-AH's argument is not merely metaphysical but normative — even if "the system decided" is a useful shorthand, it is a dangerous shorthand if it leads to abdicating human responsibility. Dennett himself, in later work, has expressed concerns about anthropomorphizing AI systems. CED-AH can accept Dennett's deflationary metaphysics while arguing that the intentional stance is pragmatically hazardous when applied to AI governance.

**Provides necessary context** for understanding why the AI discourse is confused. Dennett's framework explains *why* people so readily attribute agency to computational systems: the intentional stance is our most powerful predictive tool for complex systems, and we default to it. CED-AH's Category Error diagnosis is compatible with Dennett's psychology even if it rejects his metaphysics — people commit the category error because the intentional stance is cognitively natural, not because computational systems are actually agents.

## Limitations

- **Dennett's deflationism is itself contested.** Many philosophers (Searle, Chalmers, Block, Nagel) argue that Dennett's account eliminates rather than explains consciousness and intentionality. If original intentionality is a genuine phenomenon that Dennett's framework fails to capture, CED-AH's ontological distinction may stand. The debate is unresolved.
- **The intentional stance may explain the *practice* without validating the *attribution*.** Even Dennett's framework acknowledges that the intentional stance is a *strategy* — it works because systems are *as if* they had beliefs. CED-AH can accept this while arguing that the "as if" matters enormously for policy and design: treating a system *as if* it is an agent, when it is not, has different consequences than treating an agent as an agent.
- **Dennett's later views are more nuanced.** In subsequent work (especially *From Bacteria to Bach and Back*, 2017), Dennett has been more cautious about attributing "real" understanding to AI systems. His views have evolved in ways that may be more compatible with CED-AH than the 1987 work alone suggests.
- **Predictive success is not the only criterion for belief attribution.** Critics argue that predictive success is necessary but not sufficient — we also need to know something about the causal mechanisms underlying behavior. CED-AH's insistence on phenomenological experience as a criterion for agency is a version of this objection.
- **The framework does not distinguish between kinds of systems.** Dennett treats thermostats, chess computers, and humans as differing in degree rather than kind. CED-AH's entire project rests on the claim that the difference is one of kind (instrument vs. agent), not degree. This is the core disagreement, and neither side has a knock-down argument.
