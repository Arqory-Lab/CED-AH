## Summary

Murray Shanahan's "Talking About Large Language Models" is one of the most philosophically careful analyses of how we should — and should not — talk about what LLMs do. Shanahan, a professor of cognitive robotics at Imperial College London with deep expertise in both AI and philosophy of mind, argues that our habitual language for describing LLMs systematically misleads us about their nature.

The core argument is about linguistic practice. When we say an LLM "knows," "believes," "understands," "thinks," or "wants," we are using terms whose meaning is grounded in the human case — where they refer to states involving consciousness, intentionality, phenomenal experience, and embodied engagement with the world. Applying these terms to LLMs imports those connotations, leading us to attribute properties the systems do not possess. Shanahan does not argue that LLMs are "merely" statistical pattern matchers (a simplification he considers unhelpful) but that the question of what they are is genuinely difficult and that our existing conceptual vocabulary is poorly suited to describing them.

Shanahan proposes a disciplined linguistic practice: we should describe what LLMs do operationally (they generate text that is statistically likely given their training data and the prompt) without importing mentalistic vocabulary. When we must use mentalistic terms for convenience, we should do so with explicit scare quotes or qualifications to mark the metaphorical character of the attribution. He calls this "role-play" language: the LLM is not a knower but something playing the role of a knower in a conversational script derived from its training data.

Shanahan develops the "role-play" metaphor in detail. An LLM, he argues, does not have a single character or set of beliefs; it has absorbed the statistical patterns of millions of human authors and can simulate any of them. When it produces text that seems to express a belief, it is occupying a role — much as an actor delivers lines. The actor does not believe what the character believes; the LLM does not know what the text it generates asserts. But the performance can be so convincing that observers forget the distinction between performer and character.

The paper also addresses practical consequences: if we systematically misattribute understanding to LLMs, we will make poor decisions about their deployment, trust their outputs inappropriately, and fail to maintain the human oversight that their instrumental nature requires.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Category Error argument with a contemporary, technically informed, philosophically sophisticated analysis. Shanahan's diagnosis — that our language systematically misleads us about LLMs — is a precise articulation of the category error CED-AH identifies. When CED-AH warns that treating instruments as agents "leads to systematic policy failures," Shanahan provides the mechanism: mentalistic language imports unwarranted assumptions about consciousness, understanding, and agency, distorting our assessment of what systems can and should do.

**Directly supports** the instrument thesis as applied to LLMs specifically. Shanahan's "role-play" analysis provides a positive account of what LLMs do that is entirely consistent with their being instruments: they simulate conversational roles without occupying them. They produce text that plays the part of understanding without being understanding. This is CED-AH's instrument thesis in contemporary philosophical dress.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 3 (Transparency). Shanahan's call for disciplined linguistic practice — describing LLMs operationally rather than mentalistally — is a concrete implementation of transparency. If systems are described honestly (as role-playing statistical models rather than as knowing agents), users can make better-informed decisions about when and how to rely on them. This connects the philosophical analysis to CED-AH's practical design framework.

**Provides necessary context** by bridging the historical philosophical debate (Searle, Dreyfus, Nagel, Dennett) to the contemporary LLM landscape. Shanahan is aware of and engages with the classical arguments, updating them for a generation of systems the original thinkers could not have anticipated.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Shanahan combines deep philosophical sophistication with technical expertise in AI — a rare combination that gives his analysis exceptional authority. The paper addresses the specific system type (LLMs) most relevant to CED-AH's contemporary claims, provides a clear and actionable conceptual framework (the role-play metaphor), and connects philosophical analysis to practical consequences (how we talk about systems affects how we deploy them). The paper has been widely cited and discussed since its circulation, reflecting its perceived importance in the field.

## Limitations

- **The "role-play" metaphor has limits.** While illuminating, the metaphor of LLMs as actors playing roles may understate certain features of modern systems. Actors choose roles; LLMs do not. Actors can break character; LLMs have no character to break. The metaphor may obscure as much as it reveals about the nature of text generation.
- **Shanahan does not definitively settle the question of LLM understanding.** He argues for linguistic discipline — not importing mentalistic vocabulary — but acknowledges that the question of what LLMs "really" do is genuinely difficult. His operational descriptions are a pragmatic solution, not a metaphysical resolution. CED-AH's instrument thesis requires a stronger claim than Shanahan's linguistic modesty provides.
- **The paper may overstate the role of language in the problem.** CED-AH's Category Error argument goes beyond linguistic confusion to ontological claims: it is not merely that we *talk* about LLMs as if they were agents, but that our institutional practices, policies, and design decisions treat them as agents. Shanahan's linguistic analysis addresses only part of this problem.
- **Scale and emergence.** Shanahan's analysis was developed in the context of GPT-3 and early GPT-4. As models grow larger and exhibit emergent capabilities, the question of whether the role-play metaphor remains adequate grows more pressing. Future systems may demand conceptual resources Shanahan's framework does not provide.
- **No engagement with the ethical dimension.** Shanahan's paper is descriptive and diagnostic. It does not address the normative questions central to CED-AH: how should systems be designed? What design imperatives follow from the analysis? CED-AH's design framework goes beyond what Shanahan offers.
