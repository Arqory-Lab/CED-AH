## Summary

Hubert Dreyfus' *What Computers Can't Do* is the earliest sustained philosophical critique of artificial intelligence as a research program. Published when the AI field was still dominated by optimistic predictions about imminent human-level machine intelligence, Dreyfus argued that the fundamental assumptions of symbolic AI — what John Haugeland would later call "Good Old-Fashioned AI" (GOFAI) — were philosophically incoherent.

Dreyfus identifies ten assumptions underlying the AI research program, grounded in what he calls the "information processing" model of human cognition: that all knowledge can be formalized in rules, that human understanding proceeds by rule-following, that context can be captured by a sufficiently large set of explicit representations, and that intelligence is essentially problem-solving in a formal system. Drawing heavily on Heidegger's phenomenology and Merleau-Ponty's philosophy of embodiment, Dreyfus argues that human intelligence is fundamentally different from computation.

The core of Dreyfus' critique is the claim that human understanding is embodied, situated, and holistic in ways that resist formalization. When an expert chess player "sees" a strong move, they are not searching through a rule-based decision tree; they are perceiving a pattern shaped by years of embodied engagement with chess situations. When a native speaker understands a sentence, they are not parsing it according to formal grammatical rules; they are drawing on a background of shared practices, bodily experience, and cultural embeddedness that cannot be exhaustively specified. Dreyfus calls this the "background" or "know-how" — practical, pre-reflective understanding that is the precondition for all explicit knowledge.

Dreyfus argues that this background understanding cannot, in principle, be captured in a formal representation because it is not a set of facts or rules but a way of being in the world. Any attempt to formalize it faces an infinite regress: each rule requires context for its application, and that context requires further rules, ad infinitum. The "frame problem" in AI — the difficulty of specifying which features of a situation are relevant to a given task — is, for Dreyfus, not a technical challenge to be overcome but a symptom of a fundamental philosophical error: treating human understanding as computation.

The book was fiercely controversial when published. The AI establishment dismissed Dreyfus as a philosophical gadfly who did not understand the technical work. His predictions that symbolic AI would fail to achieve its stated goals were vindicated by the "AI winters" of the 1970s and 1980s.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that current AI discourse is built on philosophically inadequate foundations (Part 2). Dreyfus' critique of GOFAI anticipated CED-AH's argument that the dominant AI frameworks fail because they rest on unexamined assumptions about the nature of intelligence. CED-AH's claim that the field "proceeds without philosophical foundation" is a direct descendant of Dreyfus' original intervention: the assumptions have changed (from symbolic processing to neural networks to large language models), but the pattern of philosophical naivety persists.

**Supports by analogy** the instrument thesis. Dreyfus' argument that human understanding is embodied, situated, and holistic — and therefore fundamentally different from computation — provides philosophical resources for CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments, not agents. If genuine understanding requires embodied engagement with a world (as Dreyfus argues, following Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty), then disembodied computational systems cannot genuinely understand, regardless of their functional sophistication. They can simulate the outputs of understanding without possessing the phenomenon.

**Provides necessary context** for understanding the intellectual history CED-AH participates in. CED-AH is not the first philosophical framework to challenge AI's self-understanding; it inherits and extends a tradition that includes Dreyfus, Searle (phil-030, phil-031), and Weizenbaum. Understanding this lineage is important for positioning CED-AH accurately.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Dreyfus' critique was vindicated in its predictions about symbolic AI, and his philosophical arguments about embodiment and the background remain influential. However, the book was written about a form of AI (symbolic rule-based systems) that no longer dominates the field. The central question — whether Dreyfus' arguments extend to modern connectionist systems, deep learning, and large language models — requires additional argumentation that the 1972 text does not provide. This is addressed in the companion source, phil-026.

## Limitations

- **The target has moved.** Dreyfus' critique is directed at symbolic AI — rule-based, explicit-representation systems. Contemporary AI (deep learning, transformers, LLMs) does not rely on explicit rules or formalized knowledge in the way GOFAI did. Whether Dreyfus' arguments apply to systems that learn statistical patterns from data is a genuinely open question. CED-AH must bridge this gap carefully.
- **The phenomenological framework is contested.** Dreyfus' reliance on Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty ties the argument to a specific philosophical tradition (continental phenomenology) that not all readers will accept. Analytic philosophers of mind may find the arguments about "being-in-the-world" and "embodied know-how" insufficiently precise.
- **Dreyfus' predictions about AI capability were partly wrong.** While symbolic AI did fail to achieve human-level intelligence, AI systems (particularly since the deep learning revolution) have achieved capabilities Dreyfus did not anticipate. The fact that modern systems achieve impressive performance without the embodied understanding Dreyfus considers necessary raises questions about whether embodiment is truly required for the relevant cognitive functions.
- **The critique may prove too much.** If Dreyfus is right that all understanding requires embodied engagement, this applies not only to computational systems but to any attempt to formalize knowledge — including CED-AH's own formal architecture (T1, S1–S6, B1–B4). CED-AH should consider whether its formalism is vulnerable to a Dreyfusian critique.
- **No engagement with ethical or design questions.** Dreyfus' critique is about what computers *can't* do, not about what they *should* do. The normative questions at the heart of CED-AH — how should computational systems be designed? — are not addressed.
