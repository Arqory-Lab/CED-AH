## Summary

*What Computers Still Can't Do* is Dreyfus' updated and expanded critique, published twenty years after the original and in the wake of two developments: the collapse of symbolic AI (which vindicated many of his 1972 predictions) and the rise of connectionism (neural networks), which represented a fundamentally different approach to AI that could not be dismissed on the same grounds as GOFAI.

The most important addition is Dreyfus' engagement with connectionism. He acknowledges that neural networks avoid many of the problems he identified in symbolic AI: they do not rely on explicit rules, they learn from data rather than from programmed knowledge, and they exhibit a form of pattern recognition that is closer to the holistic, background-sensitive understanding he attributed to human cognition. Dreyfus concedes that connectionism represents a genuine advance and is more philosophically promising than GOFAI.

However, Dreyfus argues that connectionism still falls short of genuine human understanding for several reasons. First, neural networks lack bodies: human understanding is shaped by embodied interaction with a physical and social world, and neural networks process disembodied data. Second, neural networks lack what Dreyfus (following Heidegger) calls "care" or "concern" — they have no stakes in the world, no needs, no vulnerability. Human understanding is motivated by practical engagement; computational pattern recognition is not. Third, neural networks lack developmental history: human understanding emerges through a lifetime of situated experience, while networks are trained on static datasets. Dreyfus argues these are not merely engineering limitations to be overcome but reflect a fundamental difference between computational processing and human understanding.

The new introduction provides a sweeping intellectual history, tracing the dream of formalizing all knowledge from Socrates through Leibniz through GOFAI, arguing that each version of the dream fails for the same reason: the background of human understanding resists formalization.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments, not agents (the instrument thesis), with specific attention to modern connectionist and learning-based systems. While the 1972 critique targeted symbolic AI, the 1992 update addresses the more plausible claim that neural networks might possess something closer to genuine understanding. Dreyfus' concession that connectionism is an advance — combined with his argument that it still falls fundamentally short — maps directly onto CED-AH's position: modern AI systems are more capable than their predecessors, but the capability is instrumental, not agentive.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's three-part Agency Test. Dreyfus' arguments about embodiment (phenomenological experience requires a body), care (self-generated purpose requires stakes in the world), and developmental history (understanding requires ontological trajectory) correspond remarkably closely to CED-AH's three agency criteria: phenomenological experience, self-generated purpose, and ontological independence. Dreyfus provides the phenomenological grounding for criteria that CED-AH states but does not fully philosophically develop.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's argument in a productive way. Dreyfus' acknowledgment that connectionism is genuinely different from symbolic AI — and more promising — introduces a nuance CED-AH should incorporate. The instrument thesis should not be stated as a timeless truth about all possible computational systems but as a claim about the current and foreseeable relationship between computational architectures and the requirements for genuine agency. Dreyfus models this kind of careful, historically situated argumentation.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** The 1992 edition is philosophically more sophisticated than the original because it engages with the strongest contemporary alternative (connectionism) rather than only the weakest target (symbolic AI). Dreyfus' willingness to concede connectionism's advances while maintaining his core critique demonstrates philosophical rigor. The arguments about embodiment, care, and developmental history are well-grounded in the phenomenological tradition and have proven remarkably durable. The vindication of Dreyfus' earlier predictions about symbolic AI lends credibility to his subsequent analysis.

## Limitations

- **The target has moved again.** Published in 1992, this critique predates the deep learning revolution (2012+), the transformer architecture (2017), and large language models (2020+). Whether Dreyfus' arguments about connectionism apply to systems that are orders of magnitude more complex, trained on vastly larger datasets, and capable of emergent behaviors he could not have anticipated is a genuinely open question.
- **Embodiment may not be necessary.** Dreyfus' insistence on embodiment as a prerequisite for understanding is philosophically contentious. Functionalists argue that what matters is the functional organization, not the substrate. If a disembodied system could reproduce the relevant functional relationships, embodiment per se would be irrelevant. CED-AH should not rely solely on the embodiment argument.
- **"Care" and "concern" are philosophically loaded.** These Heideggerian concepts are not universally accepted in philosophy of mind. Analytic philosophers may find them too vague or anthropocentric to ground a rigorous criterion for agency. CED-AH should operationalize these concepts more precisely if it relies on them.
- **Scale may change the picture.** Dreyfus could not have foreseen systems trained on trillions of tokens of human-generated text. While this does not constitute embodied experience, it may constitute a form of cultural embeddedness that partially addresses Dreyfus' concerns about disembodiment. The question is open.
- **Dreyfus provides no constructive alternative.** His work is primarily critical. He tells us what computers can't do but offers limited guidance on how they *should* be designed or deployed. CED-AH's design imperatives go beyond anything Dreyfus provides.
