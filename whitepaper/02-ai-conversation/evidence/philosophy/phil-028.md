## Summary

John Haugeland's *Artificial Intelligence: The Very Idea* provides the definitive philosophical anatomy of classical AI — what Haugeland memorably christened "Good Old-Fashioned AI" or GOFAI. The book asks: what exactly is the claim that computers can think, and what would it take for this claim to be true?

Haugeland argues that GOFAI rests on a specific philosophical thesis he calls the "GOFAI hypothesis": (1) thinking is reasoning, (2) reasoning is computation, and (3) computation is formal symbol manipulation according to rules. If all three claims hold, then a machine that manipulates symbols according to the right rules — regardless of what those symbols are physically made of — would genuinely think.

Haugeland examines each premise with philosophical care. He grants that formal systems are powerful and that the GOFAI hypothesis is not obviously false. But he identifies deep problems. The first is the "frame problem" (related to Dreyfus' critique, phil-025): formal systems can only reason about what has been explicitly represented, but real-world reasoning requires knowing what to ignore — an open-ended capacity that resists formalization. The second is the problem of meaning or "intentionality": formal symbols are meaningless in themselves; they acquire meaning only through interpretation by an external agent. A computer manipulating the symbols for "cat" and "mat" has no idea what cats or mats are — the symbols could be anything. Meaning, Haugeland argues, is not intrinsic to formal systems but is projected onto them by human interpreters.

Haugeland introduces the distinction between "original" and "derived" intentionality: human thoughts have original intentionality (they are intrinsically about things), while the "intentionality" of a computer program is derived (it means something only because humans designed it to). This distinction — later contested by Dennett (phil-023) but defended by Searle (phil-030) — is central to the philosophical debate about AI.

The book concludes that GOFAI's "very idea" is philosophically coherent but probably empirically false: thinking is probably not formal symbol manipulation. Haugeland speculates that genuine artificial intelligence might require very different architectures — possibly ones grounded in embodiment and real-world engagement.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's historical analysis of the AI conversation (Part 2). Haugeland's anatomy of GOFAI explains *why* the AI field made the promises it made and *why* those promises were philosophically grounded in specific (and contestable) assumptions. CED-AH's claim that AI discourse "proceeds without philosophical foundation" is refined by Haugeland: the discourse had a philosophical foundation (the GOFAI hypothesis), but that foundation was inadequate. Understanding why it failed illuminates why current frameworks — which rest on different but equally unexamined assumptions — may also fail.

**Directly supports** the instrument thesis through the original/derived intentionality distinction. Haugeland's argument that formal systems have only derived intentionality — meaning projected onto them by human interpreters — is a precise formulation of CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments (their "intelligence" is attributed by humans) rather than agents (possessing intrinsic understanding). The distinction between original and derived intentionality maps directly onto the distinction between agents and instruments.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's formal architecture. Haugeland's careful examination of what formal systems can and cannot do provides a template for CED-AH's own formal structure: CED-AH's four-tier architecture (T1, S1–S6, B1–B4, Design Imperatives) is a formal system, and Haugeland's analysis helps clarify what such formalism can achieve (precision, testability, communicability) and what it cannot (the formalism does not replace philosophical judgment about the correctness of the axioms).

**Provides necessary context** for the broader evidence base. Haugeland's coinage "GOFAI" and his philosophical taxonomy of AI claims have become standard reference points. Understanding Haugeland is essential for understanding the subsequent debate, including Searle, Dennett, and Dreyfus.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Haugeland's work is philosophically rigorous and has become a standard reference in philosophy of AI. The original/derived intentionality distinction directly supports CED-AH's instrument thesis. The rating is Moderate rather than Strong because, like Dreyfus' original critique, the primary target is GOFAI rather than modern AI architectures. The question of whether Haugeland's arguments extend to systems that learn from data (rather than executing programmed rules) requires additional argumentation. However, the derived intentionality point may have broader application: even systems that learn from data acquire their "intentionality" from the human-generated data they are trained on, which is a form of derivation.

## Limitations

- **GOFAI is no longer the primary paradigm.** Modern AI is dominated by deep learning, not formal symbol manipulation. Haugeland's analysis of GOFAI's specific failures does not automatically extend to systems that do not rely on explicit rules and representations. The bridge from GOFAI critique to LLM critique must be built, not assumed.
- **The original/derived intentionality distinction is contested.** Dennett (phil-023) argues this distinction is incoherent — that all intentionality is "derived" in the relevant sense, including human intentionality (which is "derived" from evolutionary processes). If Dennett is right, the distinction cannot ground CED-AH's instrument thesis. CED-AH must engage with this challenge.
- **Haugeland's framework is primarily diagnostic, not prescriptive.** He explains why GOFAI fails but does not propose design principles for systems that would succeed or that would serve human flourishing. CED-AH's design imperatives go beyond anything Haugeland provides.
- **Published before connectionism's resurgence.** The 1985 publication predates the significant advances in neural networks. Haugeland's brief speculations about alternative architectures are not developed enough to assess whether modern systems address his concerns.
- **Introductory genre.** Written partly as an accessible introduction to the field, the philosophical arguments are sometimes developed at less depth than in more specialized works (e.g., Searle, Dreyfus). CED-AH should draw on Haugeland for conceptual clarity and terminology but rely on deeper sources for philosophical argumentation.
