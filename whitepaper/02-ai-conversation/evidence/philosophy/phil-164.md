## Summary

The *Philosophical Investigations* is Ludwig Wittgenstein's (1889–1951) second masterwork — a radical departure from his earlier *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921) and one of the most influential philosophical texts of the 20th century. Where the *Tractatus* proposed a picture theory of meaning (language means by picturing facts), the *Investigations* dismantles that theory and replaces it with a practice-based account: meaning is not a relationship between words and objects but a function of how words are used within "language games" — structured activities embedded in "forms of life." For CED-AH, the *Investigations* provides the most rigorous philosophical framework for understanding what LLMs do and do not do with language, and for defending the instrument thesis against claims that language-producing systems are language-understanding agents.

**Sections 1–64: Language Games.** Wittgenstein's central concept is the "language game" (Sprachspiel) — a structured activity in which language is used for a specific purpose within a specific context. Naming objects, giving orders, describing scenes, reporting events, telling jokes, making promises, asking questions — these are all different language games, each with its own rules, purposes, and criteria for success. There is no single essence of language, no one thing that all language does. Language is a family of related activities, connected by "family resemblances" rather than by a common essence.

Crucially, language games are embedded in what Wittgenstein calls "forms of life" (Lebensformen) — the broader patterns of human activity, culture, institution, and embodied existence within which language games have their place. Language does not exist in a vacuum; it exists within practices, and those practices are part of human life. "To imagine a language means to imagine a form of life" (PI, section 19). Meaning is not an abstract relation between a sign and what it signifies; meaning is a function of use within a form of life. "For a large class of cases — though not for all — in which we employ the word 'meaning' it can be defined thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language" (PI, section 43).

This analysis has devastating implications for the claim that LLMs "understand" language or "mean" anything by what they produce. An LLM generates statistically probable sequences of tokens based on patterns in training data. It does not participate in a form of life. It does not use language for purposes — it does not name, promise, joke, report, or ask. It produces strings that are syntactically well-formed and semantically plausible (to human readers) but that are not moves in a language game because no language game is being played. The LLM's output has the *form* of meaningful language without the *substance* of meaningful language — because substance, for Wittgenstein, consists in use within a form of life, and the LLM inhabits no form of life.

**Sections 143–242: Rule Following.** Wittgenstein's rule-following discussion addresses the question of what it means to follow a rule — to continue a series, apply a formula, or use a word correctly. The paradox (developed most famously in sections 198–202) is that no rule can determine its own application: any finite set of examples is compatible with infinitely many rules, so no amount of training data can uniquely specify the rule that generated it. Following a rule is not a matter of mechanically computing the next step from an algorithm; it is a practice — something that members of a community do, and do correctly or incorrectly, within the context of shared customs, training, and reactive responses.

This paradox applies directly to LLMs. An LLM processes training data and generates outputs that appear to follow rules (grammatical rules, logical rules, factual rules). But the rule-following paradox suggests that what the LLM does may not be rule-following in Wittgenstein's sense at all. Rule-following requires a normative context — a community of practitioners who can recognize correct and incorrect applications, who can correct mistakes, and who share a form of life within which the rule has its sense. The LLM has no such normative context; it has statistical patterns. Whether statistical pattern-matching constitutes rule-following — or merely mimics it — is precisely the question that Wittgenstein's analysis raises and that CED-AH's instrument thesis answers in the negative.

**Sections 243–315: The Private Language Argument.** Wittgenstein argues that a "private language" — a language that only one person could in principle understand, based on private sensations accessible to no one else — is impossible. The argument is that language requires criteria for correct use, and criteria require a public practice: "An inner process stands in need of outward criteria" (PI, section 580). Without public criteria — without the possibility of being checked by others — there is no distinction between following a rule correctly and merely thinking one is following it. Language is inherently public, social, and normatively constrained.

The private language argument bears on the question of whether LLMs have "inner experience" or "understanding." If they do, that experience or understanding would be radically private — accessible to no observer, verifiable by no public criteria. But Wittgenstein's argument is that claims about inner states are meaningful only to the extent that they are connected to public, observable criteria. If we cannot in principle verify whether an LLM "understands" — if there are no public criteria that would distinguish genuine understanding from sophisticated simulation — then the attribution of understanding to the LLM is, on Wittgenstein's account, not merely unverified but meaningless. The question "does the LLM really understand?" may not be a genuine question at all but a conceptual confusion — a case of applying a concept (understanding) outside the form of life within which it has its sense.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's analysis of what LLMs actually do (Part 2) by providing the philosophical framework for the claim that language production without participation in a form of life is not meaningful language use. Wittgenstein's language-game analysis shows that meaning is not a property of strings of symbols but a function of use within practices embedded in forms of life. LLMs produce strings of symbols that are statistically shaped by patterns in human language use, but they do not *use* language — they do not participate in the practices, purposes, and normative contexts that constitute meaning. CED-AH's claim that LLMs are instruments, not agents, receives its strongest philosophical support from Wittgenstein: an entity that does not participate in a form of life cannot mean anything by the symbols it produces, however fluent those symbols appear.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's instrument thesis (Part 4) through the rule-following paradox. If rule-following requires a normative community — shared practices, correction, agreement in judgments — then computational pattern-matching, however sophisticated, is not rule-following in the relevant sense. An LLM's outputs may coincide with rule-governed behavior, but coincidence is not compliance. The LLM does not follow grammatical rules; it produces outputs that are statistically likely to be grammatical. This distinction — between genuine rule-following (a normative achievement) and statistical pattern-matching (a computational process) — supports CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments operating according to algorithms, not agents acting according to understood rules.

**Directly supports** the claim that attributing understanding to LLMs is a conceptual confusion, not an empirical question. The private language argument establishes that understanding, meaning, and experience are public, normatively constrained concepts. They apply to entities that participate in forms of life — entities whose behavior can be evaluated against public criteria within shared practices. Attributing these concepts to LLMs is not a bold empirical hypothesis; it is, on Wittgenstein's analysis, a misapplication of concepts — like asking whether the number 5 is heavier than the number 7. CED-AH can draw on Wittgenstein to argue that the "does AI understand?" debate is in large part a conceptual confusion rather than an empirical question awaiting resolution.

**Provides the philosophical bridge** to contemporary philosophy of AI. Wittgenstein's framework is the explicit starting point for several important contemporary analyses of LLMs, most notably Shanahan, M. (2024), "Talking About Large Language Models" (*Communications of the ACM*), which argues that we systematically misunderstand LLMs because we apply concepts (understanding, knowing, believing) that presuppose forms of life that LLMs do not inhabit. CED-AH can cite Wittgenstein as the historical source for the contemporary philosophical arguments that support its instrument thesis.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Philosophical Investigations* is one of the most influential philosophical works of the 20th century, and its analysis of language games, rule following, and the private language argument are directly relevant to the central questions of CED-AH's framework: what LLMs do, whether they understand, and whether they can be agents. The connection between Wittgenstein's arguments and contemporary AI philosophy (especially via Shanahan) is already established in the scholarly literature. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) Wittgenstein's arguments are notoriously subject to multiple interpretations — the reading offered here (broadly aligned with Hacker's interpretation) is defensible but not universally accepted; (2) functionalist philosophers of mind (e.g., Dennett) argue that Wittgenstein's criteria can be met by sufficiently complex computational systems — making the application to LLMs contested; (3) the *Investigations* was written decades before digital computation, and its application to AI requires interpretive work that goes beyond Wittgenstein's text; and (4) the private language argument's relevance to AI consciousness is itself a matter of scholarly debate.

## Limitations

- **Interpretive pluralism.** Wittgenstein's text is famously open to multiple interpretations. The "resolute" reading (Diamond, Conant) treats the *Investigations* as primarily therapeutic — dissolving philosophical problems rather than solving them. The "metaphysical" reading (Hacker) treats it as establishing substantive philosophical conclusions about language and mind. The CED-AH application depends on a substantive reading; if the therapeutic reading is correct, Wittgenstein's arguments may dissolve the question of LLM understanding rather than answering it.
- **Functionalist objections.** Functionalist philosophers (Dennett, 1991; Churchland, 1995) argue that Wittgenstein's criteria for understanding, meaning, and rule-following can in principle be satisfied by computational systems — that what matters is functional organization, not substrate. If a system behaves as if it follows rules, participates in practices, and responds to correction, then it *does* follow rules, on a functionalist account. CED-AH's Wittgensteinian position must engage with this objection.
- **The "form of life" concept is vague.** Wittgenstein never precisely defines "form of life," and scholars disagree about what it means and what it requires. Whether an LLM embedded in human social practices (answering questions, generating text used by humans, participating in workflows) thereby participates in a form of life — at least derivatively — is an open question that the text does not settle.
- **Pre-digital context.** Wittgenstein died in 1951, before digital computers were widespread. He could not have anticipated LLMs. His arguments about language, rules, and meaning were developed in the context of human linguistic practice, not computational text generation. All applications to AI are extensions that must be independently argued.
- **The "Chinese Room" overlap.** Wittgenstein's arguments overlap significantly with Searle's Chinese Room argument (phil-021). The two are not identical — Searle's is a thought experiment about syntactic manipulation vs. semantic understanding; Wittgenstein's is an analysis of meaning as use in practice — but the overlap means that many of the objections to Searle (systems reply, robot reply, other minds reply) may also challenge the Wittgensteinian position.
- **Wittgenstein himself might resist this application.** Wittgenstein was deeply suspicious of philosophical theorizing — the *Investigations* is in large part a critique of the impulse to construct philosophical theories. Using his work to construct a theoretical position about AI may violate the spirit of his philosophy, even if individual arguments support it.
