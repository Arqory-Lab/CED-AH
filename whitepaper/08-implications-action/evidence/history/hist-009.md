## Summary

Marchetti and Maassarani provide a comprehensive survey of the contemporary global AI governance landscape — the patchwork of national strategies, regional regulations, multilateral initiatives, and multi-stakeholder frameworks that collectively constitute the emerging governance regime for artificial intelligence. Writing in 2023, they capture a critical moment: the European Union's AI Act is moving toward adoption, the United States is pursuing a sector-specific regulatory approach, China has enacted AI-specific regulations, and international organizations (OECD, UNESCO, G7) are developing AI governance principles — but no coherent global framework exists.

The article identifies several structural challenges in global AI governance:

**Fragmentation.** Different jurisdictions are adopting different approaches — risk-based (EU), innovation-friendly (US), state-controlled (China), capacity-building (Global South) — with no mechanism for harmonization. This creates regulatory arbitrage opportunities, compliance complexity for multinational developers, and governance gaps for cross-border AI systems.

**Speed mismatch.** AI capability advances far faster than governance frameworks can evolve. By the time regulations are drafted, debated, and enacted, the technology they address may have been superseded. This creates a permanent governance lag that favors industry self-regulation — which has a poor track record in other technology domains.

**Definitional ambiguity.** There is no global consensus on what "AI" means, what "safety" requires, or what "ethical AI" entails. Different frameworks use different definitions, making comparative analysis and mutual recognition difficult.

**Power asymmetry.** AI development is concentrated in a small number of countries and corporations. Governance frameworks risk reflecting the priorities of the powerful (innovation, competition, market access) while marginalizing the concerns of the affected (displacement, surveillance, cultural erosion).

Marchetti and Maassarani argue for a multi-layered governance approach combining binding regulation (for high-risk applications), voluntary standards (for lower-risk applications), multi-stakeholder dialogue (for emerging applications), and capacity-building (for developing nations).

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's governance claims. CED-AH proposes a philosophical framework and design standard for AI systems, but it operates within a governance landscape that is fragmented, lagging, and contested. Marchetti and Maassarani's survey shows why CED-AH's approach — grounding governance in a universal philosophical principle (coherence) rather than in jurisdiction-specific regulations — is potentially valuable: it provides a basis for governance that transcends jurisdictional fragmentation.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that current governance frameworks are inadequate. The fragmentation, speed mismatch, and definitional ambiguity that Marchetti and Maassarani document are, in CED-AH's terms, symptoms of a deeper problem: the absence of a coherent first principle. Without philosophical grounding, governance frameworks are reactive (responding to harms after they occur), superficial (addressing symptoms rather than structural causes), and incommensurable (different jurisdictions cannot agree because they lack shared criteria for evaluation).

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's open-source governance model. The multi-stakeholder approach that Marchetti and Maassarani advocate is structurally similar to CED-AH's open-source model: a framework developed through transparent processes, subject to challenge and revision, incorporating diverse perspectives while maintaining coherence. CED-AH can position itself as a contribution to the multi-stakeholder governance ecosystem — providing the philosophical substrate that specific regulatory frameworks can implement.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's universalist aspirations. The governance landscape Marchetti and Maassarani describe is shaped by geopolitical competition, power asymmetries, and divergent values. CED-AH's claim that coherence is a universal first principle must contend with the reality that different governance actors have different priorities, and "coherence" may mean different things in different cultural and political contexts.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The article provides a useful and timely survey of the global AI governance landscape, published in a reputable international affairs journal. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) governance surveys are inherently time-bound — the landscape described in 2023 has already evolved significantly; (b) the article is descriptive rather than analytically deep — it maps the terrain without providing substantial theoretical analysis; (c) it does not engage with the philosophical questions CED-AH raises about the foundations of governance. Its value is as context, not as a primary analytical source.

---

## Limitations

- **Rapidly dating.** AI governance is evolving faster than publication cycles can capture. The EU AI Act has been adopted since this article was written; new national strategies have emerged; and the governance landscape continues to shift.
- **Survey breadth limits depth.** The article covers many governance initiatives but cannot analyze any of them in depth. CED-AH requires deeper analysis of specific governance approaches and their compatibility with coherence-based principles.
- **Does not address philosophical foundations.** Marchetti and Maassarani describe governance frameworks without questioning their philosophical underpinnings. CED-AH's contribution — grounding governance in coherence — addresses a gap this article does not itself identify.
- **Western and institutional focus.** Despite acknowledging the Global South, the article's analysis centers on EU, US, and multilateral institutional governance. Community-based, indigenous, and non-Western governance traditions are underrepresented.
- **The multi-layered governance proposal is general.** The article's prescriptive conclusions (binding regulation + voluntary standards + multi-stakeholder dialogue + capacity-building) are reasonable but not specific enough to evaluate against CED-AH's framework.
