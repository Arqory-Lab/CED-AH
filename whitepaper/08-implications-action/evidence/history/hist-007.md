## Summary

Sheila Jasanoff's *The Ethics of Invention* is the most important recent work on the governance of emerging technologies from the perspective of Science and Technology Studies (STS). Jasanoff, a professor of Science and Technology Studies at Harvard's Kennedy School, argues that technological innovation is not a neutral process of discovery but a profoundly social, political, and ethical activity that shapes — and is shaped by — the societies in which it occurs.

Jasanoff's central concept is the "sociotechnical imaginary" — the collectively held, institutionally stabilized visions of desirable futures that are attainable through advances in science and technology. These imaginaries shape which technologies are developed, how they are regulated, who benefits from them, and whose risks they externalize. Different societies hold different sociotechnical imaginaries: the United States emphasizes individual innovation and market disruption; European nations emphasize precaution and social welfare; developing nations emphasize capacity-building and sovereignty.

The book's most relevant argument for CED-AH is Jasanoff's critique of what she calls "technologies of hubris" — approaches to risk assessment and governance that systematically overestimate our ability to predict and control technological outcomes while underestimating uncertainty, ignorance, and the potential for surprise. Jasanoff contrasts these with "technologies of humility" — approaches that acknowledge the limits of prediction, engage diverse perspectives, create mechanisms for learning and adaptation, and distribute the burdens and benefits of innovation equitably.

Jasanoff argues that effective technology governance requires three things that current frameworks often lack: (1) **anticipation** — the capacity to foresee not just intended effects but unintended consequences and systemic interactions; (2) **participation** — the inclusion of affected communities in decisions about technologies that shape their lives; and (3) **reflexivity** — the willingness to question assumptions, acknowledge uncertainty, and revise course when evidence warrants it.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's governance framework (Part 8). Jasanoff's "technologies of humility" map remarkably well onto CED-AH's design imperatives: anticipation corresponds to systematic failure mode analysis; participation corresponds to Authority Retention (those affected by decisions should participate in making them); reflexivity corresponds to Calibrated Honesty and the ongoing measurement of coherence impact. CED-AH can position itself as a specific instantiation of Jasanoff's general governance philosophy, applied to the AI domain.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's critique of the current AI safety discourse. Jasanoff's "technologies of hubris" describe the dominant approach to AI governance: risk assessments that focus on quantifiable harms while ignoring systemic coherence erosion, governance frameworks that assume technical experts can predict and control AI's social effects, and innovation narratives that treat displacement and disruption as acceptable costs of progress. CED-AH's coherence-first approach is, in Jasanoff's terms, a technology of humility — it acknowledges the limits of prediction and foregrounds the preservation of human agency and capability.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's claims about the social embeddedness of technology. Jasanoff's STS framework demonstrates that technologies are not deployed into a neutral social context — they reshape the social contexts they enter. This supports CED-AH's argument that the amplification/replacement distinction is not merely a technical design choice but a social and political decision with consequences for human agency, capability, and flourishing.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's open-source governance model. Jasanoff's emphasis on participation and reflexivity supports the CED-AH project's structure: an open framework where claims can be challenged, evidence can be contested, and diverse perspectives can shape the framework's development. The BDFL governance model with lazy consensus is a specific mechanism for implementing Jasanoff's participatory ideal while maintaining coherence and direction.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Jasanoff is the founder of the field of sociotechnical imaginaries and one of the most influential scholars in Science and Technology Studies. *The Ethics of Invention* synthesizes decades of research on technology governance across multiple domains (biotechnology, nuclear power, information technology, climate engineering). The book is published by a major academic press, draws on extensive comparative governance research, and has shaped policy discourse internationally. For CED-AH's governance claims, this is the strongest available source from the STS tradition.

## Limitations

- **STS perspective may be difficult to operationalize.** Jasanoff's framework is analytically powerful but does not provide specific regulatory mechanisms or design standards. CED-AH must translate her principles (anticipation, participation, reflexivity) into concrete governance and design requirements — which the five design imperatives and Coherence Dashboard attempt to do.
- **Does not address AI specifically in depth.** While the book's principles apply to AI, Jasanoff's primary examples come from biotechnology, environmental regulation, and nuclear power. The specific governance challenges of AI systems (speed of development, global deployment, opacity of neural networks) require additional analysis beyond what Jasanoff provides.
- **The "sociotechnical imaginary" concept is descriptive, not prescriptive.** Jasanoff shows that different societies hold different visions of technological futures, but does not provide a normative framework for choosing between them. CED-AH provides this normative framework through the coherence criterion — but must argue for its universality rather than assuming it.
- **May be perceived as anti-innovation.** Jasanoff's emphasis on humility, precaution, and participation can be read as hostile to technological innovation. CED-AH should frame its use of Jasanoff carefully: the goal is not to slow innovation but to ensure innovation serves human flourishing — which is itself a form of ambition.
- **Academic genre may limit accessibility.** Jasanoff writes for an academic audience in STS and political science. CED-AH must translate her insights for the broader audiences (engineers, entrepreneurs, policymakers) it seeks to reach.
