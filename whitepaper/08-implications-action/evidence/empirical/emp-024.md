## Summary

Andrew Tutt's "An FDA for Algorithms" is the most influential academic proposal for a regulatory framework specifically designed for algorithmic decision-making systems. Writing in 2017 — before the current AI governance explosion — Tutt argues that algorithms used in consequential decisions (healthcare, criminal justice, finance, employment) should be subject to a regulatory regime modeled on the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's approach to pharmaceutical regulation.

Tutt's argument proceeds in three stages:

**The problem.** Algorithms are increasingly making or substantially influencing decisions with life-altering consequences: who gets a loan, who gets released on bail, which patients receive treatment, which job applicants are interviewed. These algorithms are largely unregulated — they are proprietary, opaque, and not subject to systematic testing for safety, efficacy, or fairness before deployment. The existing regulatory framework (anti-discrimination law, consumer protection, tort liability) was designed for human decision-makers and does not adequately address algorithmic decision-making.

**The analogy.** The pharmaceutical industry faced a similar governance challenge in the early twentieth century: powerful, widely deployed interventions (drugs) with the potential for both great benefit and great harm, developed by private companies with incentives to overstate efficacy and understate risk. The regulatory response — the FDA — required pre-market testing, demonstrated safety and efficacy, ongoing monitoring, and mandatory reporting of adverse events. Tutt argues that algorithms present structurally similar challenges and warrant a structurally similar response.

**The proposal.** Tutt proposes an "FDA for algorithms" with four key functions: (1) **Pre-deployment testing** — algorithms used in consequential decisions must demonstrate safety and efficacy (including fairness) before deployment; (2) **Transparency requirements** — developers must disclose the logic, training data, and known limitations of their systems; (3) **Post-deployment monitoring** — ongoing surveillance for adverse outcomes, bias drift, and unintended consequences; (4) **Adverse event reporting** — mandatory reporting of algorithmic harms, analogous to the FDA's adverse event reporting system for drugs.

Tutt acknowledges significant challenges in implementing this proposal — including the difficulty of defining "safety" and "efficacy" for algorithms, the trade secrets concerns of developers, and the speed of algorithmic development relative to regulatory processes — but argues that these challenges are tractable and that the alternative (continued unregulated deployment) is untenable.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that regulatory frameworks for AI are needed and feasible. Tutt's proposal demonstrates that algorithmic regulation can be structured — it need not be ad hoc or reactive. CED-AH's design imperatives could serve as the substantive criteria that Tutt's regulatory framework enforces: an "FDA for algorithms" needs to know what "safe" and "effective" mean for algorithmic systems, and CED-AH's coherence criterion provides precisely this definition.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 3 (Transparency) through regulatory mechanism. Tutt's transparency requirements — disclosure of logic, training data, and known limitations — are the regulatory implementation of CED-AH's Transparency imperative. Tutt provides the legal and institutional mechanisms; CED-AH provides the philosophical justification and the specific criteria for what transparency requires.

**Provides a potential implementation pathway** for CED-AH's compliance framework. CED-AH's roadmap includes "formal CED-AH compliance criteria and certification framework" as an exploring-phase goal. Tutt's FDA analogy suggests a concrete institutional model: pre-deployment certification against CED-AH criteria, ongoing coherence monitoring (the Coherence Dashboard), and mandatory reporting of coherence failures. This is not the only possible implementation, but it is the most well-developed regulatory proposal in the literature.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's framework by identifying practical regulatory challenges. Tutt's discussion of implementation challenges — trade secrets vs. transparency, speed of development vs. regulatory process, definitional ambiguity in "safety" and "efficacy" — identifies obstacles that CED-AH's compliance framework must address. These are not reasons to abandon regulation but design constraints that shape how regulation should work.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The article is published in a respected administrative law review and has been widely cited in the algorithmic governance literature. The FDA analogy is illuminating and has shaped subsequent regulatory proposals. However, the rating is Moderate rather than Strong because (a) the proposal is theoretical — no "FDA for algorithms" has been implemented, so its feasibility remains untested; (b) the pharmaceutical analogy has significant limitations (algorithms are not drugs; the FDA model has its own well-documented problems); (c) the article was written before the current AI era and does not address LLMs, foundation models, or the specific challenges of generative AI; and (d) the regulatory proposal may be too U.S.-centric for CED-AH's global aspirations.

---

## Limitations

- **The FDA analogy breaks down in important ways.** Drugs are discrete products with measurable effects; algorithms are continuously evolving, context-dependent, and interact with their environments. Pre-deployment testing is meaningful for drugs but may be less meaningful for algorithms that change behavior based on the data they encounter in deployment.
- **Trade secrets tension.** Tutt acknowledges but does not fully resolve the tension between transparency requirements and proprietary interests. CED-AH's open framework resolves this tension for the framework itself (it is open by design) but does not address how proprietary systems can be evaluated against CED-AH criteria without disclosing trade secrets.
- **Regulatory capture risk.** Any regulatory body for algorithms risks being captured by the industry it regulates — a well-documented problem with the FDA itself. CED-AH's open-source, community-governed model may be more resistant to capture than a centralized regulatory agency.
- **U.S.-centric.** Tutt's proposal is grounded in U.S. administrative law and institutional design. CED-AH's global framework needs governance models that work across jurisdictions — which Ostrom's polycentric governance (hist-010) may address better than Tutt's centralized model.
- **Published pre-LLM.** The specific regulatory challenges of foundation models, generative AI, and multi-purpose AI systems are not addressed. An "FDA for algorithms" designed in 2017 would need significant updates to address 2026 AI systems.
