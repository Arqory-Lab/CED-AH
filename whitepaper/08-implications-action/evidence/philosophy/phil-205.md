## Source

**Access:** Available via academic libraries; Springer. DOI: 10.1007/s13347-022-00571-x

---

## Summary

Taddeo and Blanchard propose the **"moral gambit"**: human agents who design, develop, or deploy autonomous systems accept responsibility for outcomes *ex ante*, acknowledging that they cannot predict all consequences but choosing to bear accountability anyway. The gambit resolves the predictability objection — the claim that no one can be responsible because no one could have predicted the specific outcome. The moral gambit says: you accept responsibility *because you chose to deploy the system*, not because you predicted its specific actions. The deployer's culpability derives from the deployment decision itself, not from foreknowledge of every possible output.

The authors develop this argument in the context of autonomous weapons systems, where the stakes are lethal and the unpredictability objection is strongest. If the moral gambit holds for weapons — where the consequences of unpredictable behavior include death — it holds a fortiori for less-lethal AI deployment contexts.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's accountability principle at its most philosophically challenging point. The strongest objection to the accountability principle is: "How can a deploying entity be accountable for outcomes it couldn't predict?" Taddeo and Blanchard's answer — the moral gambit — is operationally identical to CED-AH's position: the deployment decision itself creates the accountability, regardless of whether specific outcomes were foreseeable. You chose to deploy; you bear the consequences.

**Resolves the predictability objection** that Matthias (2004) identified as the core barrier. Matthias showed that traditional responsibility requires prediction and control; learning systems break this requirement. Taddeo and Blanchard show that responsibility can be grounded in the deployment decision rather than in output prediction — a different basis for accountability that survives the learning-system challenge.

**Applies directly to AI companies:** they chose to train on copyrighted data, chose to deploy systems that generate content at scale, and chose to release those systems for public use. The moral gambit says they accepted accountability for consequences when they made those deployment decisions, whether or not they predicted specific harms.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in a leading philosophy of technology journal, this paper addresses the hardest philosophical challenge to deployment-based accountability. The moral gambit concept is original, rigorously argued, and directly applicable to CED-AH's accountability principle. The autonomous weapons context — the most extreme deployment scenario — provides the strongest possible test case.

---

## Limitations

- **Military context.** The argument is developed for autonomous weapons, where deployment decisions involve institutional deliberation, legal review, and rules of engagement. Consumer AI deployment decisions are often less deliberate. The moral gambit may need adaptation for casual deployment contexts (e.g., an individual deploying an open-source model).
- **Voluntariness assumption.** The gambit assumes the deployer *voluntarily* accepts the risk. In competitive markets, companies may feel compelled to deploy AI systems to remain viable. If deployment is economically coerced, the voluntariness of the gambit is questionable.
- **No design standard provided.** Like Goetze, Taddeo and Blanchard establish WHO bears responsibility and WHY, but not WHAT they are responsible FOR. CED-AH's five Design Imperatives complete the picture.
