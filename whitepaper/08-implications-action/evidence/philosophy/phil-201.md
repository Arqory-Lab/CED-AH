## Source

**Citation:**
Thompson, D. F. (1980). Moral responsibility of public officials: The problem of many hands. *American Political Science Review*, 74(4), 905–916.

**Access:** Available via academic libraries; Cambridge University Press / JSTOR.

---

## Summary

Dennis Thompson's seminal analysis identifies how organizational complexity defeats moral responsibility by distributing causal contribution across so many actors that no individual bears sufficient causal weight to be blamed. Originally applied to government bureaucracies, Thompson showed that when outcomes result from the combined decisions of many officials — each making individually reasonable choices within their role — moral responsibility "falls through the cracks." No one is to blame because no one did anything individually wrong, yet the collective outcome is harmful.

Thompson examines and rejects two common responses: (1) **hierarchical responsibility** (blame the person at the top) fails because top officials often lack specific knowledge of the contributing decisions; (2) **collective responsibility** (blame the organization) fails because it diffuses accountability so broadly that it attaches to no one in particular. His positive proposal is that we must develop more nuanced criteria for assigning individual responsibility within organizational contexts, including retrospective (who contributed to the outcome?) and prospective (who had a duty to prevent it?) dimensions.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's insistence that the **deploying entity** must be identifiable. Thompson's analysis demonstrates what happens when no designated entity bears accountability: responsibility falls through the cracks. CED-AH's accountability principle responds to the many-hands problem by designating a specific accountability target — the entity that makes the deployment decision — rather than attempting to distribute responsibility across the entire causal chain.

**Explains the structural dynamics** that CED-AH's accountability principle must overcome. The AI deployment pipeline is a many-hands problem: model developers, data curators, fine-tuners, integrators, deployers, and end users all contribute to outcomes, and each can point to others as more causally responsible. Thompson's analysis shows that this is not a failure of will but a failure of structure — and therefore requires a structural solution.

**Supports by analogy** the argument that organizational accountability is the minimum viable response. Thompson's rejection of collective responsibility (too diffuse) and hierarchical responsibility (too detached) suggests that accountability must be assigned at the level where the deployment decision is made — precisely CED-AH's position.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in the flagship journal of American political science, Thompson's paper has become a foundational reference across political philosophy, organizational ethics, and computing ethics. Nissenbaum (1996) explicitly adapted Thompson's many-hands analysis for computing contexts. The argument is analytically rigorous and has been refined but not refuted over four decades.

---

## Limitations

- **Government bureaucracy focus.** Thompson's original analysis concerns public officials, not private technology companies. The extension to AI deployment chains is natural but not established by Thompson.
- **No computational context.** Written before personal computers, let alone AI. The specific dynamics of AI development pipelines (stochastic models, emergent behavior, scale) are not addressed.
- **Does not resolve the problem.** Thompson identifies the many-hands problem but does not fully solve it. His criteria for individual responsibility within organizations are suggestive but not algorithmic. CED-AH's "deploying entity" designation is more operationally specific.
