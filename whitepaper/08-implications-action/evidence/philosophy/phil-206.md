## Summary

Robert Sparrow argues that no one can bear moral responsibility for the actions of genuinely autonomous weapons systems. He systematically eliminates each candidate: (1) **the programmer** cannot be responsible because the system's behavior emerges from learning, not from code; (2) **the commanding officer** cannot be responsible because they cannot predict or control the system's specific actions; (3) **the machine itself** cannot be responsible because it lacks the moral agency required for responsibility (it cannot suffer punishment, feel guilt, or understand moral reasons). Since none of these candidates can bear responsibility, deploying autonomous weapons systems creates an irresolvable accountability gap — making their deployment morally impermissible.

Sparrow's argument is important precisely because it reaches a conclusion CED-AH must either accommodate or refute: that for sufficiently autonomous systems, the responsibility gap may be genuinely unsolvable.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Adversarial source — the strongest published challenge to the accountability principle.** If Sparrow is right that responsibility cannot be assigned for sufficiently autonomous system behavior, then CED-AH's Commitment 2 has a hard limit: it applies only to systems that are not "genuinely autonomous" in Sparrow's sense.

**However, CED-AH's instrument thesis (Commitment 1) neutralizes Sparrow's challenge.** Sparrow's argument depends on the premise that autonomous weapons systems are genuinely autonomous — i.e., that they are agents rather than instruments. If the instrument thesis holds — if computational systems are instruments by nature, not agents — then Sparrow's third candidate (the machine itself) is correctly eliminated not because autonomy creates an impossible gap but because instruments cannot bear moral responsibility as a matter of classification. And his first two candidates (programmer, commanding officer) are subsumed by CED-AH's "deploying entity" — the entity that chose to put the instrument into consequential operation.

**This is the interlock argument at its sharpest.** Sparrow's challenge is real if Commitment 1 fails (if the system IS an autonomous agent). It dissolves if Commitment 1 holds (if the system IS an instrument). The accountability principle needs the instrument thesis; the interlock is not optional.

**CED-AH should acknowledge Sparrow's challenge explicitly** in its self-critique (Paper 3's falsification conditions): if a computational system achieves genuine autonomous agency — meeting the criteria for moral agency that Sparrow specifies (capacity for suffering, guilt, moral understanding) — then the accountability principle may be insufficient, because the system would bear its own moral responsibility and the deploying entity's vicarious liability would need reconceptualization.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong (Adversarial)

**Justification:** Published in a leading applied philosophy journal, Sparrow's argument is analytically rigorous and has been widely cited in the autonomous weapons and AI ethics literature. It represents the most forceful philosophical argument that the responsibility gap is genuinely unsolvable for sufficiently autonomous systems. Its value to CED-AH is precisely as a challenge: it defines the conditions under which the Two Commitments framework would fail.

---

## Limitations

- **Assumes genuine autonomy.** Sparrow's argument depends on the premise that autonomous weapons systems are genuinely autonomous agents. CED-AH's instrument thesis challenges this premise — current computational systems are not autonomous in Sparrow's sense (they do not possess moral agency, cannot suffer, cannot understand moral reasons). If no current or foreseeable system meets Sparrow's criteria, the challenge is hypothetical.
- **Military-specific.** The argument concerns lethal autonomous weapons, where the moral stakes are extreme. It is not clear that the same analysis applies to non-lethal AI systems (content recommendation, language generation, decision support).
- **Does not engage with deployment-decision accountability.** Sparrow eliminates programmer and commanding officer as individual candidates but does not consider organizational-level accountability or the moral gambit (accepting responsibility ex ante for unpredictable outcomes).
