## Summary

Luciano Floridi's *The Ethics of Information* is the most comprehensive philosophical treatment of ethics in the information age. Floridi develops "Information Ethics" (IE) as a macroethics — a framework that extends moral consideration to all informational entities (not just sentient beings) and evaluates actions based on their impact on the "infosphere" (the totality of informational entities and their interactions).

Floridi's central innovation is the concept of "levels of abstraction" (LoA). Any system can be described at multiple levels of abstraction, and the properties that are observable depend on which level is chosen. At a high level of abstraction, a thermostat can be described as "wanting" to maintain temperature; at a lower level, it is a mechanical device responding to physical inputs. Floridi argues that ethical analysis must be explicit about the level of abstraction at which it operates, because moral properties (agency, responsibility, harm) are level-dependent.

IE proposes four fundamental principles: (1) entropy (destruction or corruption of informational entities) is bad; (2) entropy ought to be prevented; (3) entropy ought to be removed if it occurs; and (4) the flourishing of informational entities ought to be promoted. These principles are explicitly inspired by bioethics but extended to the informational domain.

Floridi develops a sophisticated account of informational privacy as a constitutive dimension of personal identity — we are, in part, our informational patterns, and violations of privacy are therefore violations of personal integrity. He also addresses the ethics of artificial agents, building on earlier work with Sanders (phil-034), arguing that at appropriate levels of abstraction, artificial systems can be considered moral agents.

The book is notable for its rigor, its willingness to challenge conventional ethical categories, and its systematic engagement with the philosophical implications of the information revolution.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's concern with the ethical stakes of computational systems. Floridi's Information Ethics shares CED-AH's conviction that the design and deployment of computational systems raise fundamental ethical questions, not merely technical ones. Both frameworks insist that technology is not ethically neutral and that a philosophical foundation is needed for evaluating its impact.

**Provides necessary context** for understanding the intellectual landscape CED-AH operates in. Floridi is the most prominent philosopher working on the ethics of information and AI. CED-AH must position itself relative to IE, clarifying where it agrees (technology raises deep ethical questions), where it diverges (on the nature of computational agency), and where it extends (CED-AH's specific design imperatives and measurement framework).

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position in important ways. Floridi's "levels of abstraction" framework introduces a challenge: CED-AH's instrument thesis claims that computational systems are instruments at *every* level of abstraction, but Floridi argues that agency and moral status are level-dependent. At a sufficiently high LoA, a computational system might legitimately be described as an agent — not because it "really" is one, but because at that level of description, agency is an observable property. CED-AH's claim that the instrument/agent distinction is ontological (not merely descriptive) is in tension with Floridi's methodological pluralism about levels of abstraction.

**Complicates** CED-AH's entropy/coherence argument. Floridi's IE uses "entropy" as the fundamental ethical disvalue (destruction or corruption of information). CED-AH uses coherence (the struggle against entropy) as the fundamental value. These may be compatible — both oppose entropy — but they frame the ethical stakes differently. Floridi focuses on the infosphere (all informational entities); CED-AH focuses on human flourishing specifically. Floridi's broader scope could be seen as either complementary to or in tension with CED-AH's anthropocentrism.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Floridi's *Ethics of Information* is the most important single work in information ethics and is directly relevant to CED-AH's domain. The "levels of abstraction" framework poses a genuine philosophical challenge to CED-AH's ontological claims about instrument vs. agent status. The rating is Moderate rather than Strong because the connection is primarily contextual and qualificatory — IE does not directly address CED-AH's specific claims about coherence, design imperatives, or measurement. The challenge from the LoA framework is real but can be navigated.

## Limitations

- **IE's scope is much broader than CED-AH's.** Floridi's framework extends moral consideration to all informational entities, including non-sentient objects. CED-AH is specifically about human flourishing in relation to computational systems. The broader scope makes IE relevant but not directly applicable without significant narrowing.
- **"Levels of abstraction" may dissolve the question CED-AH asks.** If agency is level-dependent, then asking "is this system *really* an agent?" may be malformed — the answer depends on the level of description. CED-AH's ontological claim (computational systems are *really* instruments, not agents) presupposes that there is a level-independent answer. If Floridi is right, CED-AH must either argue for a privileged level of description or reformulate its claims as level-relative.
- **The entropy framework is controversial.** Extending moral consideration to all informational entities (including non-sentient objects) has been criticized as implausible. If a database is an informational entity deserving moral consideration, ethical analysis becomes unmanageably broad. CED-AH's focus on human flourishing avoids this problem.
- **Floridi's framework does not provide CED-AH's practical tools.** IE offers general ethical principles but not the specific design imperatives, failure mode analysis, or measurement framework that CED-AH provides. The practical gap between abstract ethics and implementable design guidance is significant.
- **Tension with Floridi's own GPT-3 analysis (phil-027).** In the GPT-3 paper, Floridi describes LLMs as "agentless." In IE (and phil-034), he argues artificial agents can have moral status at appropriate levels of abstraction. This internal tension in Floridi's work must be navigated carefully when citing him.
