## Summary

Trystan Goetze reviews and criticizes four proposed solutions to the responsibility gap: (1) **legal liability frameworks** — assigning legal accountability through strict liability or negligence standards; (2) **new moral theories** — developing ethical frameworks that do not require predictability as a precondition for responsibility; (3) **blaming the systems themselves** — treating AI systems as moral agents that bear their own responsibility; and (4) **professional ethical standards** — holding computing professionals to codes of conduct. Goetze finds each inadequate as a *conceptual* solution, though each may have practical value.

His positive proposal: **vicarious moral responsibility** — computing professionals bear responsibility for their systems' actions analogously to how parents bear responsibility for their children's actions. The parent did not perform the child's action, may not have predicted it, and cannot fully control it — but chose to bring the child into the world and has a duty of care. Similarly, the developer/deployer chose to bring the system into consequential operation and has a duty of care for its effects.

Goetze insists that this keeps moral responsibility "firmly in the human realm." The system is not a moral agent; the human who deployed it is.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's insistence that accountability must rest with human agents or legally recognized organizations, never with the computational system itself. Goetze's critique of "blaming the systems" is the most rigorous philosophical argument against treating AI systems as accountability targets — precisely the move CED-AH's instrument thesis prevents.

**Supports the interlock argument.** Goetze's vicarious responsibility model works because it presupposes that the system is NOT a moral agent. This presupposition is exactly the instrument thesis (Commitment 1). Without it, the vicarious responsibility analogy collapses — if the system IS an agent, it bears its own responsibility and the developer/deployer is not vicariously liable. The interlock: Commitment 1 (instrument classification) enables Commitment 2 (human accountability) by establishing that the system cannot bear accountability for itself.

**Provides the philosophical mechanism** for CED-AH's "deploying entity" concept. Goetze's vicarious responsibility model is operationally equivalent to CED-AH's accountability principle: the entity that chose to deploy the system bears accountability for its effects, not because they predicted or controlled every output, but because they made the deployment decision.

**Justification:** Published at FAccT, the premier venue for fairness and accountability research, Goetze's paper is a rigorous philosophical contribution that directly addresses the core challenge CED-AH's accountability principle must solve. The vicarious responsibility analogy is philosophically productive and avoids the conceptual errors of alternative approaches. The paper has been well-received in the AI ethics community.

---

## Limitations

- **The parent analogy has limits.** Parents have a biological and emotional relationship with children that developers do not have with systems. The analogy may overstate the duty of care — or understate it, since parents' liability for children's actions varies significantly across jurisdictions.
- **Does not address organizational accountability.** Goetze's analysis focuses on individual computing professionals. CED-AH extends accountability to organizations (the deploying entity can be a corporation). The vicarious responsibility model needs adaptation for organizational contexts.
- **Does not provide a design standard.** Goetze establishes WHO bears responsibility but not WHAT they are responsible FOR. CED-AH's five Design Imperatives fill this gap — they define the standard against which the vicariously responsible entity is assessed.
