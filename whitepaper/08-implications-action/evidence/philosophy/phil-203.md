## Source

**Citation:**
Santoni de Sio, F., & Mecacci, G. (2021). Four responsibility gaps with artificial intelligence: Why they matter and how to address them. *Philosophy & Technology*, 34(4), 1057–1084.

**Access:** Available via academic libraries; Springer. DOI: 10.1007/s13347-021-00450-x

---

## Summary

Santoni de Sio and Mecacci extend the responsibility gap literature by identifying not one but **four distinct gaps**: (1) the **culpability gap** — no one is blameworthy for the outcome because no one acted with the requisite mens rea (intent, recklessness, negligence); (2) the **moral responsibility gap** — no one bears moral responsibility because the causal chain from human decision to system output is too attenuated; (3) the **accountability gap** — no one answers for outcomes because institutional structures lack accountability mechanisms for AI-mediated actions; and (4) the **active responsibility gap** — no one has a prospective duty to prevent harm because the obligation has not been assigned.

The authors argue that these four gaps are analytically distinct: a system can close one gap while leaving others open. For example, a regulatory framework might close the accountability gap (someone must answer) while leaving the culpability gap open (the person who answers is not blameworthy in the traditional sense). They propose that different gaps require different solutions — legal, organizational, philosophical, and technical — and that treating "the responsibility gap" as a single problem leads to inadequate solutions.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** the comprehensiveness of CED-AH's accountability principle. CED-AH's Two Commitments framework, properly implemented, addresses all four gaps:

- **Culpability gap:** Closed by the deployment decision — the entity that chose to deploy the system with knowledge of its general capabilities is culpable for outcomes within the foreseeable range.
- **Moral responsibility gap:** Closed by the instrument thesis — if systems are instruments (Commitment 1), then moral responsibility cannot rest with the instrument and must rest with the deployer.
- **Accountability gap:** Closed directly by the accountability principle (Commitment 2) — the deploying entity must answer for outcomes.
- **Active responsibility gap:** Closed by the five Design Imperatives — the deploying entity has a prospective duty to ensure the system meets the imperatives, assessed by the measurement framework.

**Supports the compound failure analysis.** The four-gap taxonomy allows CED-AH's failure cases to be classified with precision. Social media's failure involved all four gaps simultaneously (compound failure). AI copyright infringement involves primarily the culpability and accountability gaps. AI-generated likenesses involve primarily the accountability and active responsibility gaps.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's approach. If Santoni de Sio and Mecacci are right that different gaps require different solutions, CED-AH must demonstrate that its unified framework (Two Commitments + five imperatives) addresses all four adequately. A single principle cannot close gaps that require fundamentally different remedies.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in a leading philosophy of technology journal, this paper provides the most refined taxonomy of responsibility gaps in the current literature. It extends and systematizes a research tradition running from Thompson (1980) through Nissenbaum (1996) and Matthias (2004). The four-gap taxonomy is analytically rigorous and practically useful for CED-AH's failure mode analysis.

---

## Limitations

- **Taxonomy may be incomplete.** Four gaps may not exhaust the ways responsibility fails in AI contexts. Emerging phenomena (deepfakes, autonomous weapons, AI-generated scientific fraud) may reveal additional gap types.
- **No unified solution proposed.** The authors argue that different gaps require different solutions but do not propose a unified framework. CED-AH's Two Commitments are an attempt at such unification; the question is whether unification is possible or whether the gaps are irreducibly heterogeneous.
- **European regulatory context.** The analysis assumes a European legal and philosophical framework. Its applicability to other jurisdictional and cultural contexts requires translation.
