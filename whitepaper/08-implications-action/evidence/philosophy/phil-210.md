## Summary

David Kaye, former UN Special Rapporteur on the promotion and protection of the right to freedom of opinion and expression, examines how platform companies assumed governance functions — deciding what billions of people can say, see, and share — without the accountability structures that traditionally accompany governance power. He argues that platforms have become the de facto regulators of global speech, exercising powers comparable to state censorship but without constitutional constraints, democratic accountability, or judicial review. His analysis spans multiple jurisdictions and demonstrates that the accountability gap is global, not merely American.

---

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Extends the accountability analysis beyond U.S. context.** While Citron & Wittes and Rozenshtein focus on Section 230, Kaye demonstrates that the accountability gap in platform governance is a global phenomenon — not a quirk of American law but a structural feature of how computational systems operate at civilizational scale without governance accountability.

**Supports CED-AH's jurisdiction-agnostic accountability principle.** Kaye's global analysis validates CED-AH's approach: the accountability principle is a philosophical commitment (every deploying entity bears accountability for its system's effects) rather than a legal claim tied to any specific jurisdiction. Different legal systems will implement it differently, but the underlying principle applies universally.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Written by a former UN Special Rapporteur with unique access to government, platform, and civil society perspectives, this is an authoritative treatment of the global accountability gap. Its value is primarily as evidence that the problem CED-AH addresses is not jurisdiction-specific but civilizational.

---

## Limitations

- **Journalistic treatment.** Written for a general audience, not as academic scholarship. Arguments are developed narratively rather than with formal rigor.
- **Focused on speech governance.** Kaye's analysis concerns content moderation and speech regulation specifically. CED-AH's accountability principle extends to all computational system effects (capability atrophy, dependency, agency erosion), not just speech.
