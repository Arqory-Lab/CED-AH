## Summary

Alvin Goldman's *Epistemology and Cognition* is the founding text of reliabilism — the view that a belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process (one that tends to produce true beliefs). Goldman's central innovation is to make epistemology empirically accountable: rather than evaluating beliefs solely by internal logical relations, we should evaluate the *processes* that produce them by whether those processes track truth reliably.

Goldman draws a crucial distinction between individual epistemics (how individual believers form justified beliefs) and social epistemics (how institutional and social structures promote or hinder justified belief). He argues that epistemic evaluation must attend to both levels. A perfectly rational individual embedded in a distorted information environment will form unjustified beliefs; a flawed individual reasoner in a well-designed epistemic environment may do better.

Goldman also develops the concept of "epistemic paternalism" — the question of when it is appropriate for institutions to constrain information flow for epistemic ends. He argues for a generally anti-paternalist position: epistemic autonomy is valuable in its own right, and reliable processes tend to be undermined when external authorities control what information is available.

The work bridges analytic epistemology and cognitive science, insisting that philosophical theories of knowledge must be consistent with what we know about actual human cognition. Normative epistemology must be psychologically realistic.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's formal architecture, particularly the bridging principles. Goldman's two-level analysis — individual cognition and social/institutional structures — parallels CED-AH's concern with both individual human-system interactions and systemic effects on human coherence. The CED-AH Coherence Dashboard, which measures understanding, capability, agency, and dependency across time, is structurally similar to Goldman's project of evaluating epistemic processes by their outcomes.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's commitment S2 (flourishing requires agency, capability, understanding, meaning). Goldman's anti-paternalist argument — that epistemic autonomy is intrinsically valuable and that reliable belief formation requires access to information without distortion — directly grounds CED-AH's Design Imperatives 1 (Authority Retention) and 3 (Transparency). If systems that conceal their reasoning or limit information access undermine the reliability of human belief-forming processes, they decrease epistemic coherence.

**Provides necessary context** for evaluating CED-AH's measurement framework. Goldman's insistence that epistemology must be empirically accountable — that we should measure the reliability of belief-forming processes, not just their logical properties — supports CED-AH's emphasis on measurable coherence outcomes rather than purely formal criteria.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's pure coherentism. Goldman's reliabilism is not a coherentist position — it evaluates beliefs by the reliability of their causal origins, not by their coherence with other beliefs. To the extent that CED-AH appeals to reliability (in its measurement framework), it draws on non-coherentist epistemological resources. This is not necessarily a contradiction, but CED-AH should be transparent about where its epistemological commitments are coherentist and where they are reliabilist.

## Limitations

- **Reliabilism is not coherentism.** Goldman's framework evaluates beliefs by the reliability of their causal processes, not by their coherence. CED-AH's appeal to Goldman requires navigating a genuine epistemological tension: either CED-AH is ecumenical about its epistemological foundations (drawing on both coherentism and reliabilism), or it must show how reliabilist insights can be accommodated within a coherentist framework.
- **Pre-digital context.** Published in 1986, *Epistemology and Cognition* addresses human cognitive processes and social institutions but not computational systems. The extension to human-AI interaction is reasonable but requires bridging that Goldman does not provide.
- **"Reliable process" is difficult to individuate.** A persistent criticism of reliabilism (the "generality problem") is that any belief is produced by many cognitive processes at different levels of description, and reliability varies across levels. This complicates the application to CED-AH's measurement framework, which must specify what processes are being measured.
- **Epistemic paternalism debate has evolved.** Goldman's anti-paternalist stance has been challenged by subsequent work on epistemic injustice (Fricker, 2007) and structured ignorance. The question of when computational systems should filter or structure information for users is more complex than Goldman's 1986 treatment suggests.
