## Summary

Herbert Simon's *The Sciences of the Artificial* is one of the most important books in the intellectual history of both AI and cognitive science. Simon — who won the Nobel Prize in Economics and the Turing Award — developed the concepts of "bounded rationality," "satisficing," and the "artificial" as designed system that have shaped multiple fields for over half a century.

Simon's central distinction is between the "natural" (phenomena governed by the laws of nature) and the "artificial" (objects and systems designed by human beings to serve human purposes). Artificial systems — including tools, organizations, institutions, and computational devices — are characterized by their dependency on human purpose. They exist because humans designed them; they function in ways humans determined; their value derives from their service to human goals.

"Bounded rationality" — Simon's most influential concept — describes how humans actually make decisions: not by optimizing (considering all alternatives and selecting the best) but by "satisficing" (searching until a satisfactory alternative is found). This is not irrationality but rational adaptation to cognitive limitations and environmental complexity. Human decision-making is bounded by attention, memory, and processing capacity — but these bounds are navigated with remarkable effectiveness through heuristics, domain knowledge, and experiential learning.

For CED-AH, the most important chapter is "The Architecture of Complexity," which argues that complex systems — both natural and artificial — are organized hierarchically, with stable intermediate structures (modules) that enable both complexity and adaptability. This has direct implications for how computational systems should be designed: as modular, transparent, human-comprehensible architectures rather than opaque, monolithic systems.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's instrument thesis (S3). Simon's distinction between natural and artificial systems is the intellectual foundation for CED-AH's claim that computational systems are human-dependent instruments. Simon's "artificial" is defined by its relationship to human purpose — designed by humans, for humans, dependent on human infrastructure and intention. CED-AH's axiom that computational systems are instruments follows directly from Simon's ontology of the artificial.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's coherence criterion through bounded rationality. Simon's bounded rationality demonstrates that human cognition is limited but adaptive — not optimal by formal standards, but effective in navigating real-world complexity. CED-AH's coherence criterion (internal consistency + flourishing alignment) is, in a sense, a formalization of satisficing: the goal is not optimal outcomes but coherent ones — outcomes that are internally consistent and aligned with human flourishing.

**Provides necessary context** for the ecological rationality tradition. Simon's bounded rationality is the intellectual ancestor of both Gigerenzer's ecological rationality (emp-015, emp-016) and Klein's naturalistic decision-making (emp-019). CED-AH draws on all three, and Simon provides the common foundation.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's design approach through the architecture of complexity. Simon's argument that complex systems should be organized hierarchically with modular, comprehensible intermediate structures is the design-theoretic foundation for CED-AH's Transparency imperative. Transparent systems are hierarchically organized systems whose intermediate structures are comprehensible to human users.

## Limitations

- **Published originally in 1969, revised 1996.** While the concepts remain relevant, the specific examples and technological context are dated. The extension to modern AI systems — particularly LLMs and neural networks — requires additional argumentation.
- **Simon was optimistic about AI.** Simon famously predicted (in 1957) that computers would beat humans at chess within ten years — an overestimate of timeline but not of eventual outcome. His optimism about AI capability complicates CED-AH's use of his work: Simon believed artificial systems could replicate human cognitive capabilities, which CED-AH disputes.
- **Bounded rationality does not address all of CED-AH's concerns.** Simon's framework addresses cognition and design but not the normative dimensions of human flourishing, meaning, and agency that CED-AH's coherence criterion encompasses.
- **"The artificial" category is very broad.** Simon's definition includes everything from hammers to organizations to computers. CED-AH's instrument thesis makes specific claims about computational systems that may not follow from the general category of "artificial."
- **The architecture of complexity may not apply to neural networks.** Simon's hierarchical, modular design ideal may be in tension with the structure of modern neural networks, which are neither hierarchical nor modular in Simon's sense.
