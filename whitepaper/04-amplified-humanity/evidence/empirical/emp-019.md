## Summary

Gary Klein's *Sources of Power* is the foundational text of the naturalistic decision-making (NDM) research tradition — the study of how experts actually make decisions in real-world, high-stakes, time-pressured environments. Where laboratory-based judgment research (Kahneman, emp-017) documents how humans deviate from formal rationality, Klein documents how experienced practitioners achieve remarkable performance under conditions where formal rationality is impossible.

Klein's central model is "recognition-primed decision-making" (RPD): experienced practitioners do not make decisions by generating and comparing alternatives (the classical rational model). Instead, they rapidly recognize the situation as an instance of a familiar pattern and implement the response associated with that pattern. If the initial recognition does not fit, they mentally simulate the response to check for problems and adjust. This is not "intuition" in the vague popular sense — it is structured pattern recognition built through extensive experience.

Klein documented RPD through field studies of firefighters, military commanders, ICU nurses, chess players, and other domain experts. His findings show that expert decision-making is: (1) fast — experts typically consider only one option rather than comparing multiple alternatives; (2) pattern-based — experts recognize situations rather than analyzing them from scratch; (3) experientially grounded — expertise requires extensive domain experience with feedback; (4) contextually sensitive — experts adapt their responses to specific situational features that novices do not perceive.

Klein explicitly challenges the assumption that expert judgment is merely "biased intuition" that should be replaced by algorithms. He argues that expert intuition is a genuine cognitive achievement — a form of intelligence that combines domain knowledge, pattern recognition, and contextual sensitivity in ways that formal algorithms cannot replicate.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that human expertise is a capability to be amplified, not a deficiency to be replaced. Klein's RPD model demonstrates that expert judgment is a structured, reliable, and genuinely intelligent form of cognition — not "gut feeling" or random guessing. This is the strongest empirical evidence for CED-AH's amplification thesis: experts possess capabilities that computational systems cannot replicate because those capabilities are grounded in experiential learning, contextual sensitivity, and embodied knowledge.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth). Klein's research shows that expert decision-making develops through extensive real-world experience with meaningful feedback. Systems that replace this experience (by making decisions for practitioners) prevent the development of recognition-primed expertise. Systems that amplify it (by providing information, supporting pattern recognition, offering simulations for mental rehearsal) support expertise development.

**Provides necessary context** for the Kahneman-Klein synthesis. Chapter 22 of Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (emp-017) describes the Kahneman-Klein joint framework: expert intuition is reliable in regular, learnable environments where the expert has had sufficient practice with feedback. Klein's work provides the evidence for the "expert intuition is reliable" side of this framework; Kahneman's provides the evidence for the "expert intuition can fail" side. CED-AH's position — amplify where reliable, support where unreliable, replace nowhere — is compatible with the joint framework.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's critique of the replacement paradigm. Klein's research demonstrates that the replacement paradigm misunderstands the nature of expert judgment. Replacing RPD with algorithmic decision-making does not "remove bias" — it removes pattern recognition, contextual sensitivity, and adaptive expertise. The resulting system may be more consistent but is also more brittle, less adaptive, and incapable of handling novel situations that fall outside its training data.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Klein is the founder of the naturalistic decision-making field, and *Sources of Power* has shaped how researchers understand expert cognition in real-world contexts. The RPD model has been validated across multiple domains (firefighting, military, medicine, aviation) and is one of the most robust findings in applied cognitive science. The relevance to CED-AH is direct and substantial: Klein provides the empirical evidence that expert judgment is a genuine capability worth preserving and amplifying.

---

## Limitations

- **The NDM tradition may overvalue expertise.** Klein studies experts performing well. He may underrepresent cases where expert intuition fails — which is Kahneman's concern (emp-017). CED-AH should draw on both traditions.
- **RPD describes existing expertise, not its development.** Klein documents how experts *use* their expertise but is less detailed about how expertise *develops*. Ericsson (emp-014) provides the complementary developmental account.
- **Field studies have ecological validity but limited control.** Klein's research is conducted in real-world settings, which provides ecological validity but makes causal inference difficult. Laboratory studies (like Endsley & Kiris, emp-005) provide better causal evidence.
- **RPD may not apply to all decision domains.** The model is strongest in domains with time pressure, incomplete information, and dynamic environments. In domains with ample time for deliberation (policy-making, strategic planning), the classical rational model may be more appropriate.
- **Does not address AI-specific challenges.** Klein's work predates the current AI era. How RPD interacts with AI-assisted decision-making is an open empirical question.
