## Summary

Gerd Gigerenzer's *Gut Feelings* presents a radical challenge to the dominant view of human judgment in cognitive science — the "heuristics and biases" tradition associated with Kahneman and Tversky (emp-017). Where Kahneman emphasizes the systematic errors humans make when deviating from formal rationality, Gigerenzer argues that the so-called "biases" are often features, not bugs — adaptive responses to the environments in which human cognition evolved.

Gigerenzer's central concept is "ecological rationality" — the idea that the rationality of a cognitive strategy cannot be evaluated in the abstract but only relative to the environment in which it operates. A heuristic (a simple decision rule) that appears "biased" in a laboratory experiment may be optimally adapted to the real-world environments humans actually navigate. The "less is more" effect — the finding that simple heuristics often outperform complex algorithms in real-world prediction — demonstrates that cognitive simplicity is an asset, not a liability, in uncertain and information-rich environments.

Key examples include: (1) the "recognition heuristic" (if you recognize one option but not another, choose the recognized one) outperforms complex statistical models in many prediction tasks; (2) the "1/N heuristic" (divide your investment equally among N options) outperforms sophisticated portfolio optimization in many financial contexts; (3) expert intuition in medicine, sports, and business often relies on fast-and-frugal heuristics that use less information more effectively than comprehensive analysis.

Gigerenzer argues that the "bias" framing leads directly to the replacement paradigm: if human judgment is systematically flawed, then replacing it with algorithms seems rational. But if human judgment is ecologically adapted, then the appropriate response is amplification — supporting and extending the adaptive capabilities humans already possess.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's position that human judgment is a capability to be amplified, not a deficiency to be replaced. Gigerenzer's ecological rationality framework provides the cognitive science foundation for CED-AH's most important claim: that the appropriate response to human cognitive limitations is not replacement but amplification. If human heuristics are adaptive rather than defective, then replacing them with algorithms is not correction but destruction of adapted capability.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's critique of the replacement paradigm's intellectual foundations. The replacement paradigm draws much of its justification from the Kahneman/Tversky "biases" literature: humans are biased, algorithms are not, therefore algorithms should replace human judgment. Gigerenzer's work undermines this reasoning by showing that (a) the "biases" are often adaptive, (b) algorithms are not bias-free but are adapted to different environments, and (c) the comparison between human and algorithmic performance depends critically on the environment.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by requiring nuance. CED-AH cannot simply assert that all human judgment is good. Gigerenzer himself acknowledges that heuristics can fail — ecological rationality means the strategy must match the environment. CED-AH's framework must specify which aspects of human judgment are adaptive (and should be amplified) and which are genuinely problematic (and should be supported or corrected, but not replaced).

**Provides necessary context** for the Kahneman-Gigerenzer debate (emp-017 vs. emp-015/emp-016). CED-AH does not need to resolve this debate — but it must engage with both positions. CED-AH's claim is compatible with both: even Kahneman's view (humans have systematic biases) supports amplification over replacement, because replacing biased human judgment with algorithmic judgment still leaves humans incapable of understanding, evaluating, or overriding the algorithm.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Gigerenzer is one of the most prominent cognitive scientists of the past three decades, and his ecological rationality research program is well-established and empirically supported. The rating is Moderate rather than Strong because *Gut Feelings* is a popular book rather than primary research, and because the Kahneman-Gigerenzer debate remains unresolved in cognitive science. The evidence supports CED-AH's position but does not settle the underlying scientific question.

---

## Limitations

- **Popular book, not primary research.** The underlying research is published in peer-reviewed venues (Gigerenzer et al. 1999, emp-016, is the more rigorous source). CED-AH should cite both.
- **The Kahneman-Gigerenzer debate is unresolved.** Cognitive scientists disagree about whether human heuristics are primarily adaptive or primarily biased. CED-AH should acknowledge this uncertainty rather than siding definitively with Gigerenzer.
- **Ecological rationality has limits.** Heuristics that were adaptive in ancestral environments may not be adaptive in modern technological environments. The environments in which humans now make decisions (financial markets, social media, AI-mediated information) may be sufficiently novel that evolved heuristics misfire.
- **"Less is more" does not always hold.** While Gigerenzer demonstrates that simple heuristics often outperform complex algorithms, this is not universally true. In some domains (weather prediction, certain medical diagnostics), complex models do outperform human judgment.
- **Does not address the specific question of AI-human interaction.** Gigerenzer's research addresses human judgment in general, not the specific question of how AI systems should relate to human judgment.
