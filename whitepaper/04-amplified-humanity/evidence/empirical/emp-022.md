## Summary

*Superforecasting* is the sequel to *Expert Political Judgment* (emp-021) — and where the earlier book was sobering, this one is cautiously optimistic. Tetlock and Gardner report the results of the Good Judgment Project (GJP), an IARPA-funded forecasting tournament that pitted thousands of volunteer forecasters against professional intelligence analysts with access to classified information.

The central finding: a small subset of volunteer forecasters — the "superforecasters" — consistently outperformed not only average forecasters but also professional intelligence analysts with classified data. And they did so using publicly available information alone.

What made superforecasters exceptional was not domain expertise, intelligence, or access to information. It was cognitive style and practice — precisely the characteristics of Tetlock's "foxes" from the earlier study, taken to a higher level:

(1) **Granular probability estimates.** Superforecasters made fine-grained probability assessments rather than vague predictions. (2) **Frequent updating.** They revised their forecasts frequently in response to new information. (3) **Diverse perspectives.** They actively sought out views that challenged their own. (4) **Self-critical humility.** They tracked their accuracy and learned from errors. (5) **Deliberate practice.** They treated forecasting as a skill to be improved, not a talent to be displayed.

The most important finding for CED-AH: superforecasting is trainable. Ordinary people, given appropriate training in probabilistic reasoning, bias awareness, and update discipline, significantly improved their forecasting accuracy. The combination of individual skill and team aggregation (superforecasters working in teams with structured discussion protocols) produced the best results of all.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification thesis with the strongest available evidence. The superforecasting results are a proof of concept for amplification: structured support (training, feedback, team protocols, probability calibration) dramatically improved human judgment in a domain where unstructured human judgment had previously failed. This is exactly what CED-AH's amplification paradigm predicts: human capability, when appropriately supported, can achieve performance levels that neither humans alone nor algorithms alone can match.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth). Superforecasting is the clearest empirical demonstration that human judgment can be improved through deliberate practice — even in domains (political forecasting) where Tetlock's earlier research (emp-021) showed that untrained experts perform poorly. CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative requires exactly this: systems that support the kind of structured practice that produces superforecasters.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Superforecasters' most distinctive trait is their probabilistic calibration — their stated confidence closely matches their actual accuracy. This is precisely what Calibrated Honesty requires of AI systems. The superforecasting evidence shows that calibration is achievable and that it produces dramatically better outcomes than overconfident judgment.

**Provides the empirical model** for what CED-AH-compliant AI systems should do. An AI system designed for amplification would do what the GJP did for its superforecasters: provide structured frameworks for analysis, facilitate access to diverse perspectives, track accuracy over time, provide calibration feedback, support team deliberation with structured protocols, and scaffold the updating process. The AI system would be a "superforecasting environment" — not replacing judgment but creating the conditions under which human judgment excels.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that the amplification/replacement choice has measurable consequences. Superforecasters (amplified human judgment) outperformed both unaided human judgment and simple algorithms. The evidence suggests that the optimal configuration is neither pure human nor pure algorithm but structured human-system collaboration — which is CED-AH's amplification paradigm.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Based on the IARPA-funded Good Judgment Project — a large-scale, multi-year forecasting tournament with rigorous methodology (thousands of participants, hundreds of questions, repeated measurements, professional intelligence analysts as benchmarks). The findings have been replicated in subsequent forecasting tournaments. The evidence that structured training and practice can dramatically improve human judgment is robust and directly relevant to CED-AH's amplification framework. This is the single most important positive evidence source for CED-AH's design approach.

## Limitations

- **Popular book, not primary research.** The GJP results are published in peer-reviewed venues (Mellers et al. 2014, 2015), which CED-AH should also cite.
- **Forecasting is a specific domain.** Whether the superforecasting approach generalizes to other judgment domains (medical diagnosis, engineering design, ethical reasoning) is an empirical question. The principles (calibration, updating, diverse perspectives) seem domain-general, but this has not been formally demonstrated.
- **Selection effects.** Superforecasters may have been unusually talented individuals who happened to volunteer. The extent to which their cognitive style can be trained in a general population is debated.
- **The GJP had specific structural features.** The tournament format, scoring rules, and team structures created specific incentives that may not exist in real-world decision-making. Transferring GJP methods to organizational contexts requires adaptation.
- **Does not address the role of AI.** The GJP used human teams with structured protocols, not AI-assisted decision-making. Whether AI systems can provide the same benefits as GJP's team structures and training is an open question — but the evidence suggests they could.
