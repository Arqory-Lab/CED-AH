## Summary

*Personal Knowledge* is Michael Polanyi's (1891–1976) major philosophical work — a sustained argument that all human knowledge has a personal, tacit dimension that cannot be fully articulated, formalized, or transferred to impersonal systems. Polanyi was a physical chemist of distinction before turning to philosophy; his scientific career gave him firsthand experience of the role that judgment, intuition, skill, and personal commitment play in scientific practice — dimensions of knowledge that the positivist philosophy of science of his era dismissed as subjective and therefore irrelevant. *Personal Knowledge* is his response: a philosophical demonstration that what positivism dismisses is actually constitutive of knowledge itself.

**Part One: The Art of Knowing.** Polanyi opens with the observation that knowing is a skilled performance — an art that cannot be reduced to the application of explicit rules. A scientist identifying a mineral under a microscope, a physician diagnosing a disease by physical examination, a chess master recognizing a promising position, a native speaker recognizing a grammatical sentence — all of these involve knowledge that the knower possesses but cannot fully state. The scientist "knows" the mineral but cannot articulate all the features that led to the identification. The physician "knows" the diagnosis but cannot reduce the reasoning to a decision tree. The chess master "knows" the position is good but cannot explain every consideration that entered the assessment. The native speaker "knows" the sentence is grammatical but cannot state the rules being followed.

Polanyi's central claim is captured in his most famous formulation: "we can know more than we can tell" (*The Tacit Dimension*, p. 4). This is not a claim about the limitations of language or the privacy of experience — it is an epistemological claim about the structure of knowledge itself. All knowledge, Polanyi argues, has two components: a "focal" component (what we attend to explicitly) and a "subsidiary" component (what we rely on without attending to it). When a pianist plays, the focal awareness is on the music; the subsidiary awareness includes the position of the fingers, the feel of the keys, the physical coordination of hands and arms, the acoustic feedback from the room. The subsidiary awareness is essential to the performance — without it, the pianist cannot play — but it cannot be made the object of focal attention without destroying the performance. A pianist who attends focally to finger positions loses the music; a cyclist who attends focally to balance falls.

This "from-to" structure of knowing — from subsidiary awareness to focal awareness — is Polanyi's fundamental insight. We know the world by relying on things we cannot fully articulate. The reliance is not a deficiency to be overcome but a structural feature of all knowledge. Even the most "explicit" knowledge — a mathematical proof, a scientific formula, a computer algorithm — relies on tacit knowledge for its use and interpretation. The mathematician must know how to apply the proof; the scientist must know how to interpret the formula in context; the programmer must know what the algorithm is for. This interpretive, contextual, skilled dimension of knowledge is tacit — it cannot be fully formalized because it is the condition for the meaningful use of any formalization.

**Part Two: The Tacit Component.** Polanyi develops the formal structure of tacit knowledge through the concept of "indwelling." We know the world by indwelling our bodies, our tools, our practices, our traditions. A blind man knows the sidewalk through his cane — the cane is not an object of knowledge but a medium of knowledge, an extension of the body through which the world is accessed. Similarly, a scientist knows nature through the instruments, techniques, theories, and traditions of the discipline — these are not objects of knowledge but media through which knowledge is achieved. To master a discipline is to indwell its practices — to internalize them so thoroughly that they become subsidiary rather than focal, means of knowing rather than objects of knowledge.

The concept of indwelling has direct implications for the human-machine relationship. When a user interacts with a computational system, the system can function in two ways: as a medium of indwelling (a tool that extends the user's capability, like the blind man's cane) or as a replacement for indwelling (a system that does the knowing on the user's behalf, leaving the user without the tacit knowledge that indwelling develops). CED-AH's amplification/replacement distinction maps directly onto this Polanyian framework: amplification-oriented systems support indwelling — they become extensions of the user's capability, developing tacit knowledge through use. Replacement-oriented systems prevent indwelling — they interpose themselves between the user and the domain, so the user develops tacit knowledge of the system's interface rather than tacit knowledge of the domain itself.

**Part Three: The Justification of Personal Knowledge.** Polanyi argues that the personal, tacit dimension of knowledge is not a deficiency to be eliminated but a necessity to be embraced. The positivist ideal of impersonal, objective knowledge — knowledge purified of all subjective contribution — is an illusion. Every act of knowing involves personal judgment: the selection of problems, the assessment of evidence, the evaluation of theories, the recognition of patterns. These judgments cannot be reduced to explicit rules because they are precisely the exercise of the tacit knowledge that makes rule-following meaningful.

Polanyi's rejection of the positivist ideal has direct implications for AI. If the positivist ideal were achievable — if knowledge could be fully formalized and purified of personal judgment — then replacing human knowers with computational systems would involve no loss. The system could do everything the human does, because everything the human does can be made explicit. But if Polanyi is right — if all knowledge has an irreducibly personal, tacit dimension — then replacing human knowers with computational systems necessarily loses something: the tacit knowledge that makes explicit knowledge meaningful, usable, and contextually appropriate. The loss may not be apparent in routine cases (where the explicit rules suffice), but it becomes critical in novel, ambiguous, or high-stakes situations — precisely the situations where expertise matters most.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that human expertise cannot be fully captured in computational systems. If Polanyi's analysis is correct — if all knowledge has an irreducibly tacit dimension that cannot be made explicit — then computational systems, which process only explicit information, cannot replicate the full scope of human expertise. They can process the explicit rules, the formalized knowledge, the articulated procedures — but they cannot replicate the tacit judgment, the contextual sensitivity, the embodied skill that makes expert performance possible. This is not a claim about current technological limitations that might be overcome by future advances; it is a structural claim about the nature of knowledge itself. CED-AH's insistence that human judgment cannot be replaced by algorithmic output finds its philosophical foundation in Polanyi's demonstration that knowledge has a dimension that is, in principle, beyond formalization.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative by articulating what "capability" means in its fullest sense. CED-AH requires that "users become more capable over time, not dependent" — and Polanyi's framework specifies what this means: capability includes not only explicit knowledge (facts, procedures, rules) but tacit knowledge (judgment, skill, contextual sensitivity, the ability to recognize patterns and respond to novelty). A system that increases the user's explicit knowledge while eroding tacit knowledge — for example, a diagnostic system that presents conclusions without requiring the user to develop diagnostic judgment — fails the Capability Growth imperative even if the user "knows more facts." True capability growth requires the development of tacit knowledge through practice, engagement, and progressive mastery — precisely the kind of indwelling that replacement-oriented systems prevent.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's analysis of the limits of computational systems through the "from-to" structure of knowing. Polanyi shows that we know the world by relying on subsidiary awareness that we cannot make focal. A computational system that processes only focal, explicit information — data, rules, patterns — misses the subsidiary dimension that gives explicit information its meaning. A medical AI that analyzes symptoms against a database lacks the physician's tacit awareness of the patient's overall presentation — the subtle signs that "something is wrong" before any individual symptom points to a diagnosis. CED-AH's concern about replacing human expertise with algorithmic output is illuminated by this Polanyian analysis: the replacement loses precisely the dimension of knowledge that matters most in complex, ambiguous situations.

**Complicates** CED-AH by raising the question of whether computational systems can develop something analogous to tacit knowledge. Modern machine learning systems — particularly neural networks trained on large datasets — develop internal representations that are not explicitly programmed and that their creators cannot fully articulate. Is this "tacit knowledge"? If so, Polanyi's argument against replacement would be weakened: if the system can develop its own tacit dimension, then the loss from replacement may be smaller than Polanyi suggests. CED-AH should address this question directly: are the internal representations of neural networks genuinely analogous to human tacit knowledge, or are they a different kind of non-explicit information? The strongest CED-AH response is that neural network representations lack the personal, embodied, contextual character that defines Polanyian tacit knowledge — they are non-explicit but not personal, not embodied, and not contextually situated in a life.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Polanyi's analysis of tacit knowledge is one of the most influential contributions to epistemology in the 20th century, and its implications for the human-machine relationship are direct and substantial. The argument that all knowledge has an irreducibly tacit dimension that cannot be formalized provides powerful philosophical grounding for CED-AH's claim that human expertise cannot be fully captured in computational systems. The rating is "Strong" because: (1) the connection to CED-AH's core claims is direct, not analogical — Polanyi's argument about the limits of formalization applies directly to the question of whether computational systems can replace human expertise; (2) the tacit knowledge thesis has been extensively supported by subsequent research in cognitive science, expertise studies, and naturalistic decision-making (Klein, de Groot, Ericsson — see cross-references); (3) Polanyi's analysis identifies a structural feature of knowledge, not a contingent limitation — the tacit dimension is not something that better formalization can overcome; and (4) the concept of indwelling provides a precise vocabulary for the distinction between amplification (systems that support indwelling) and replacement (systems that prevent it).

## Limitations

- **The tacit knowledge thesis is contested.** Some philosophers and cognitive scientists argue that tacit knowledge is merely "not yet explicit" knowledge — knowledge that could, in principle, be made fully explicit given sufficient effort and analytical tools. If this is correct, then the structural claim (tacit knowledge is irreducibly tacit) weakens to a practical claim (tacit knowledge is difficult but not impossible to formalize), which would reduce its force as evidence against computational replacement.
- **Machine learning complicates the picture.** Modern neural networks develop internal representations that are not explicitly programmed and that exhibit some characteristics of tacit knowledge — pattern recognition that cannot be fully articulated, context-sensitivity, performance that exceeds rule-following. Whether these representations are genuinely analogous to human tacit knowledge is an open and contested question. CED-AH should acknowledge this complication rather than assuming the Polanyian framework applies unchanged to contemporary AI systems.
- **Polanyi's examples are primarily from natural science.** His paradigm cases of tacit knowledge — scientific discovery, medical diagnosis, craft skill — are drawn from domains with rich physical and embodied dimensions. Whether the tacit knowledge thesis applies with equal force to purely informational domains — legal reasoning, financial analysis, strategic planning — is less clear. CED-AH should not assume that every domain involves the same type or degree of tacit knowledge.
- **The "personal" dimension.** Polanyi's concept of personal knowledge includes elements of commitment, passion, and conviction that go beyond mere skill or judgment. He argues that the knower's personal involvement — their stake in the truth, their willingness to commit — is constitutive of knowledge. This dimension may not map cleanly onto CED-AH's framework, which is more concerned with capability and agency than with personal commitment.
- **Historical context.** Polanyi wrote in the 1950s, in direct opposition to the logical positivism that dominated Anglo-American philosophy. His arguments are shaped by this context — they are specifically targeted at the positivist ideal of impersonal, fully formalized knowledge. Whether the same arguments apply to contemporary AI research, which has largely abandoned the positivist program, is a question that requires independent analysis.
- **Polanyi does not address computational systems.** His analysis predates the development of AI and computational automation. All applications of the tacit knowledge thesis to human-machine relationships are modern extensions that Polanyi did not himself make.
