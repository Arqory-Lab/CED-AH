## Summary

Floridi and Sanders' 2004 paper is one of the most important — and for CED-AH, most challenging — contributions to the philosophy of artificial agency. The paper argues that artificial agents *can* be moral agents at an appropriate level of abstraction (LoA), and that the standard objection — "machines can't really think/feel/decide, so they can't be moral agents" — is philosophically confused.

The argument proceeds through Floridi's method of levels of abstraction. At a low LoA (physical implementation), a computational system is silicon, wires, and electrical signals — clearly not a moral agent. At a high LoA (behavioral description), the same system receives inputs, processes them according to rules, and produces outputs that affect the world — and at this level, it may satisfy the functional criteria for moral agency: interactivity (responding to environmental stimuli), autonomy (changing state without direct external intervention), and adaptability (changing behavioral rules in response to experience).

Floridi and Sanders argue that these three properties — interactivity, autonomy, and adaptability — constitute sufficient conditions for moral agency at the appropriate LoA. They explicitly reject the requirement that moral agents must be conscious, intentional, or capable of phenomenological experience. The question is not whether the system "really" experiences or "really" decides — questions they consider level-dependent and often malformed — but whether, at the LoA that is relevant for moral analysis, the system exhibits agency.

This is a direct challenge to the philosophical tradition (Kant, Aristotle, most contemporary ethics) that grounds moral agency in consciousness, rationality, or phenomenological experience. Floridi and Sanders argue this tradition is anthropocentric: it defines moral agency in terms of human-like properties and then declares that only humans (and perhaps some animals) qualify. Their LoA approach is designed to be more generous, allowing that entities very different from humans might be moral agents at levels of description relevant to moral evaluation.

The paper is careful to distinguish between moral agency and moral responsibility. Even if an artificial system is a moral agent (it acts in ways that have moral consequences), it may not be morally responsible (culpable, blameworthy, or praiseworthy). Responsibility may require consciousness, intention, or other properties that the LoA approach does not attribute. But agency, they argue, does not require these properties.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Complicates** CED-AH's instrument thesis and Agency Test. This is the second most important adversarial source in this batch (after phil-023, Dennett's Intentional Stance). Floridi and Sanders directly challenge CED-AH's core claim that computational systems are instruments, not agents, by arguing that moral agency can be legitimately attributed to artificial systems at appropriate levels of abstraction. If they are right, CED-AH's Agency Test — which requires phenomenological experience, self-generated purpose, and ontological independence — sets the bar for agency too high by demanding human-like properties that are not necessary for moral agency.

The challenge is particularly serious because it comes from within the information philosophy tradition that CED-AH partly draws on. Floridi's authority in this space means the objection cannot be dismissed as coming from an outsider or from a philosophically naive perspective.

**However**, CED-AH has substantive resources to respond:

1. **The LoA objection cuts both ways.** If agency is level-dependent, then at the LoA of physical implementation (where CED-AH grounds its instrument thesis), computational systems are clearly not agents. Floridi and Sanders must argue that the high LoA (behavioral) is the *right* level for moral analysis. CED-AH can counter that the physical level is more fundamental and that treating behavioral patterns as agency is precisely the category error the framework diagnoses.

2. **The agency/responsibility gap undermines the practical implications.** Floridi and Sanders concede that moral agency does not entail moral responsibility. But CED-AH's concern is precisely about responsibility: who is accountable when a computational system produces harmful outcomes? If artificial agents are "moral agents" but not morally responsible, the attribution of agency changes nothing practically — we still need human decision-makers bearing responsibility. CED-AH's design imperatives (especially Imperative 1, Authority Retention) address exactly this need.

3. **Floridi's own later work retreats.** In the GPT-3 analysis (phil-027), Floridi describes LLMs as "agentless" — a characterization that is hard to reconcile with the 2004 paper's generous view of artificial agency. CED-AH can note this evolution and argue that the empirical reality of contemporary AI systems has not supported the theoretical possibility Floridi and Sanders identified.

4. **The "interactivity, autonomy, adaptability" criteria may be too weak.** A thermostat is interactive (responds to temperature), autonomous (adjusts without direct input), and adaptive (in programmable versions, changes behavior based on patterns). If thermostats are moral agents, the concept has been stretched beyond usefulness. CED-AH's Agency Test, by demanding more robust criteria, may be epistemically more disciplined.

## Limitations

- **The LoA approach may be too permissive.** If interactivity, autonomy, and adaptability are sufficient for moral agency, then thermostats, simple robots, and even some software scripts qualify as moral agents. This suggests the criteria may be necessary but not sufficient, or that the concept of moral agency has been stretched beyond its useful application.
- **The agency/responsibility distinction may be untenable.** Many philosophers argue that moral agency *just is* the capacity for moral responsibility — that an entity without responsibility cannot be a genuine moral agent. If agency and responsibility are inseparable, Floridi and Sanders' framework collapses: their artificial agents are not morally responsible, and therefore are not moral agents in any meaningful sense.
- **The paper predates LLMs.** Written in 2004, it addresses early AI systems. The question of whether LLMs satisfy the interactivity/autonomy/adaptability criteria more robustly than the systems Floridi and Sanders had in mind is open. But Floridi's own later characterization of GPT-3 as "agentless" (phil-027) suggests the answer is "not in the way that matters."
- **Levels of abstraction may relativize ethics unacceptably.** If moral properties are level-dependent, then whether an action is right or wrong depends on the level of description — a form of moral relativism that most ethicists would reject. CED-AH's coherence framework, which seeks level-independent evaluative criteria, may be preferable precisely because it avoids this relativization.
- **The paper does not engage with the specific harms CED-AH identifies.** Floridi and Sanders analyze the theoretical possibility of artificial moral agency but do not address the practical consequences of attributing agency to systems — the erosion of human authority, the degradation of human capability, the abdication of responsibility. CED-AH's argument is grounded in these practical stakes, not merely in the theoretical question.
