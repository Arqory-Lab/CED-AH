## Summary

The *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* is Immanuel Kant's (1724–1804) foundational work in moral philosophy — the text in which he identifies and develops the supreme principle of morality (the categorical imperative) and establishes the concepts of autonomy, dignity, and the kingdom of ends that have shaped ethical philosophy ever since. It is the most systematic Enlightenment argument for the unconditional moral worth of rational beings — and therefore the most powerful philosophical resource for CED-AH's claim that human decision-making authority is non-negotiable.

**Section I: The Good Will and Duty.** Kant begins with the claim that the only thing that is unconditionally good — good in all circumstances, without qualification — is a *good will*. Talents, temperaments, and fortune can all be misused; only the will that acts from duty — from respect for the moral law — is unconditionally valuable. This is not a claim about feelings or consequences but about the structure of moral reasoning: an action has moral worth not when it achieves good outcomes (which may depend on luck) but when it is performed because the agent recognizes it as required by the moral law.

The concept of the good will establishes Kant's foundational moral claim: moral value resides in the rational agency of the moral agent, not in the effects of actions or the utility of outcomes. A computational system that produces beneficial outputs has no moral worth in Kant's framework because it acts from programming, not from recognition of the moral law. It does not have a will, much less a good one. The moral weight rests entirely with the human agents who design, deploy, and use the system — which is precisely CED-AH's position.

**Section II: The Categorical Imperative.** The categorical imperative is Kant's formulation of the supreme moral principle — the principle that governs all moral reasoning. Kant presents it in several formulations, three of which are central:

1. **The Formula of Universal Law:** "Act only in accordance with that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law" (4:421). An action is morally permissible only if the principle behind it could be consistently universalized — only if you could will that everyone act on the same principle in the same circumstances. This is a test of rational consistency — a coherence test.

2. **The Formula of Humanity:** "So act that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means" (4:429). This is the most consequential formulation for CED-AH. Every rational being has unconditional worth — dignity (Würde) — and must be treated accordingly. Persons are not instruments to be used for purposes defined by others; they are ends in themselves — beings whose purposes matter intrinsically. To treat a person merely as a means is to violate their dignity by reducing them to the status of a tool.

3. **The Formula of the Kingdom of Ends:** "Act in accordance with the maxims of a member giving universal laws for a merely possible kingdom of ends" (4:439). The kingdom of ends is a community of rational beings who treat each other as ends in themselves and legislate universal moral laws together. It is the ideal of a moral community in which every member's rational autonomy is respected.

The Formula of Humanity is the strongest Enlightenment argument for CED-AH's core thesis. Computational systems are means — instruments designed to serve human purposes. Humans are ends — beings with inherent dignity whose purposes have intrinsic worth. The CED-AH framework can be read as the application of the Formula of Humanity to the domain of computational systems: every system must be designed so that it treats the humans who use it as ends (beings with autonomous rational agency whose judgment matters) and never merely as means (data sources, behavioral targets, attention reservoirs to be exploited for system optimization).

**Section III: Autonomy and Freedom.** Kant argues that the ground of the moral law is autonomy — the capacity of rational beings to give themselves the moral law, to determine their own actions through the exercise of practical reason. Autonomy is not mere independence (acting without external constraint) but self-legislation — the capacity to identify the moral law through reason and to act in accordance with it because one recognizes its authority. Heteronomy — being governed by external forces (desires, inclinations, commands, or the outputs of computational systems) rather than by one's own rational judgment — is the opposite of moral agency.

Kant's account of autonomy supports CED-AH's claim that moral agency requires both rationality and freedom — and that computational systems possess neither. A computational system does not give itself the moral law; it operates according to the instructions and training data provided by its designers. It does not exercise practical reason; it executes algorithms. It does not recognize the authority of the moral law; it follows parameters. It is, in Kant's precise sense, heteronomous — governed by external determination, not self-legislated. Therefore, it cannot be a moral agent. It cannot bear moral responsibility. It cannot legitimately hold decision-making authority over beings who *can*.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the strongest Enlightenment argument** for CED-AH's non-negotiable human authority through the Formula of Humanity. Kant's principle that humanity must always be treated as an end, never merely as a means, provides the most powerful philosophical foundation for CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative. Computational systems are means — instruments of human purpose. Humans are ends — beings with unconditional dignity. A system that makes decisions for humans without their genuine rational consent treats them as means — as objects to be managed, directed, or optimized rather than as autonomous rational agents whose judgment has inherent authority. CED-AH's first design imperative — "humans make all decisions; AH supports but never decides" — is the technological instantiation of the Formula of Humanity: every system must be designed so that human rational agency is the final authority, because human rational agency is what has unconditional moral worth.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that computational systems cannot be moral agents. Kant's account of moral agency is the most rigorous Enlightenment formulation of what agency requires: rationality (the capacity to identify the moral law through reason), freedom (the capacity to act in accordance with or contrary to the moral law), and autonomy (the capacity for rational self-legislation). Computational systems possess none of these. They do not reason about moral principles — they process instructions. They do not choose to act morally — they execute algorithms. They do not legislate moral laws for themselves — they are programmed by human designers. In Kant's precise framework, computational systems are heteronomous entities — things governed by external determination — and heteronomous entities cannot be moral agents. This directly supports CED-AH's instrument thesis: computational systems are instruments because they lack the moral agency that would make them anything else.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's Formula of Universal Law and the coherence test. Kant's Formula of Universal Law — "act only on that maxim you can will as universal law" — is, at its core, a test of rational coherence. A maxim that cannot be universalized without contradiction is rationally incoherent — it relies on conditions (e.g., others' trustworthiness) that its own universalization would destroy. CED-AH's first principle — coherence as the unavoidable criterion — echoes this Kantian insight: a system, principle, or practice that cannot maintain consistency when generalized is incoherent and therefore fails the most fundamental rational test. The connection is suggestive rather than direct, but it places CED-AH in a recognizable philosophical lineage: coherence as the test of moral and practical reason.

**Provides the concept of dignity (Würde)** that grounds CED-AH's ethical framework. Kant distinguishes between things that have a *price* (Preis) — a market value for which they can be exchanged — and things that have *dignity* (Würde) — an unconditional worth that admits of no equivalent. Rational beings have dignity; everything else has a price. Computational systems, however valuable, have a price — they can be replaced, upgraded, or discarded. Human beings have dignity — their worth is not contingent on their utility, efficiency, or productivity. CED-AH's framework preserves this distinction: the value of computational systems is instrumental (measured by their contribution to human coherence and flourishing); the value of human beings is intrinsic (non-negotiable, regardless of comparative performance on any metric).

**Qualifies** CED-AH by raising the formalism objection. Kant's moral philosophy has been criticized for being excessively formal — for providing a procedure (the categorical imperative) without substantive content. Hegel objected that the categorical imperative is an "empty formalism" that can rationalize any action through clever maxim construction. The same objection could be directed at CED-AH's coherence principle: is "coherence" substantive enough to generate specific design requirements, or is it so formal that it can be satisfied trivially? CED-AH must demonstrate that its coherence criterion has enough content to do real evaluative work — that it can distinguish between genuinely coherent and merely formally consistent systems. The AH Design Imperatives are intended to provide this content, but whether they do so successfully is a question that CED-AH's critics will press.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Groundwork* is the most influential single text in modern moral philosophy, and Kant's arguments about the unconditional worth of rational beings, the Formula of Humanity, and the nature of moral agency are among the most rigorously formulated and extensively debated in the Western philosophical tradition. The Formula of Humanity provides a direct and powerful philosophical foundation for CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative. Kant's account of moral agency as requiring rationality, freedom, and autonomy directly supports the claim that computational systems cannot be moral agents. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) Kant's moral philosophy is highly contested — utilitarian, virtue ethicist, and care ethicist critics have raised substantial objections to its formalism, its individualism, and its neglect of emotion and context; (2) the application of Kantian moral philosophy to computational systems requires interpretive extension — Kant addressed relations among rational beings, not between rational beings and instruments; (3) Kant's metaphysics of freedom (noumenal freedom vs. phenomenal determinism) is among the most notoriously difficult and contested positions in the history of philosophy; and (4) the historical distance (241 years) requires interpretive bridging.

## Limitations

- **Formalism objections.** Kant's moral philosophy has been criticized for excessive formalism — for providing a test (universalizability) without substantive moral content. Whether the categorical imperative can actually generate specific moral conclusions is debated. The same critique applies to CED-AH's coherence criterion: is it formal enough to be unhelpful, or substantive enough to be genuinely evaluative?
- **The metaphysics of freedom.** Kant's account of moral agency depends on his distinction between the phenomenal world (governed by causal determinism) and the noumenal world (where freedom is possible). This metaphysics is among the most contested in the history of philosophy. CED-AH does not need to adopt Kant's transcendental idealism, but it must acknowledge that the Kantian account of freedom that grounds moral agency rests on metaphysical commitments that many philosophers reject.
- **Neglect of emotion and context.** Kant's moral philosophy has been criticized for its sharp separation of duty from inclination and its neglect of the role of emotion, empathy, and context-sensitivity in moral life (Hume, Williams, Gilligan, Murdoch). CED-AH, which draws on Aristotelian phronesis (context-sensitive practical wisdom) as well as Kantian autonomy, must navigate the tension between these two moral traditions.
- **Individualism.** Like Locke, Kant's moral philosophy is grounded in the individual rational agent. Communitarian, feminist, and relational critics (MacIntyre, Noddings, Baier) argue that this individualism misrepresents moral life, which is fundamentally relational and contextual. CED-AH's framework operates at both individual and institutional levels; Kant provides mainly individual-level analysis.
- **The "merely as a means" qualification.** The Formula of Humanity prohibits treating persons *merely* as means — not treating them as means at all. We treat each other as means constantly (the baker serves my need for bread; I serve the baker's need for income). The question is whether computational systems treat humans *merely* as means — whether they reduce humans to data sources or behavioral targets without respecting their autonomous rational agency. CED-AH must specify what "treating as an end" requires in the context of computational system design.
- **Historical distance and technological anachronism.** Kant wrote in 1785. He did not contemplate automated decision-making, machine learning, or computational systems of any kind. All applications of his framework to these technologies are modern extensions.
