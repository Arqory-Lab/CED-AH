## Summary

The *Politics* is Aristotle's systematic inquiry into the nature of the political community (polis), its constitution, and the conditions for its flourishing. The passages relevant to CED-AH are concentrated in Book I, Chapters 3–7, where Aristotle develops his account of the household (oikos) as the basic unit of the political community and, in doing so, articulates the oldest surviving philosophical analysis of the distinction between instruments and agents — between things that are *used* and beings that *use*.

Aristotle's argument proceeds as follows. The household consists of three relationships: master-slave, husband-wife, and parent-child. In analyzing the master-slave relationship, Aristotle distinguishes between two kinds of possessions (ktēmata): **instruments of production** (poiētika organa) and **instruments of action** (praktika organa). A shuttle is an instrument of production — it produces cloth. A garment is an instrument of action — it is used in the activity of living. The critical distinction is between instruments that produce something beyond their own use and instruments that are constitutive of an activity.

Aristotle then introduces a thought experiment that is, remarkably, the oldest philosophical description of automation:

> "For if every instrument could accomplish its own work, obeying or anticipating the will of others, like the statues of Daedalus, or the tripods of Hephaestus, which, says the poet, 'of their own accord entered the assembly of the Gods'; if, in like manner, the shuttle would weave and the plectrum touch the lyre without a hand to guide them, chief workmen would not want servants, nor masters slaves." (Politics I.4, 1253b33–1254a1, Jowett trans.)

This passage is extraordinary because it imagines precisely the condition that modern AI discourse assumes is novel: instruments that operate without direct human guidance. Aristotle's response to this hypothetical is not to consider whether such instruments would be agents — whether they would deserve rights, have interests, or merit moral consideration. His response is that such instruments would simply *eliminate the need for human slaves as instruments*. Self-moving instruments remain instruments. Their motion does not confer agency.

Aristotle then develops his account of **natural slavery** — the most morally repugnant passage in his philosophical corpus. He argues that some human beings are "natural slaves" — people who possess bodily strength but lack the rational capacity for self-governance and are therefore "living tools" (empsychon organon) whose function is to serve those with greater rational capacity (1254a14–17, 1254b16–20). The natural slave, for Aristotle, is an instrument that happens to be alive — a tool with a soul.

This argument is, by any reasonable modern standard, morally indefensible. It was used for centuries to justify chattel slavery, colonial exploitation, and racial hierarchy. CED-AH must engage with it not because the argument is correct but because the conceptual vocabulary it introduced — the distinction between instruments and agents, between things that are *used for* purposes and beings that *have* purposes — remains philosophically operative.

What matters for CED-AH is not Aristotle's horrifying application of the instrument/agent distinction (to human beings) but the distinction itself. Aristotle identifies the defining feature of an agent (as opposed to an instrument) as **self-directed rational activity in pursuit of one's own good**. An instrument acts for the good of its user; an agent acts for its own good. A lyre does not play itself for its own pleasure — it is played by a musician for the musician's purpose. Even Aristotle's imagined self-moving shuttle does not weave for its own sake — it weaves because it has been designed and directed to weave. Autonomous motion is not sufficient for agency. What is sufficient is self-directed rational activity aimed at one's own flourishing.

Aristotle also distinguishes between **instruments** (organa) and **assistants** (hupēretai). An instrument is wholly subordinate to the user's purpose — it has no independent contribution to make beyond the function for which it is designed. An assistant, by contrast, is a rational being who brings judgment, initiative, and understanding to the collaborative activity. The distinction between instrument and assistant maps onto a crucial question for CED-AH: is a computational system more like a tool (which simply executes) or an assistant (which contributes independent judgment)? Aristotle would classify even the most sophisticated AI as an instrument — a self-moving shuttle, perhaps, but a shuttle nonetheless — because it lacks the self-directed rationality that characterizes genuine agency.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the foundational concept** for CED-AH's instrument thesis (Part 4). CED-AH's claim that "computational systems are instruments, not agents" is the modern articulation of a distinction Aristotle drew 2,300 years ago. Aristotle's analysis provides the conceptual vocabulary: an instrument acts for the good of its user, not for its own good; an agent acts for its own good on the basis of self-directed rational activity. By this criterion, computational systems — which have no good of their own, no self-directed purposes, and no rational activity independent of their designers' intentions — are instruments. CED-AH extends Aristotle's analysis to the specific case of computational systems, but the philosophical architecture is Aristotelian.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's argument that autonomous operation does not confer agency. Aristotle's thought experiment about self-moving instruments (the statues of Daedalus, the tripods of Hephaestus, the autonomous shuttle) is the oldest philosophical argument for exactly this claim. Instruments that move themselves are still instruments. They remain tools designed for purposes defined by their users. The fact that a computational system can generate outputs without moment-to-moment human guidance does not make it an agent any more than Hephaestus's self-moving tripods are guests at the divine assembly. This directly counters the implicit assumption in much AI discourse that increasing autonomy places systems on a spectrum toward genuine agency.

**Provides essential critical context** that CED-AH must acknowledge. Aristotle's instrument/agent distinction was developed in the context of his defense of natural slavery — the argument that some human beings are "living instruments" whose purpose is to serve others. This is not an incidental association; it is the original application of the very distinction CED-AH employs. CED-AH must explicitly acknowledge this history and explain why the conceptual distinction survives the moral collapse of its original application. The argument, which CED-AH can and should make, is: Aristotle was catastrophically wrong to apply the instrument category to human beings (who are agents by any defensible criterion) but the instrument category itself is real and correctly applied to non-sentient, non-rational artifacts (including computational systems). The moral failure was a failure of *application* (misclassifying agents as instruments), not a failure of *distinction* (recognizing that instruments and agents are fundamentally different kinds of things).

**Qualifies** CED-AH's instrument thesis by revealing the stakes of misclassification. Aristotle's history demonstrates that the instrument/agent distinction is not merely an abstract philosophical taxonomy — it is a distinction with profound moral, political, and practical consequences. Getting it wrong in one direction (classifying agents as instruments) produces slavery and exploitation. Getting it wrong in the other direction (classifying instruments as agents) produces what CED-AH identifies as the anthropomorphization of technology — the attribution of agency, interests, and moral status to systems that have none. Both errors are failures of coherence. CED-AH's framework must maintain the distinction with precision because the consequences of losing it — in either direction — are severe.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's critique of the "alignment" paradigm. Aristotle's analysis implies that the question "how do we align an instrument with human values?" is malformed in the same way that "how do we align a shuttle with the weaver's intentions?" is malformed. Instruments do not have intentions that could be aligned or misaligned — they have functions that are well-designed or poorly designed. The alignment discourse treats computational systems as proto-agents whose values need aligning; Aristotle's framework treats them as instruments whose design needs evaluating. CED-AH's shift from "alignment" to "design" mirrors Aristotle's distinction between aligning wills (which requires agency) and designing instruments (which requires craft).

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Politics* provides the oldest surviving philosophical analysis of the instrument/agent distinction, and the self-moving instrument thought experiment is a remarkably prescient anticipation of the philosophical questions posed by autonomous technology. The conceptual analysis is rigorous and has been developed, challenged, and refined over 2,300 years of philosophical engagement. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) the historical distance is enormous and the interpretive translation required is substantial; (2) the passage is embedded in a defense of slavery, which requires careful handling; and (3) Aristotle's conception of "instruments" did not include anything resembling computational information-processing systems, so the extension involves an interpretive leap that, while philosophically warranted, cannot be attributed directly to Aristotle.

## Limitations

- **The slavery context cannot be sanitized.** Aristotle's instrument/agent distinction was developed to classify human beings as instruments. Any use of this distinction must explicitly acknowledge this history and demonstrate that the distinction can survive the collapse of its original application. Failing to do so risks intellectual dishonesty and undermines credibility with audiences aware of the history.
- **Historical distance and conceptual anachronism.** Aristotle's "instruments" were physical objects — shuttles, lyres, plows. Extending the instrument category to computational systems that process information, generate language, and exhibit complex emergent behaviors requires philosophical argument that Aristotle did not and could not provide. CED-AH must make this argument on its own terms, not simply assert that Aristotle said so.
- **The self-moving instrument thought experiment is a hypothetical.** Aristotle imagined self-moving instruments but did not have to contend with their actual existence. Whether his analysis would survive confrontation with real autonomous systems — which exhibit behaviors far more complex than self-moving tripods — is a question Aristotle never had to answer.
- **The agent/instrument binary may be too sharp.** Some philosophers (Floridi, 2013; Gunkel, 2018) argue for intermediate categories — "artificial agents," "moral patients," entities with some but not all features of full agency. Aristotle's binary (instrument or agent, nothing in between) may not accommodate the full range of entities that modern technology produces. CED-AH's instrument thesis may need to engage with these intermediate positions rather than simply asserting the binary.
- **Translation complications.** Key terms — organon (instrument/tool), ktēma (possession), hupēretēs (assistant), empsychon organon (ensouled instrument) — carry connotations in Greek that no English translation fully captures. Scholarly debate about the precise meaning of these terms affects the reliability of any philosophical conclusions drawn from them.
- **Selective appropriation risk.** Taking Aristotle's conceptual vocabulary while rejecting his conclusions about slavery risks the charge of philosophical cherry-picking. CED-AH should demonstrate that the instrument/agent distinction is independently defensible (as indeed it is, through Searle, Dennett, and contemporary philosophy of mind), not merely inherited from Aristotle.
