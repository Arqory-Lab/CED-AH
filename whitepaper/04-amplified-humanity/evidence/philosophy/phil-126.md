## Summary

The *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788) is the second of Kant's three Critiques and the work in which he provides the most systematic account of practical reason — the faculty by which rational beings determine their own actions through the recognition of moral law. While the *Groundwork* (1785) identified and formulated the supreme principle of morality (the categorical imperative), the *Critique of Practical Reason* provides its philosophical justification, examines its relationship to freedom and autonomy, and addresses the question of how the moral law can serve as an incentive for action.

**The Moral Law as a "Fact of Reason."** The *Critique of Practical Reason* introduces Kant's most distinctive and contested claim about the moral law: that it is a "fact of reason" (Faktum der Vernunft) — not a fact about the empirical world but an irreducible datum of practical reason itself. The moral law is not derived from experience, from human nature, from divine command, or from any theoretical foundation. It is given to rational beings in and through the exercise of practical reason. To be a rational agent is to be aware of the moral law — not as an external constraint but as the law one gives oneself through one's own rational nature.

This claim is philosophically radical and has been extensively debated. If the moral law is a "fact of reason," it cannot be further justified — it is not derived from anything more basic. Critics (e.g., Schopenhauer) have charged that this makes Kantian morality dogmatic — an assertion rather than a demonstration. Defenders (e.g., Allison, Korsgaard) argue that the moral law's status as a fact of reason is not a weakness but a strength: it reflects the self-grounding character of practical reason, which cannot be reduced to theoretical reason, empirical psychology, or any external foundation.

For CED-AH, this claim has a structural parallel: CED-AH identifies coherence as a first principle that cannot be argued against without presupposing it — a principle that is self-grounding in the same way Kant claims the moral law is self-grounding. Both Kant's moral law and CED-AH's coherence principle are presented as unavoidable for any rational agent: to deny them is to presuppose them. Whether this structural parallel constitutes genuine philosophical support or merely terminological similarity is a question CED-AH must address.

**Autonomy and Heteronomy Deepened.** The *Critique of Practical Reason* develops the *Groundwork*'s account of autonomy (self-legislation of the moral law by rational agents) and heteronomy (determination of the will by external forces) into a more rigorous distinction. Kant argues that all non-moral incentives — desire, inclination, self-interest, happiness — are heteronomous: they determine the will from outside, through the contingent character of the agent's desires. Only the moral law is an autonomous incentive: it determines the will from within, through the agent's own rational nature.

This deepened autonomy/heteronomy distinction extends the *Groundwork*'s implications for CED-AH's instrument thesis. A computational system that generates recommendations, predictions, or decisions operates heteronomously by definition: its outputs are determined by training data, algorithms, and optimization functions — external determinants that the system does not and cannot evaluate through rational self-legislation. A human agent who acts on a computational system's output without exercising independent rational judgment is, in Kant's framework, allowing their will to be determined heteronomously — by an external force rather than by their own practical reason. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative protects against this: it requires that humans exercise autonomous rational judgment rather than passively accepting heteronomous determination by computational outputs.

**Respect for the Moral Law.** Kant argues that the only morally appropriate incentive for action is *respect* (Achtung) for the moral law — a unique feeling that is produced by reason itself, not by empirical inclination. Respect is not an emotion in the ordinary sense but the subjective experience of the moral law's authority over the will. It involves a "humiliation" of self-love (the subordination of personal inclination to the demands of duty) and simultaneously an "elevation" of the person (the recognition of one's own dignity as a rational, autonomous being).

This account of respect has implications for CED-AH's understanding of what "treating humanity as an end" requires in practice. Respect for persons, in Kant's sense, is not mere politeness or consideration but the recognition of their rational autonomy — their capacity to determine their own actions through the exercise of practical reason. A computational system that manipulates users (through dark patterns, attention capture, or behavioral nudging) violates the Kantian requirement of respect by treating users' rational autonomy as an obstacle to be circumvented rather than a capacity to be supported. CED-AH's design imperatives — particularly Transparency and Calibrated Honesty — operationalize Kantian respect: they require that systems engage with users as rational agents capable of evaluating information and making their own judgments, not as behavioral targets to be managed.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Extends and deepens** the *Groundwork*'s support for CED-AH's instrument thesis. The *Critique of Practical Reason*'s more rigorous account of autonomy and heteronomy sharpens the argument that computational systems cannot be moral agents. Moral agency, for Kant, requires not merely rationality (which a sufficiently sophisticated computational system might simulate) but *autonomy* — the capacity for rational self-legislation, for giving oneself the moral law through the exercise of practical reason. No computational system possesses this capacity: it does not legislate moral laws for itself but follows instructions and optimizes objectives defined by external agents. It is heteronomous by design, by construction, and by nature. CED-AH's instrument thesis is reinforced: computational systems are instruments because they are constitutively heteronomous — they cannot be autonomous in the sense that moral agency requires.

**Provides structural precedent** for CED-AH's self-grounding first principle. Kant's claim that the moral law is a "fact of reason" — a self-grounding principle that cannot be derived from anything more basic and cannot be denied without presupposing it — is structurally analogous to CED-AH's claim about coherence. Both identify a first principle that is self-grounding rather than externally justified. Both claim that denying the principle requires presupposing it (denying the authority of practical reason requires exercising practical reason; denying coherence requires producing a coherent argument). Whether this structural parallel is philosophically deep or merely rhetorical is a question CED-AH should address honestly — but the parallel is genuine and places CED-AH in a serious philosophical lineage.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Transparency and Calibrated Honesty imperatives through the concept of respect. Kant's account of respect for rational autonomy — the recognition that persons are rational agents capable of determining their own actions through practical reason — provides the moral-philosophical foundation for CED-AH's requirements that systems be transparent (reasoning explicable, limitations disclosed) and calibrated (stated confidence matches actual accuracy). These imperatives express respect for the user's rational autonomy: they treat the user as a being capable of evaluating information and exercising independent judgment, not as a behavioral target to be managed through information asymmetry.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by raising the problem of moral motivation. Kant's account of respect as the sole moral incentive has been criticized for its austerity — for demanding that moral agents act from duty alone, without support from emotion, inclination, or habit. If CED-AH requires that users always exercise autonomous rational judgment when interacting with computational systems, it may be demanding something psychologically unsustainable. Behavioral research (Kahneman, 2011; Thaler & Sunstein, 2008) demonstrates that human decision-making is systematically influenced by heuristics, biases, and contextual factors that Kant's framework does not accommodate. CED-AH must bridge the gap between Kantian moral idealism and the realities of human cognitive limitation — designing systems that support rational autonomy as humans actually experience it, not as Kant's idealized rational agent exercises it.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Critique of Practical Reason* is the most systematic Enlightenment account of moral agency, autonomy, and practical reason, and it extends and deepens the *Groundwork*'s arguments in ways directly relevant to CED-AH. The autonomy/heteronomy distinction provides a precise philosophical vocabulary for the instrument thesis, and the concept of respect grounds CED-AH's transparency and honesty imperatives. The "fact of reason" doctrine provides a structural parallel to CED-AH's self-grounding coherence principle. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) Kant's metaphysics of freedom remains deeply contested; (2) the "fact of reason" doctrine has been criticized as philosophically question-begging; (3) the gap between Kantian moral idealism and actual human cognitive capacities is substantial; and (4) the application to computational systems requires interpretive extension that Kant did not and could not perform.

## Limitations

- **The "fact of reason" is contested.** Kant's claim that the moral law is a self-grounding "fact of reason" has been challenged as dogmatic, circular, or insufficiently justified (Schopenhauer, Hegel, contemporary naturalists). CED-AH's structural parallel (coherence as self-grounding) faces analogous challenges and should not assume that the Kantian strategy resolves them.
- **The autonomy/heteronomy binary may be too sharp.** Human decision-making is rarely purely autonomous or purely heteronomous — it involves complex interactions between rational deliberation, emotional input, social context, and cognitive limitation. CED-AH's design imperatives must accommodate this complexity, not simply mandate Kantian autonomy.
- **Psychological realism.** Kant's moral psychology — which sharply separates duty from inclination and identifies respect as the sole moral incentive — has been criticized for failing to account for the role of emotion, empathy, and habituation in moral life (Hume, Williams, Murdoch, contemporary moral psychology). CED-AH should not rely on a moral psychology that empirical research contradicts.
- **The formal structure may not translate.** The structural parallel between the "fact of reason" and CED-AH's coherence principle may be more rhetorical than philosophical. Both claim self-grounding status, but whether the same argumentative strategy works for different first principles requires independent demonstration.
- **Historical distance.** Kant wrote in 1788. The application of his framework to computational systems, machine learning, and AI governance is entirely a modern extension.
- **Relationship to the First Critique.** The *Critique of Practical Reason* presupposes the epistemological framework of the *Critique of Pure Reason* — particularly the distinction between phenomena and noumena that makes transcendental freedom possible. CED-AH does not adopt this framework. Whether Kant's account of moral agency survives outside his transcendental idealism is an open question.
