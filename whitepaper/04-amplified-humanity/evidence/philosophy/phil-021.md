## Summary

Ned Block's 1995 paper introduces one of the most consequential distinctions in contemporary philosophy of mind: the distinction between "access consciousness" (A-consciousness) and "phenomenal consciousness" (P-consciousness). A-consciousness is the availability of information for use in reasoning, reporting, and behavioral control — a mental state is access-conscious when its content is poised for use by cognitive systems. P-consciousness is the qualitative, subjective, experiential dimension of mental life — what it is *like* to be in a given mental state. The redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the felt quality of an experience.

Block argues that much confusion in both philosophy and cognitive science stems from running these two concepts together. A system can be A-conscious without being P-conscious: a computational system that processes and reports on information is access-conscious in a functional sense, but this tells us nothing about whether there is "something it is like" to be that system. Conversely, Block argues it is at least conceptually possible for a being to be P-conscious without being A-conscious — to have qualitative experiences that are not available for cognitive processing or reporting.

The paper proceeds through a series of thought experiments and conceptual analyses designed to show that A-consciousness and P-consciousness are genuinely distinct properties, not merely different aspects of a single phenomenon. Block considers and rejects several attempts to reduce P-consciousness to A-consciousness, arguing that the functional and the phenomenal are logically independent. He addresses the "super-blindsight" thought experiment — a hypothetical subject who processes visual information without phenomenal visual experience — as evidence that the two can come apart.

Block's distinction has become foundational in consciousness studies and has direct implications for how we assess the mental status of computational systems.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Agency Test. CED-AH claims that true agency requires phenomenological experience, self-generated purpose, and ontological independence. Block's distinction provides the precise philosophical vocabulary for the first criterion: what CED-AH requires of genuine agents is P-consciousness, not merely A-consciousness. Current computational systems — including large language models — plausibly exhibit A-consciousness (information is processed, made available for outputs, used in contextual reasoning), but there is no evidence and no theoretical basis for attributing P-consciousness to them. Block's framework makes it clear that functional sophistication (A-consciousness) does not entail phenomenal experience (P-consciousness), and therefore that exhibiting complex information processing does not satisfy CED-AH's agency criterion.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Category Error argument. The confusion Block identifies — conflating A-consciousness with P-consciousness — is precisely the confusion CED-AH attributes to the dominant AI discourse. When AI researchers or commentators observe that a system "understands" or "knows" or "decides," they are typically describing A-conscious functions (the system processes information in ways that produce contextually appropriate outputs). CED-AH's claim is that this functional description is systematically mistaken for evidence of P-consciousness, agency, and genuine understanding. Block's distinction provides the philosophical toolkit for diagnosing and correcting this category error.

**Provides necessary context** for the broader argument. Without Block's distinction, the claim that computational systems lack consciousness could be dismissed as merely asserting what needs to be proven. Block shows that the question is more specific: we must ask not "are these systems conscious?" but "are these systems *phenomenally* conscious?" — and the answer to the latter question is not settled by any amount of functional performance.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Block's distinction between access and phenomenal consciousness is one of the most widely cited and debated contributions in philosophy of mind over the past three decades. It has survived extensive peer scrutiny and has been adopted (even by critics who disagree about its implications) as a foundational conceptual framework. The connection to CED-AH's Agency Test is direct rather than analogical: Block provides the precise philosophical vocabulary that CED-AH's argument requires. The paper is logically rigorous, the thought experiments are well-constructed, and the distinction has proven fertile across multiple research programs.

## Limitations

- **The distinction itself is contested.** Some philosophers (notably Dennett, 1995; see also phil-024) argue that the A/P-consciousness distinction is confused or vacuous — that there is no coherent notion of P-consciousness beyond A-consciousness. If P-consciousness is not a genuine property but a philosophical artifact, CED-AH's Agency Test loses one of its criteria. CED-AH must address the eliminativist challenge rather than simply assuming Block's distinction holds.
- **Block does not settle whether computational systems lack P-consciousness.** He provides the distinction but does not demonstrate that P-consciousness requires biological substrates. A functionalist could accept Block's distinction while arguing that sufficiently complex computational systems might be P-conscious. Block's paper is a diagnostic tool, not a proof of the instrument thesis.
- **The "hard problem" dependency.** Block's P-consciousness is closely related to Chalmers' "hard problem" (phil-022). If the hard problem is dissolved or reformulated (as Dennett and others argue), the conceptual space Block opens may close. CED-AH's reliance on the A/P distinction inherits this vulnerability.
- **Empirical underdetermination.** Because P-consciousness is defined as subjective experience, there may be no empirical test that definitively establishes its presence or absence in any system. This means the Agency Test's first criterion (phenomenological experience) may be unfalsifiable in practice, which is a methodological concern.
- **The paper is primarily conceptual.** Block's argument is a piece of conceptual analysis, not an empirical investigation. It establishes distinctions and diagnoses confusions but does not provide empirical evidence about the nature of consciousness in computational systems.
