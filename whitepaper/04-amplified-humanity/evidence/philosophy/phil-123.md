## Summary

The *Two Treatises of Government* is John Locke's (1632–1704) most important political work and one of the foundational texts of liberal political theory. The First Treatise is a detailed refutation of Robert Filmer's *Patriarcha* (which argued for the divine right of kings); the Second Treatise, which is the philosophically significant text, develops Locke's positive account of legitimate political authority based on natural rights, consent, and the social contract.

**The State of Nature (Ch. II).** Locke begins by imagining a condition of human beings prior to the establishment of political authority — the "state of nature." Unlike Hobbes's state of nature (which is a "war of all against all"), Locke's is a condition of natural freedom and equality governed by natural law. All persons are born free and equal in the state of nature — no one has natural authority over anyone else. Natural law, discoverable by reason, prescribes that "no one ought to harm another in his life, health, liberty, or possessions" (II.6). This natural equality is not merely a descriptive claim but a normative one: it establishes the baseline against which all claims to authority must be justified.

For CED-AH, Locke's state of nature establishes a philosophical principle that is directly relevant to the human-AI relationship: authority over persons must be justified, and the default condition is freedom. No entity — human or computational — has authority over a human being unless that authority has been legitimately established through consent. A computational system that makes decisions for a human being without that person's genuine, informed consent operates without legitimate authority — it is, in Lockean terms, exercising power without right.

**Property in One's Person (Ch. V).** Locke's most philosophically consequential claim about property begins not with land or goods but with the self: "every Man has a Property in his own Person. This no Body has any Right to but himself. The Labour of his Body, and the Work of his Hands, we may say, are properly his" (II.27). This is the foundation of what Locke calls "property" in the broad sense — not merely material possessions but the fundamental right of each person to their own body, mind, labor, and the products thereof.

The concept of self-ownership — property in one's own person — is directly relevant to CED-AH's non-negotiable human authority. If each person has property in their own person, then each person has a prior and inviolable right to their own cognitive activity — their own judgment, reasoning, and decision-making. A system that supplants a person's judgment is, in Lockean terms, violating that person's property in themselves. CED-AH's "Authority Retention" imperative — "humans make all decisions; AH supports but never decides" — can be read as the application of Locke's self-ownership principle to the domain of human-computational interaction: just as no one has the right to appropriate another person's labor without consent, no system has the right to appropriate another person's judgment without consent.

**Consent as the Basis of Legitimate Authority (Ch. VIII).** The social contract, for Locke, is the mechanism by which individuals leave the state of nature and establish political society. The essential feature is **consent**: legitimate political authority exists only because free individuals have agreed to be governed, for the purpose of better protecting their natural rights (life, liberty, property). Authority that is not grounded in consent is illegitimate — it is tyranny, not governance.

Locke distinguishes between express consent (explicit agreement to join a political community) and tacit consent (implicit agreement through the enjoyment of benefits). Both require that the consenting party be a rational agent capable of understanding and voluntarily accepting the terms. This requirement is crucial for CED-AH: computational systems cannot give consent because they are not rational agents with wills, interests, or understanding. They cannot enter into a social contract. They cannot be parties to a consent relationship. Therefore, they cannot hold legitimate authority. The delegation of decision-making power to a computational system is not a consent-based transfer of authority — it is an abdication of authority to an entity that cannot legitimately hold it.

**The Dissolution of Government (Ch. XIX).** Locke argues that when a government betrays the trust placed in it by the people — when it acts contrary to the purposes for which it was established — the people retain the right to dissolve it and establish a new one. Authority is conditional: it depends on the continued fulfillment of the purposes for which it was granted. This principle applies, by extension, to any system to which authority has been delegated: if the system ceases to serve the purposes for which it was deployed — if it begins to undermine rather than support human flourishing — then the authority delegated to it should be revoked. CED-AH's "Reversibility" imperative — "every action can be undone; humans can always override" — is the design-level instantiation of Locke's dissolution principle: no delegation of operational capacity to a computational system is irrevocable, because no delegation of authority is unconditional.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's "Authority Retention" imperative through the consent theory of legitimate authority. Locke's argument that legitimate authority requires the consent of rational agents provides the political-philosophical foundation for CED-AH's claim that human decision-making authority is non-negotiable. Computational systems cannot give consent (they are not rational agents), cannot be accountable (they have no interests to place at stake), and cannot enter into the trust relationship that legitimates authority. Therefore, they cannot hold legitimate authority. The claim that "the AI recommended it" as sole justification for a decision is, in Lockean terms, the exercise of power without right — the defining feature of illegitimate authority.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that agency is grounded in self-ownership. Locke's principle that "every Man has a Property in his own Person" — that each individual has a prior right to their own cognitive and physical activity — provides philosophical grounding for the claim that human judgment cannot be legitimately appropriated by computational systems. Just as another person cannot rightfully take the products of your labor without consent, a system cannot rightfully take the products of your cognition — your decisions, your judgments, your evaluations — without genuine, informed agreement. CED-AH's framework operationalizes this principle: AH-compliant systems support but never supplant human cognitive activity.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's "Reversibility" imperative through the dissolution-of-government doctrine. Locke's argument that the people retain the right to dissolve a government that betrays its trust provides a philosophical model for CED-AH's insistence that humans must always be able to override, undo, or revoke the operational capacity of any computational system. Authority is conditional, delegated, and revocable — never absolute, inherent, or irrevocable. A system that takes irreversible actions without explicit human confirmation violates this principle not merely as a design failure but as a philosophical transgression against the nature of legitimate authority.

**Provides the political-philosophical framework** for CED-AH's governance implications (Part 8). Locke's social contract theory — with its emphasis on consent, accountability, natural rights, and the right of revolution — informs CED-AH's account of how computational systems should be governed. Just as political authority must serve the common good or be dissolved, computational systems must serve human flourishing or be redesigned. CED-AH's stakeholder responsibilities — for developers, deployers, policymakers, and users — echo Locke's distribution of political responsibility among the governed and their governors.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by raising the problem of tacit consent. Locke's distinction between express and tacit consent was problematic even in his own day — David Hume argued that most people born under a government have never consented to it, expressly or tacitly. The same problem applies to computational systems: users who interact with AI systems embedded in workplaces, institutions, and digital infrastructure may never have given meaningful consent to the authority those systems exercise over their work and decisions. CED-AH's framework should address this: if Authority Retention requires consent, what counts as genuine consent in a world where computational systems are ubiquitous and often invisible? The Lockean framework identifies the problem but does not resolve it for the computational age.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Two Treatises* is one of the foundational texts of Western political philosophy and liberal democratic theory. Locke's arguments about consent, self-ownership, and the conditions for legitimate authority are directly relevant to CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative and its broader governance framework. The consent theory provides a rigorous philosophical basis for the claim that computational systems cannot hold legitimate decision-making authority. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) Locke's political philosophy was developed for relations among human beings and human institutions, not for human-machine relationships — the extension requires interpretive work; (2) the social contract tradition has been extensively criticized (Hume, Rawls's shift from actual to hypothetical consent, feminist critiques of the public/private distinction); (3) Locke's concept of "natural rights" depends on theological premises (natural law as God's law) that CED-AH, as a secular framework, does not share; and (4) the historical distance (337 years) introduces interpretive challenges.

## Limitations

- **Theological foundation of natural rights.** Locke's natural rights are grounded in the law of nature, which Locke understands as God's rational governance of creation. The claim that humans have "property in their own person" ultimately rests on the claim that humans are God's property ("Workmanship") and therefore cannot be appropriated by other humans. Whether Lockean self-ownership survives secularization — and if so, on what basis — is a contested question (Simmons, 1992; Waldron, 1988). CED-AH uses a secularized version of Locke's principles and must provide independent grounding.
- **The consent problem.** Locke's theory of consent has been criticized as philosophically inadequate since Hume's essay "Of the Original Contract" (1748). In practice, very few people ever expressly consent to political authority; tacit consent is so expansive as to be nearly meaningless. The same criticism applies to CED-AH's application: in what sense do users "consent" to the authority of systems embedded in institutional infrastructure?
- **Individualism.** Locke's political philosophy is radically individualist — it begins from the isolated rational agent in the state of nature and constructs political community from individual consent. Critics (communitarian, feminist, and postcolonial) argue that this individualism misrepresents the fundamentally social character of human life and obscures the ways in which power operates through structures that individuals do not choose. CED-AH's framework, which addresses systemic and institutional dynamics, may need a more socially embedded political philosophy than Locke provides.
- **Property theory critiques.** Locke's labor theory of property — and by extension his concept of self-ownership — has been challenged on multiple grounds: that it legitimates colonial appropriation (Tully, 1980), that it conflates labor with ownership (Nozick's challenge in *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*), and that "self-ownership" is a metaphor rather than a rigorous philosophical concept (Cohen, 1995). CED-AH should not present Lockean self-ownership as settled philosophical ground.
- **Historical context of exclusion.** Locke's universalist language ("every Man") coexisted with his personal involvement in colonial enterprises and the slave trade, and his political philosophy was used to justify the dispossession of indigenous peoples. CED-AH should acknowledge this tension when drawing on Locke's framework for universalist claims about human authority and dignity.
- **No consideration of non-human agents.** Locke's political philosophy addresses only human agents. It provides no framework for evaluating the status of entities that process information, exhibit complex behavior, but are not human — precisely the entities CED-AH must address. The extension from Lockean political theory to human-AI relations requires philosophical work that Locke did not and could not perform.
