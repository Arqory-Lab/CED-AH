## Summary

*De Anima* (On the Soul) is Aristotle's systematic investigation into the nature of soul (psuchē), mind, life, perception, and thought. It is the foundational text of philosophical psychology in the Western tradition and the origin of debates about the relationship between mind and body that continue to structure philosophy of mind, cognitive science, and — crucially for CED-AH — the question of whether computational systems can have mental states.

Aristotle's central thesis is **hylomorphism** — the doctrine that soul is related to body as form (eidos, morphē) is related to matter (hulē). The soul is not a separate substance inhabiting the body (as Plato's dualism suggests) nor is it identical with the body (as materialism holds). Rather, the soul is the **form of a natural body that has life potentially** (412a19–21). To have a soul is to be organized in such a way as to be capable of the activities characteristic of a living thing. The soul is what *makes* a body a living body — its organizing principle, its functional structure, its actuality.

This has immediate implications for the question of whether computational systems can have souls (mental states, experiential life). For Aristotle, soul is not a stuff or a property that could be instantiated in any substrate — it is the form of a *specific kind of* body, namely a natural body with the potential for life. An axe, Aristotle argues, does not have a soul even though it has a function (cutting) — because the axe's function is not the kind of function that constitutes life (412b10–15). If the eye were an animal, sight would be its soul; but sight cannot exist in just any physical arrangement — it requires the specific biological organization of an eye. Hylomorphism entails that mental states require the specific kind of embodiment that supports them. They cannot be abstracted from their material realization and instantiated in just any substrate.

Aristotle distinguishes a hierarchy of soul-capacities:
- **Nutritive soul** (threptikon): the capacity for nutrition, growth, and reproduction — shared by all living things, including plants.
- **Sensitive soul** (aisthētikon): the capacity for perception, appetite, and locomotion — shared by animals.
- **Rational soul** (logistikon/noētikon): the capacity for thought, deliberation, and understanding — distinctive to humans.

Each higher capacity presupposes the lower ones. A being with a rational soul also has a sensitive soul and a nutritive soul; but not vice versa. This hierarchy means that thought (noēsis) is not a free-standing capacity that could exist independently of sensation, appetite, and biological life. Thought, for Aristotle, grows out of and depends upon the capacities of the living, sensing, embodied organism.

Book III, Chapters 4–5 introduce Aristotle's most difficult and debated doctrine: the **active intellect** (nous poiētikos). Aristotle distinguishes between a passive intellect (which receives intelligible forms, as the senses receive sensible forms) and an active intellect (which makes all things thinkable, "as light makes potential colors actual"). The active intellect is described as "separable, impassible, and unmixed" (430a17–18) — a passage that has generated two millennia of conflicting interpretations. Alexander of Aphrodisias identified the active intellect with God; Averroes argued it was a single universal intellect shared by all humans; Aquinas argued it was an individual human faculty; modern interpreters (Shields, 2016; Caston, 1999) offer naturalistic readings that avoid theological commitments.

For CED-AH's purposes, the crucial insight is not the contested active intellect doctrine but the broader hylomorphic thesis: **mental states are not substrate-independent**. They are the form of a particular kind of body. This directly bears on the question of whether computational systems — which are realized in silicon, copper, and electrical impulses rather than in biological tissue — can have mental states in the relevant sense. Aristotle's framework suggests they cannot: not because silicon is the wrong *material* (hylomorphism is not crude materialism) but because computational systems lack the kind of organization (the form of a natural body with potential for life) that constitutes mentality.

Aristotle's treatment of **phantasia** (imagination, representation) in Book III, Chapter 3 is also relevant. Phantasia is the capacity to form and manipulate mental images — a capacity that mediates between perception and thought. Aristotle argues that phantasia can produce both true and false representations, and that phantasmata (mental images) are necessary for thought: "the soul never thinks without a phantasm" (431a16–17). This suggests that genuine thought requires not just information processing but the formation and manipulation of experiential representations — a capacity that depends on having the kind of embodied, sensing life that generates experience in the first place.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's claim that computational systems lack phenomenological experience and therefore lack genuine agency. Aristotle's hylomorphism — soul as the form of a natural body with potential for life — implies that mental states cannot be realized in just any physical substrate. They require the specific kind of embodiment that constitutes a living organism. Computational systems, however complex their information processing, lack this embodiment. They do not have nutritive souls (they do not grow, metabolize, or reproduce), sensitive souls (they do not perceive in the phenomenological sense — there is nothing it is like to be a processor computing a function), or rational souls (they do not think, in Aristotle's sense, because thought requires phantasmata generated by embodied perception). CED-AH's agency test — which asks whether a system has phenomenological experience, self-directed purposes, and genuine understanding — is grounded in the same insight Aristotle articulated: that mentality is not a function that can be abstracted from the living body and instantiated in any mechanism.

**Provides the historical foundation** for modern philosophical positions that CED-AH relies on. Aristotle's hylomorphism is the direct ancestor of Searle's biological naturalism (phil-031) — the thesis that mental states are caused by neurobiological processes and cannot be realized in non-biological substrates. It is also the ancestor of the enactivist and embodied cognition traditions (Varela, Thompson, & Rosch, 1991; Thompson, 2007) that argue genuine cognition requires embodied, situated, living engagement with the world. CED-AH's claim that computational systems are instruments rather than agents draws, whether directly or through its modern descendants, on the Aristotelian insight that mind is not computation but the form of a specific kind of living body.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's distinction between output and understanding. Aristotle's account of phantasia — the capacity to form experiential representations that mediate between perception and thought — implies that genuine thought requires more than the manipulation of symbols or the processing of information. It requires the formation of experiential representations grounded in lived, embodied perception. A computational system can process symbols that *represent* (to a human interpreter) the same content as a phantasma, but it does not *form phantasmata* because it does not perceive, does not experience, and does not live. The system's output may be functionally equivalent to the product of thought without being an instance of thought — precisely the distinction CED-AH draws between computational output and human understanding.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by introducing complexity about the nature of mind that simple "instruments not agents" language may obscure. Aristotle's psychology is far more nuanced than a binary instrument/agent distinction suggests. The hylomorphic account recognizes degrees of soul (nutritive, sensitive, rational) and locates mentality on a continuum of increasing complexity rooted in biological organization. CED-AH's binary — instruments or agents, nothing in between — may need to engage with this Aristotelian nuance. A computational system is not an agent, but neither is it straightforwardly an instrument in the way a hammer is; it exhibits complex behaviors that invite (but do not justify) the attribution of mental states. CED-AH's framework would benefit from acknowledging this intermediate phenomenology — the appearance of mentality without the reality — and explaining why appearance does not constitute reality.

**Complicates** CED-AH by raising the active intellect problem. If Aristotle's active intellect is "separable, impassible, and unmixed" — if it can exist apart from the body — then at least one component of mind is, on Aristotle's own account, substrate-independent. Functionalists and AI proponents could argue that this Aristotelian aperture licenses the possibility of disembodied thought — including computational thought. CED-AH should either argue that the active intellect passage does not support this reading (as many modern interpreters contend) or acknowledge that Aristotle's own framework contains a tension that CED-AH resolves in favor of embodiment.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** *De Anima* is the foundational text of philosophical psychology and its hylomorphic thesis has profoundly shaped subsequent debates about the relationship between mind and body. The work's influence on philosophy of mind — from Aquinas through Brentano to contemporary embodied cognition and enactivism — is immense. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) the historical distance is vast, and Aristotle's psychology presupposes a metaphysical framework (hylomorphism, teleology, the four causes) that is not universally accepted; (2) the active intellect doctrine introduces a complication that cuts against CED-AH's position unless carefully managed; (3) modern philosophy of mind has moved well beyond Aristotle's specific claims while retaining some of his structural insights; and (4) the connection between hylomorphism and the claim that computational systems lack mental states requires interpretive work that goes beyond what Aristotle himself argued.

## Limitations

- **Hylomorphism is contested.** Aristotle's form/matter metaphysics is not universally accepted in contemporary philosophy. Functionalists (Putnam, 1967; early Putnam) argue that mental states are defined by their functional roles, not by their substrate — a position directly opposed to hylomorphism. CED-AH's reliance on the hylomorphic insight (mind requires specific embodiment) must contend with functionalist objections, not merely cite Aristotle as authority.
- **The active intellect problem.** The passage on the active intellect (Book III, Chapter 5) is one of the most contested passages in the history of philosophy. If the active intellect is separable from the body, Aristotle's own framework admits substrate-independent mental states — which would undermine the use of hylomorphism against computational mentality. CED-AH must engage with this interpretive question.
- **Aristotle did not have the concept of computation.** Aristotle's analysis of soul, perception, and thought was developed without any awareness of computational processes, information theory, or artificial intelligence. Applying his framework to questions about computational mentality requires an extrapolation that Aristotle himself did not and could not make. The extrapolation may be philosophically warranted but it is not Aristotle's.
- **Modern neuroscience complicates the picture.** Contemporary understanding of the brain, neural computation, and the relationship between biological processes and mental states is vastly more detailed than anything Aristotle had access to. While hylomorphism resonates with some neuroscientific views (embodied cognition, ecological psychology), it does not map straightforwardly onto contemporary neuroscience's framework.
- **The hierarchy of souls is biologically naive.** Aristotle's distinction between nutritive, sensitive, and rational souls reflects ancient biological classification, not modern taxonomy. The sharp distinction between plant, animal, and human capacities does not survive modern evolutionary biology's picture of gradual, continuous variation in cognitive capacities across species.
- **"Nothing it is like to be" is not Aristotle's language.** The Nagelian concept of phenomenal consciousness — "what it is like" to be a bat or a human — is a 20th-century construction (Nagel, 1974). Attributing this concept to Aristotle is an interpretive projection. Aristotle has resources for the point (his account of perception as the reception of form without matter implies experiential awareness) but he does not state it in the terms CED-AH's argument employs.
