## Summary

Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was a former slave who became one of the most influential Stoic philosophers. His *Discourses* (recorded by his student Arrian of Nicomedia) and the condensed *Enchiridion* (Handbook) constitute the most systematic surviving statement of later Stoic ethics — and contain the oldest formal philosophical account of human agency as a domain with inviolable boundaries.

The **Enchiridion** opens with what may be the most consequential sentence in Stoic philosophy:

> "Some things are within our power [eph' hēmin], while others are not. Within our power are opinion [hupolēpsis], motivation [hormē], desire [orexis], aversion [ekklisis], and, in a word, whatever is our own doing [hosa hēmetera erga]. Not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not our own doing [hosa ouch hēmetera erga]." (Enchiridion §1, Hard trans.)

This distinction — between what is **eph' hēmin** (up to us, within our power, within our control) and what is **ouk eph' hēmin** (not up to us, outside our power, beyond our control) — is the foundational principle of Epictetus's entire philosophical system. It is also the oldest systematic delineation of the domain of human agency.

For Epictetus, what is "up to us" is exclusively our faculty of rational choice — **prohairesis**. Prohairesis is the capacity to assent to or withhold assent from impressions (phantasiai), to desire or avoid on the basis of rational evaluation, and to act on the basis of reflective judgment rather than unreflective impulse. It is, in Epictetus's view, the one thing that cannot be taken away by any external force — not by tyrants, not by illness, not by death. A slave's body can be chained, but his prohairesis remains free (Discourses I.1.23–25). An emperor can confiscate property and exile a citizen, but he cannot compel the citizen to assent to a false proposition or desire what is genuinely harmful (Discourses I.2.12–18).

The practical consequence is a radical ethics of attention. Because prohairesis is the one domain of genuine human agency, the wise person (the Stoic sage) directs all effort toward what is within prohairetic control — judgments, values, desires, choices — and maintains equanimity toward what falls outside it. This is not passivity or indifference; it is precision about where human effort is effective and where it is wasted. The sage acts vigorously in the domain of prohairesis (making correct judgments, choosing wisely, acting virtuously) and accepts with equanimity the outcomes that fall outside that domain.

*Discourses* I.1 develops this framework at length. Epictetus argues that the faculty of prohairesis is given by nature (or by God/Zeus, in the Stoic theological framework) as the one thing that is entirely self-governing. Every other possession — health, wealth, relationships, social status — is subject to external interference. Only the capacity for rational choice is genuinely one's own. To identify with anything other than prohairesis — to place one's sense of self in one's body, possessions, or reputation — is to become a slave to fortune, vulnerable to distress whenever external circumstances change. To identify with prohairesis alone is to achieve the only freedom available to a mortal being: freedom of judgment.

Epictetus also develops a sophisticated account of the relationship between **impressions** (phantasiai) and **assent** (sunkatathesis). The external world produces impressions — perceptions, appearances, stimulus events. But the impression does not compel assent; the agent retains the power to evaluate the impression, to judge whether it accurately represents reality, and to choose whether to act on it. "It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things" (Enchiridion §5). The power to evaluate impressions and withhold impulsive assent is the core of human agency for the Stoics — and it is precisely this power that Epictetus argues can never be legitimately externalized.

In *Discourses* III.3, Epictetus describes the "material of the good person" as one's own impressions and the training required to handle them correctly. The good person is not the one who has the most resources, the best outcomes, or the highest status — but the one who exercises prohairesis with excellence: judging correctly, choosing wisely, acting coherently. This is a vision of human excellence — of flourishing — that is located entirely in the exercise of agency, not in its products.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's "Authority Retention" imperative. Epictetus's distinction between what is eph' hēmin and what is not is the oldest philosophical formulation of the principle that CED-AH enshrines as its first design imperative: "Humans make all decisions; AH supports but never decides." For Epictetus, the faculty of rational choice (prohairesis) is the one domain of genuine human agency — the one thing that is entirely the person's own and cannot be legitimately transferred, seized, or abdicated. CED-AH's claim that human decision-making authority is "non-negotiable" echoes Epictetus's insistence that prohairesis is the one inviolable human possession. To delegate decisions to a computational system is, in Stoic terms, to place one's agency in something that is not eph' hēmin — to hand over the one thing that is genuinely one's own to something that is, by nature, external.

**Provides the philosophical foundation** for CED-AH's claim that agency cannot be externalized without loss. Epictetus's entire ethical system is built on the insight that confusing what is "up to us" with what is not leads to suffering, confusion, and moral failure. When humans identify with things outside their rational control — possessions, reputation, outcomes — they become dependent on forces they cannot govern. When they identify with prohairesis alone, they achieve the only stable form of well-being. CED-AH's concern about dependency — "declining performance when system unavailable" as a failure signal — is structurally identical to Epictetus's concern about misplaced identification: a user who has delegated judgment to a computational system has placed their agency in something external, and when that external support is removed, their agency collapses.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's distinction between amplification and replacement. In Epictetus's framework, a tool that helps the sage exercise prohairesis more effectively — that presents information more clearly, that surfaces relevant considerations, that supports better judgment — would amplify human agency. A tool that bypasses prohairesis — that makes decisions on the sage's behalf, that acts without the sage's reflective assent — would replace human agency. The distinction maps precisely onto CED-AH's amplification/replacement distinction. An AI system that presents options and supports deliberation amplifies. An AI system that recommends and acts without genuine human deliberation replaces. Same tool, different relationship, opposite outcomes for human agency.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by raising the Stoic determinism complication. The Stoics, including Epictetus, were committed determinists — they believed that all events, including human choices, are determined by the rational order of the cosmos (logos, fate, providence). This raises a tension: if human choices are determined, in what sense are they genuinely "up to us"? The Stoic response (which is complex and debated: see Bobzien, 1998; Frede, 2011) involves a compatibilist account of freedom — the view that freedom consists not in the absence of causal determination but in acting from one's own rational nature rather than from external compulsion. CED-AH does not need to adopt Stoic determinism, but it should be aware that the Stoic account of agency it draws on was developed within a deterministic framework, and that the meaning of "up to us" in that framework is more nuanced than a simple libertarian free will.

**Complicates** CED-AH by raising the question of scope. Epictetus's domain of what is "up to us" is radically narrow — only judgments, desires, and choices, not their outcomes. If CED-AH's "Authority Retention" imperative means that humans retain authority over judgments and choices (the Epictetean domain), this is defensible. If it means that humans retain authority over outcomes — that human-made decisions must determine what actually happens in the world — this goes beyond what Epictetus's framework supports. CED-AH should clarify whether Authority Retention refers to the authority of decision (which Epictetus supports) or the authority of outcome (which Epictetus would regard as a confusion of what is and is not eph' hēmin).

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Epictetus's account of prohairesis is the most systematic ancient treatment of human agency as a bounded domain with inviolable properties, and it directly addresses the philosophical question at the heart of CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative: what belongs to the human agent and what can be legitimately externalized? The *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* have survived 1,900 years of philosophical scrutiny and remain the primary texts for understanding the Stoic account of moral agency. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) the Stoic framework presupposes a cosmological theology (divine logos ordering the universe) that CED-AH does not share; (2) Stoic determinism complicates the concept of agency in ways that require careful handling; (3) the historical distance is substantial, and Epictetus's examples (slavery, tyranny, exile) are not directly analogous to the human-AI relationship; and (4) the application of prohairesis to the specific question of computational decision-making requires interpretive extension.

## Limitations

- **Stoic determinism is in tension with the agency concept CED-AH needs.** The Stoics held that all events are determined by fate/providence. Whether prohairesis is genuinely "free" in a sense that supports CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative is a philosophically contested question. CED-AH may need a stronger (libertarian or robustly compatibilist) account of agency than the Stoics provide.
- **The eph' hēmin/ouk eph' hēmin distinction may be too sharp.** Epictetus draws a binary between what is and what is not within our control. Many decisions in the real world — especially organizational, political, and technical decisions — involve domains of partial control, shared authority, and distributed agency. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative operates in these messy intermediate domains, not in the clean binary that Epictetus describes.
- **Epictetus's ethics were developed for individuals, not for systems.** The *Discourses* address the individual moral agent seeking personal virtue and equanimity. CED-AH addresses the design of sociotechnical systems that mediate between humans and computational processes. The extension from individual virtue ethics to system design principles requires philosophical argument that Epictetus does not provide.
- **The Stoic sage is an ideal, not a description.** Epictetus's account of prohairesis describes the capacities of the ideally rational agent — the Stoic sage. Most humans, by Stoic admission, fall far short of this ideal. CED-AH must design for actual humans with limited rationality, attentional capacity, and self-governance — not for Stoic sages. The Authority Retention imperative protects human agency as it actually is, not as the Stoics imagine it could be.
- **Historical and social context.** Epictetus was a former slave writing in the Roman Empire. His emphasis on the inviolability of prohairesis can be read as a philosophy of consolation for the powerless rather than a normative theory of how authority should be distributed. CED-AH should not reduce the Stoic tradition to a management philosophy but should engage with its original social context.
- **Stoic cosmology.** The Stoic account of prohairesis is embedded in a broader cosmological and theological framework — the doctrine of logos as the rational principle governing the cosmos, the belief in divine providence, the identification of virtue with living "according to nature." CED-AH does not share these commitments. Extracting the agency concept from the cosmological framework requires demonstrating that the concept is independently defensible.
