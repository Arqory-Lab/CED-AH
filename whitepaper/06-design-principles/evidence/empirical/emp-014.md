## Summary

Anders Ericsson — the world's leading researcher on expertise and its development — synthesizes three decades of research on what he calls "deliberate practice" — the specific form of training that produces expert performance. *Peak* is Ericsson's most accessible presentation of his research program, written with journalist Robert Pool for a general audience.

Ericsson's central findings challenge popular assumptions about expertise: (1) Expert performance is not primarily a product of innate talent — it is the result of extensive, structured practice. (2) Not all practice produces expertise — only "deliberate practice" does. (3) Deliberate practice has specific characteristics: it involves well-defined goals, focused attention, immediate feedback, and progressive challenge at the edge of current capability. (4) The key psychological mechanism is the development of increasingly sophisticated "mental representations" — internal models that enable experts to perceive, remember, reason about, and act within their domain with remarkable speed and accuracy.

Ericsson's concept of "mental representations" connects directly to de Groot's findings (emp-013): the chess grandmaster's ability to perceive positions as meaningful patterns is a mental representation built through years of deliberate practice. These representations are not innate — they are constructed through active engagement with the domain.

A crucial implication: if expertise is built through active engagement, then any system that removes active engagement removes the mechanism through which expertise develops. Automated systems that perform tasks for humans prevent the deliberate practice that builds expertise. This is not an incidental side effect — it is a direct consequence of the replacement relationship.

Conversely, systems that provide well-defined goals, focused challenges, immediate feedback, and progressive difficulty — the components of deliberate practice — would actively support expertise development. This is what CED-AH means by amplification.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth) with the most rigorous empirical framework available. Ericsson's research provides the scientific foundation for CED-AH's requirement that "users become more capable over time, not dependent." Capability Growth, in Ericsson's terms, means designing systems that support deliberate practice: providing appropriate challenges, immediate feedback, and scaffolding that helps users develop increasingly sophisticated mental representations.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's failure mode analysis (capability atrophy). If expertise requires deliberate practice, and deliberate practice requires active engagement, then systems that replace active engagement with passive consumption directly prevent expertise development. The "degeneration effect" Carr documents (emp-003) is, in Ericsson's framework, the predictable consequence of removing the conditions for deliberate practice.

**Provides necessary context** for operationalizing the Capability Growth imperative. Ericsson's research specifies what "becoming more capable" requires: not just more practice, but specific kinds of practice with specific characteristics. CED-AH's measurement framework (the Coherence Dashboard) should track not just whether users are "using" the system but whether their interaction has the characteristics of deliberate practice.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by identifying the conditions under which amplification works. Not all human-system interaction supports capability growth — only interaction that incorporates the elements of deliberate practice does. A system could be designed for "amplification" in name while failing to provide the structured challenge, feedback, and progression that Ericsson's research shows is necessary.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Ericsson is the most cited researcher on expertise development, and his deliberate practice framework has been validated across dozens of domains (music, medicine, sports, chess, mathematics). The research program spans three decades and has survived extensive peer scrutiny. The connection to CED-AH's Capability Growth imperative is direct and substantial: Ericsson provides the empirical specification of what "becoming more capable" requires.

## Limitations

- **Popular book, not primary research.** *Peak* is written for a general audience. The underlying research is published in peer-reviewed journals (Ericsson, Krampe, & Tesch-Romer 1993 is the foundational paper). CED-AH should cite the primary research alongside this accessible synthesis.
- **Deliberate practice may not apply to all domains equally.** The strongest evidence comes from well-structured domains with clear performance criteria (music, chess, sports). Whether deliberate practice operates the same way in less structured domains (leadership, creativity, ethical judgment) is debated.
- **The "10,000 hours" popularization is misleading.** Ericsson himself has criticized the popular simplification of his research into "10,000 hours of practice makes you an expert." The quality and structure of practice matter more than the quantity. CED-AH should avoid this oversimplification.
- **Individual focus.** Ericsson's research addresses individual expertise development. CED-AH's concern extends to collective and organizational capability, which may require different frameworks.
- **Does not address AI-assisted practice.** Whether AI systems can effectively scaffold deliberate practice is an empirical question Ericsson's research does not directly address. CED-AH must make this case independently.
