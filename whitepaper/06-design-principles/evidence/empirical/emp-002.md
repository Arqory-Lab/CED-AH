## Summary

Charles Billings' *Aviation Automation* is the most systematic attempt to derive design principles for human-centered automation from the accumulated experience of the aviation industry — a domain that has been grappling with the human-automation relationship longer and more consequentially than any other. Billings was a senior researcher at NASA Ames Research Center, and the book synthesizes decades of aviation accident investigation, cockpit design research, and regulatory analysis into a coherent design philosophy.

Billings begins with the recognition that aviation automation has produced paradoxical outcomes: modern aircraft are extraordinarily safe overall, but automation has introduced new categories of failure — mode confusion (the pilot does not know what the automation is doing), automation surprises (the system behaves in ways the pilot did not expect), and skill degradation (pilots lose manual flying skills they rarely practice). These are Bainbridge's ironies made concrete in a high-stakes domain.

The book's central contribution is its framework for "human-centered automation" — a set of design principles that keep the human pilot in meaningful control while leveraging automation's capabilities. Billings proposes that automation should: (1) be predictable and comprehensible to the human operator, (2) keep the human informed about system state and intentions, (3) be directable — the human can modify, override, or disengage the automation at any time, (4) support the human's situational awareness rather than replacing it, and (5) fail gracefully, with clear indications and smooth transitions to manual control.

Billings draws a crucial distinction between *management by consent* (the pilot approves what the automation proposes) and *management by exception* (the automation acts unless the pilot intervenes). He argues that management by exception — the default in most modern automation — is fundamentally problematic because it inverts the authority relationship: the system acts, and the human must detect and correct errors rather than directing action. Management by consent preserves human authority but requires different design approaches.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention). Billings' distinction between management by consent and management by exception maps directly onto CED-AH's requirement that "humans make all decisions; AH supports but never decides." Management by consent is the operational implementation of Authority Retention; management by exception is its violation. Billings provides the domain-specific evidence that authority retention is not just a philosophical commitment but an engineering requirement for safe operation.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 3 (Transparency). Billings' requirements for predictability, comprehensibility, and state indication are aviation-specific instantiations of CED-AH's requirement that "reasoning is explicable; limitations are disclosed." The failure signal — "users cannot explain basis of system outputs" — is precisely what Billings identifies as the precursor to mode confusion and automation surprises.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 5 (Reversibility). Billings' requirement that automation be "directable" — modifiable, overridable, and disengageable — is an aviation-specific statement of CED-AH's requirement that "every action can be undone; humans can always override."

**Provides the closest existing precedent** for CED-AH's design approach. Billings' human-centered automation framework is, in many ways, what CED-AH would look like if developed by an aviation engineer rather than a philosopher. The key difference is grounding: Billings derives his principles from aviation experience and accident analysis; CED-AH derives its imperatives from coherence as a first principle. CED-AH's contribution is to show that Billings' domain-specific insights follow from a universal philosophical foundation — they are not just "what works in aviation" but instances of a general principle about human-system relationships.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Billings' framework is grounded in decades of aviation accident investigation and operational experience in the most safety-critical automation domain in existence. Aviation's safety record — achieved through painful, iterative learning from failures — provides the strongest available empirical evidence about what happens when automation replaces vs. augments human capability. The design principles Billings proposes have been substantially validated by subsequent aviation safety improvements. As a precedent for CED-AH's design imperatives, this source is exceptionally strong.

## Limitations

- **Aviation-specific.** Billings' framework was developed for a specific domain with specific constraints (physical systems, trained professionals, life-safety stakes, regulatory oversight). Not all conclusions transfer directly to knowledge work, consumer AI, or social media algorithms. CED-AH must argue for the generalization rather than assuming it.
- **Published in 1997.** The aviation automation landscape has evolved significantly since publication. Glass cockpits, fly-by-wire systems, and increased automation have continued to advance. More recent work (Endsley 2017, emp-004) updates some of Billings' conclusions.
- **Does not address AI/ML systems.** Billings' automation is deterministic — programmed to follow specified rules. Modern AI systems (neural networks, LLMs) are fundamentally different: their behavior is learned rather than specified, making predictability and comprehensibility harder to achieve. CED-AH's Transparency imperative faces challenges Billings did not anticipate.
- **Management by consent may not scale.** For simple aviation tasks, having the pilot approve every automated action is feasible. For complex AI systems making thousands of micro-decisions per second, management by consent may be impractical. CED-AH must address how Authority Retention works at scale — a challenge Billings did not face.
- **No philosophical grounding.** Billings' framework is empirically derived — it describes what works, not why it works. CED-AH's contribution is to provide the coherence-based explanation for *why* human-centered automation works: it maintains coherence, which Billings' management-by-exception approach does not.
