## Summary

Hoffman et al. provide a concise but influential analysis of the trust relationship between humans and automated systems, arguing that trust calibration — the alignment between a user's trust in an automated system and the system's actual reliability — is one of the most critical and underaddressed challenges in human-automation interaction design.

The authors identify three trust calibration problems:

**Over-trust (complacency).** Users who work with highly reliable automation tend to develop excessive trust, accepting the system's outputs without adequate scrutiny. Over-trust is particularly dangerous because it is self-reinforcing: the more reliable the system, the more the user trusts it; the more the user trusts it, the less they verify its outputs; the less they verify, the more likely they are to miss the rare but consequential failures. This creates what the authors call a "trust debt" — a growing vulnerability that may not be apparent until a catastrophic failure occurs.

**Under-trust (disuse).** Users who have experienced an automation failure — even a minor one — may develop excessive distrust, rejecting the system's recommendations even when they are accurate. Under-trust is wasteful (the system's capabilities go unused) and can itself be dangerous if the human's unaided performance is worse than the system's assisted performance.

**Trust oscillation.** In some contexts, users cycle between over-trust and under-trust — trusting the system completely when it is working, then rejecting it entirely when it fails, then gradually returning to over-trust. This oscillation prevents the development of a calibrated, nuanced understanding of the system's strengths and limitations.

The authors argue that trust calibration is not a user problem to be solved by training; it is a design problem to be solved by system architecture. Systems must be designed to actively support appropriate trust by: communicating their confidence levels, disclosing their limitations, making their reasoning transparent, and providing feedback that helps users develop accurate mental models of system capability.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Hoffman et al.'s analysis of trust calibration is the empirical foundation for CED-AH's requirement that "stated confidence matches actual accuracy" and that systems "never avoid saying 'I don't know.'" The trust calibration problems the authors identify — over-trust, under-trust, oscillation — are precisely the failure modes that the Calibrated Honesty imperative is designed to prevent.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 3 (Transparency). Hoffman et al.'s prescriptions for trust calibration — communicate confidence, disclose limitations, make reasoning transparent — are operational specifications of CED-AH's Transparency requirement. The paper demonstrates that transparency is not merely a philosophical desideratum but an engineering necessity for safe human-automation interaction.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's coherence framework. Trust miscalibration is, in CED-AH's terms, a coherence failure: the user's model of the system (their trust level) is misaligned with the system's actual reliability. This inconsistency — between belief and reality — is precisely the kind of incoherence CED-AH's framework is designed to detect and prevent. The Coherence Dashboard's tracking of user understanding and calibration maps directly onto Hoffman et al.'s trust calibration dimensions.

**Provides necessary context** for understanding why CED-AH requires Calibrated Honesty as a separate design imperative (rather than folding it into Transparency). Trust calibration is a distinct psychological phenomenon with distinct design requirements. A system can be transparent (its reasoning is explicable) without being calibrated (its stated confidence does not match its actual accuracy). CED-AH's separation of these imperatives is supported by the empirical distinction Hoffman et al. document.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** Published in a respected IEEE journal and authored by leading human factors researchers (Robert Hoffman is one of the most prominent scholars in naturalistic decision-making and cognitive systems engineering). The analysis is empirically grounded and practically actionable. The rating is Moderate because the paper is a brief analysis (five pages) rather than a comprehensive study, and its claims, while well-supported, are more synthetic than original. The more comprehensive treatment is Lee and See (emp-008).

---

## Limitations

- **Brief analysis, not comprehensive review.** At five pages, the paper provides a valuable framework but limited empirical detail. The underlying research is documented more fully in Lee and See 2004 (emp-008) and in Hoffman's other publications.
- **Trust is one dimension of a more complex relationship.** CED-AH's framework evaluates systems along five dimensions (understanding, capability, agency, dependency, growth). Trust calibration maps primarily to understanding and dependency. The other dimensions require additional evidence sources.
- **Trust dynamics may differ for AI systems.** Hoffman et al.'s analysis draws primarily on traditional automation (aviation, process control, military systems). Trust dynamics with conversational AI, recommendation algorithms, and generative systems may involve different psychological mechanisms — particularly because these systems can produce plausible-sounding but wrong outputs in ways traditional automation cannot.
- **Cultural and individual variation.** Trust calibration may vary across cultures, age groups, and individual differences in technology experience. The paper does not extensively address this variation.
- **The prescriptions may conflict in practice.** Fully disclosing a system's limitations may lead to under-trust; presenting confidence levels may create false precision. The design challenge is to communicate uncertainty without either inflating or deflating trust — a difficult calibration problem in its own right.
