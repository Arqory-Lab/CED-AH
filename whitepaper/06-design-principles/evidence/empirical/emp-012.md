## Summary

*Engineering Psychology and Human Performance* is the definitive textbook in the field — the standard reference for understanding how humans perceive, attend to, remember, decide, and act in complex technical environments. Now in its fourth edition, the book synthesizes decades of research on human information processing and applies it to the design of systems that humans must operate, monitor, or interact with.

For CED-AH's purposes, the book's contributions fall into three categories:

**Human information processing.** The textbook provides the empirical foundations for understanding what humans are good at and what they are not. Humans excel at pattern recognition, contextual judgment, creative problem-solving, and adapting to novel situations. They are limited in sustained attention, working memory capacity, consistent vigilance, and processing speed. These capabilities and limitations are not bugs to be fixed — they are structural features of human cognition that any system design must account for.

**Attention and workload.** Wickens's own multiple-resource theory of attention is a cornerstone of the field. It demonstrates that human attention is not a single pool but a set of resources distributed across sensory modalities, processing stages, and response types. System design should distribute demands across multiple resources rather than overloading any single channel. This has direct implications for how AH-compliant systems should present information and solicit human input.

**Automation and human performance.** The automation chapters synthesize the research by Bainbridge, Parasuraman, Endsley, and others into a coherent account of how automation affects human performance. The key findings — skill degradation, situational awareness loss, trust miscalibration, complacency — are presented with their empirical evidence and connected to design recommendations.

The textbook is not an argument for or against automation; it is a systematic account of the empirical evidence about how humans and automated systems interact. Its value for CED-AH is as a comprehensive reference for the human performance data that the framework's design imperatives must account for.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides necessary context** for the entire design imperative framework. CED-AH's five imperatives make implicit claims about human cognition: that humans can retain authority (attention and decision-making capacity exist), that human capability can grow (learning and expertise development are real), that transparency is possible (humans can form mental models of system behavior), that calibrated honesty matters (trust is sensitive to accuracy), and that reversibility is important (error recovery is a fundamental human need). Wickens et al. provide the empirical evidence base for these implicit claims.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth). The textbook's treatment of expertise development, skill acquisition, and deliberate practice (connected to emp-014, Ericsson & Pool) demonstrates that human capability is not fixed — it grows through active engagement and degrades through disuse. This is the empirical foundation for CED-AH's requirement that systems support increasing capability over time.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention) by documenting the conditions under which human decision-making is effective. Humans are not optimal decision-makers in all conditions — but they are adaptive, contextually sensitive, and capable of learning from experience in ways automated systems are not. CED-AH's insistence on human authority is supported by the evidence that human judgment, while imperfect, brings capabilities (flexibility, contextual sensitivity, ethical reasoning) that automated systems lack.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by documenting genuine human limitations. Wickens et al. provide extensive evidence of human cognitive limitations: inattentional blindness, confirmation bias, working memory constraints, fatigue effects. CED-AH must account for these limitations rather than idealizing human capability. The framework's response — amplification, not replacement — is precisely designed to address human limitations by augmenting them rather than substituting for them.

## Limitations

- **Textbook genre.** The treatment of any individual topic is necessarily less detailed than the primary research papers. For specific claims, the underlying studies should be consulted.
- **Focus on human performance, not human flourishing.** Wickens et al. address human performance in technical systems — speed, accuracy, error rates, workload — not the broader dimensions of human flourishing (meaning, agency, growth) that CED-AH's coherence criterion encompasses. Performance is necessary but not sufficient for CED-AH's evaluative purposes.
- **Engineering orientation.** The textbook is written for engineers designing human-machine systems. It does not address the philosophical questions CED-AH raises about the nature of the human-technology relationship, the ontological status of computational systems, or the normative foundations of design.
- **Traditional automation focus.** While the fourth edition includes some discussion of advanced automation, the primary evidence base is from aviation, process control, and military domains. The extension to AI-assisted knowledge work, creative tasks, and social interaction requires additional evidence.
- **Does not address societal-level effects.** The textbook focuses on individual human-system interaction. CED-AH's concerns about collective capability degradation, epistemic pollution, and societal coherence are not addressed.
