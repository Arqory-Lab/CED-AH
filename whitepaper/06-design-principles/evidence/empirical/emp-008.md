## Summary

Lee and See's 2004 paper is the definitive review and theoretical framework for understanding trust in automated systems. With over 3,000 citations, it is one of the most influential papers in human-automation interaction research. The authors synthesize several decades of trust research into a comprehensive model that explains how trust forms, evolves, and — critically — how it can be appropriately calibrated through design.

The paper's central contribution is a three-dimensional model of trust:

**Performance-based trust** derives from the system's past reliability — does it do what it's supposed to do? This is the most studied dimension and the most intuitive: systems that work reliably earn trust; systems that fail lose it. But Lee and See show that the relationship is not linear: a single catastrophic failure can destroy trust that took years of reliable performance to build.

**Process-based trust** derives from the user's understanding of *how* the system works — the algorithms, rules, or mechanisms underlying its behavior. When users understand the system's process, they can form more accurate predictions about when it will succeed and when it might fail. Process-based trust supports what Lee and See call "analytical trust" — trust grounded in understanding rather than mere experience.

**Purpose-based trust** derives from the user's perception of *why* the system was designed — whose interests it serves, what goals it pursues. Users trust systems they believe are designed to serve their interests; they distrust systems they believe serve the designer's interests at the user's expense. Purpose-based trust is particularly relevant for AI systems deployed by commercial entities with financial incentives that may conflict with user wellbeing.

Lee and See's framework emphasizes that *appropriate reliance* — not maximum trust — should be the design goal. The optimal state is not a user who trusts the system completely but a user whose trust is *calibrated*: high confidence in the system's strengths, appropriate skepticism about its limitations, and the capacity to adjust reliance based on context. Over-trust leads to complacency and misuse; under-trust leads to disuse and waste.

The paper identifies specific design strategies for supporting appropriate reliance: transparent system operation, clear communication of uncertainty, meaningful feedback about system performance, and user training that develops accurate mental models.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 4 (Calibrated Honesty). Lee and See's entire framework is organized around the goal CED-AH formalizes as Calibrated Honesty: the alignment between a system's stated confidence and its actual accuracy, and between a user's trust and the system's actual reliability. The paper's three-dimensional trust model provides the empirical foundation for understanding *why* calibrated honesty matters and *how* it can be achieved.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 3 (Transparency). Lee and See's "process-based trust" — trust grounded in understanding the system's mechanisms — directly maps to CED-AH's requirement that "reasoning is explicable; limitations are disclosed." The paper demonstrates that transparency is not merely ethically desirable but psychologically necessary for appropriate trust calibration.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention). Lee and See's concept of "appropriate reliance" is structurally identical to CED-AH's requirement that humans retain decision-making authority. "Appropriate reliance" means the human *decides* how much to rely on the system in each context, rather than the system's behavior determining the human's role. This is authority retention in operation: the human evaluates the system's recommendation, considers its reliability in this context, and makes the decision — rather than defaulting to the system's output.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by identifying the complexity of trust dynamics. Lee and See demonstrate that trust is not a simple on/off variable but a multidimensional, dynamic process influenced by performance history, process understanding, and purpose attribution. CED-AH's Calibrated Honesty imperative must account for this complexity — a system cannot achieve calibrated honesty simply by stating a confidence percentage. It must support the user in developing the nuanced understanding that underlies appropriate reliance.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's purpose-based critique. Lee and See's "purpose-based trust" dimension has profound implications for CED-AH's analysis of commercial AI systems. When users perceive — correctly — that a system is designed to maximize engagement, advertising revenue, or data extraction rather than user flourishing, purpose-based trust erodes. CED-AH's coherence framework provides the principled criterion for evaluating system purpose: does it increase human flourishing, or does it serve other interests at the expense of flourishing?

## Limitations

- **Trust model predates modern AI.** The framework was developed primarily from research on traditional automation (aviation, process control, manufacturing). Conversational AI, generative models, and recommendation algorithms present novel trust challenges — particularly the "fluency illusion" (systems that produce confident-sounding but unreliable outputs) — that the model may not fully capture.
- **Individual and cultural variation.** Trust formation and calibration vary across individuals, cultures, and contexts. The general model may not predict specific trust dynamics for specific populations.
- **"Appropriate reliance" is hard to operationalize.** Lee and See describe appropriate reliance as the design goal but acknowledge that defining "appropriate" requires knowing the system's true reliability in each context — which may be unknown or unknowable. CED-AH faces the same operationalization challenge with "calibrated honesty."
- **Does not address trust in AI-generated content.** The paper addresses trust in automated actions and recommendations. Trust in AI-generated text, images, and analysis — where the output may be indistinguishable from human-produced content — presents distinct challenges that Lee and See do not address.
- **The model is descriptive, not prescriptive.** Lee and See describe how trust works but provide only general design guidance. CED-AH's design imperatives are more specific, but they must be validated against Lee and See's empirical framework to ensure they actually produce the trust calibration they intend.
