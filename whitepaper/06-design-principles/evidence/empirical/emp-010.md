**Access:** IEEE via DOI. Available through institutional library access. One of the most cited frameworks in human-automation interaction research.

## Summary

Parasuraman, Sheridan, and Wickens present the most influential framework for analyzing and designing levels of automation (LOA). The paper proposes a two-dimensional model that classifies automation along two axes: the *type* of function being automated and the *level* of automation applied to that function.

**Types of functions** are organized along the stages of human information processing: (1) information acquisition (sensing and filtering), (2) information analysis (integrating, interpreting, and predicting), (3) decision and action selection (choosing among alternatives), and (4) action implementation (executing the chosen action). Each stage can be automated independently.

**Levels of automation** range from 1 (fully manual — the human does everything) through 10 (fully autonomous — the system does everything, ignoring the human). Intermediate levels include: the system offers alternatives (level 3), the system suggests one alternative (level 5), the system executes automatically but informs the human (level 7), and the system executes automatically and informs the human only if asked (level 9).

The framework's key insight is that automation is not a binary choice (automated vs. manual) but a design space with many possible configurations. Different functions may be automated at different levels within the same system. The optimal configuration depends on the specific domain, task, and human-system interaction requirements.

Parasuraman et al. make specific recommendations: automation of *information acquisition and analysis* (stages 1-2) is generally less problematic than automation of *decision-making and action* (stages 3-4). Automating information processing supports the human's understanding; automating decision-making replaces it. The highest levels of automation (9-10) at the decision and action stages are most likely to produce the misuse, disuse, and out-of-the-loop problems documented in the broader literature.

The paper explicitly connects LOA to human performance consequences: higher levels of automation at the decision and action stages lead to lower situational awareness, poorer manual performance when automation fails, and more severe trust miscalibration.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's amplification/replacement distinction with a granular analytical framework. Parasuraman et al.'s model provides the empirical basis for CED-AH's claim that the design choice matters more than the technology. Their finding that information-stage automation (supporting understanding) is less problematic than decision-stage automation (replacing judgment) maps precisely onto CED-AH's distinction: amplification automates at the information stages, replacement automates at the decision stages.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's position by demonstrating that "automation" is not monolithic. CED-AH's binary distinction between amplification and replacement is philosophically clean but may be too simple for design practice. Parasuraman et al.'s LOA framework shows that real systems involve *combinations* of automation types and levels. CED-AH's design imperatives should be operationalized with reference to this granularity: Authority Retention does not mean no automation — it means keeping automation at the information stages while retaining human authority at the decision stages.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 1 (Authority Retention). The framework's finding that decision-stage automation (levels 7-10) produces the worst human performance outcomes provides the empirical justification for CED-AH's requirement that "humans make all decisions." Authority Retention, in LOA terms, means keeping the decision and action stages at low automation levels while allowing high automation at the information stages.

**Provides necessary context** for CED-AH's integration architecture. When CED-AH prescribes how systems should be designed, it must be specific about *which functions* are automated and *to what degree*. Parasuraman et al.'s framework provides the vocabulary and dimensional structure for this specification.

**Complicates** CED-AH's absolute prohibition on decision-stage automation. Parasuraman et al. acknowledge that moderate levels of decision-support automation (levels 3-5: offering alternatives, suggesting options) can improve human performance. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative must distinguish between decision *support* (offering alternatives — compatible with authority retention) and decision *replacement* (executing automatically — incompatible).

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Published in a leading IEEE journal, authored by three of the most prominent researchers in human factors (Parasuraman, Sheridan, and Wickens are among the most cited scholars in the field), and cited over 4,000 times. The LOA framework is the standard analytical tool for human-automation interaction design and has been validated across dozens of studies in multiple domains. The framework's relevance to CED-AH is direct and substantial: it provides the empirical vocabulary for translating CED-AH's philosophical principles into specific design recommendations.

## Limitations

- **The 10-level scale may be overly granular for practical design.** While useful for research, designers often find it difficult to map specific system configurations to precise LOA levels. CED-AH's simpler framework (amplify or replace) may be more actionable, even if less precise.
- **The model is static.** Parasuraman et al. describe automation levels as fixed design choices. In practice, the optimal level may vary dynamically based on context, workload, and user capability. CED-AH's framework, with its emphasis on trajectory over time, may need to accommodate dynamic automation levels.
- **Does not account for AI's emergent capabilities.** The LOA model assumes automation that operates within well-defined functional boundaries. Modern AI systems (especially LLMs) blur the stages: a single system may simultaneously acquire information, analyze it, suggest decisions, and (if given tools) take actions. The LOA framework may need extension to accommodate these blurred boundaries.
- **Focuses on individual operator-system interaction.** The framework addresses one human working with one automated system. CED-AH must address more complex configurations: multiple humans, multiple AI systems, organizational contexts, and societal-level effects.
- **No philosophical grounding.** The framework is empirical and pragmatic. It describes what works and what fails but does not explain *why* at a principled level. CED-AH's coherence criterion provides the theoretical explanation that the LOA framework lacks.
