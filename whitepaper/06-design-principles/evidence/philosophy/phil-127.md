## Summary

The *Federalist Papers* are a collection of 85 essays written by Alexander Hamilton (1755/57–1804), James Madison (1751–1836), and John Jay (1745–1829) to persuade the citizens of New York to ratify the proposed Constitution of the United States. They constitute the most influential single work of American political theory and the most detailed surviving record of the philosophical reasoning behind the design of a constitutional republic. For CED-AH, the *Federalist Papers* represent something rare and valuable: a body of political philosophy written not by abstract theorists but by practical system designers — men who were simultaneously articulating philosophical principles and building the institutional architecture to implement them.

**Federalist No. 10: Factions.** Madison's Federalist No. 10 addresses the problem of "faction" — groups of citizens united by a common passion or interest that is adverse to the rights of other citizens or to the common good. Madison defines faction broadly: it includes any group driven by shared interest that could dominate others if unchecked. The causes of faction, Madison argues, are "sown in the nature of man" — they arise from the diversity of human opinions, passions, and interests, and cannot be eliminated without destroying liberty itself.

Since the causes of faction cannot be removed, the remedy must address its *effects*. Madison argues that a large, extended republic with representative government is the best structural remedy: the diversity of interests in a large republic makes it difficult for any single faction to form a majority, and the mechanism of representation filters popular passions through a deliberative body. The solution is not to eliminate self-interest (which is impossible) but to design institutions that channel self-interest toward the common good.

This analysis maps directly onto CED-AH's design philosophy. CED-AH does not assume that developers, deployers, or users of computational systems will always act in the interest of human flourishing — it assumes that all actors have interests that may conflict with the common good, and it designs structural safeguards (the five imperatives, the measurement framework, the contestability architecture) to channel those interests toward coherence. Madison's insight — that good institutional design accounts for human imperfection rather than assuming human virtue — is the political-philosophical foundation for CED-AH's structural approach to AI governance.

**Federalist No. 51: Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances.** Federalist No. 51 is Madison's most famous essay and contains the most quoted passage in the *Federalist Papers*: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary." The essay argues that the constitutional design must provide "auxiliary precautions" — structural mechanisms that constrain power even when the people who hold power are not virtuous.

Madison's argument proceeds in three steps: (1) Each branch of government must have "the necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist encroachments of the others" — structural capacity to check other branches. (2) "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition" — the self-interest of officeholders must be harnessed to defend institutional boundaries. (3) "The interest of the man must be connected with the constitutional rights of the place" — personal incentives must align with institutional responsibilities.

This is institutional design as engineering — the systematic construction of structures that produce desirable outcomes (liberty, justice, the common good) not despite human imperfection but through it. CED-AH's design imperatives function analogously: Authority Retention prevents the concentration of decision-making power in computational systems (separation of powers between human and machine); Transparency ensures that no actor (developer, deployer, or system) can operate without accountability (checks and balances through information symmetry); Reversibility ensures that no action is irrevocable (the constitutional capacity to undo and override); Capability Growth ensures that users' competence increases rather than atrophies (preventing the institutional decay that Madison warns against in Federalist 51's discussion of departmental dependency).

**Federalist No. 70: Energy in the Executive.** Hamilton argues that an effective government requires a unified, energetic executive — a single person with clear authority and clear accountability. A plural executive (multiple people sharing executive power) diffuses responsibility, making it impossible to hold anyone accountable for failures. Unity, Hamilton argues, provides: (1) decision-making capacity (one person can decide faster than a committee), (2) activity (the ability to act with dispatch when circumstances require), (3) secrecy (when appropriate), and (4) accountability (the public knows who is responsible).

Hamilton's argument about accountability is directly relevant to CED-AH. One of the problems with delegating decisions to computational systems is that it diffuses accountability: when "the AI recommended it" becomes the justification for a decision, no human is clearly responsible. Hamilton would recognize this as the defect of a plural executive magnified: not merely multiple humans sharing authority but a non-human entity that cannot be held accountable absorbing authority. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative can be read as Hamilton's unity-of-executive principle applied to the human-AI relationship: decision-making authority must reside with an identifiable human who can be held accountable, not with a system that diffuses responsibility into algorithmic opacity.

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Provides the most detailed historical model** for CED-AH's approach to institutional design as a safeguard against human imperfection. Madison's insight that "if men were angels, no government would be necessary" — that institutional design must account for the reality of self-interest, cognitive limitation, and the tendency toward power concentration — is the philosophical foundation of CED-AH's structural approach. CED-AH does not assume that developers will always prioritize human flourishing, that deployers will resist the pressure to automate, or that users will maintain critical engagement. It designs structural safeguards — imperatives, measurement frameworks, contestability mechanisms — that produce coherent outcomes even when individual actors fall short. This is Madison's constitutionalism applied to computational systems.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's design imperatives as a system of checks and balances. The five AH design imperatives function as a system of constitutional checks: Authority Retention prevents the concentration of decision-making power (separation of powers); Transparency prevents asymmetric information (accountability mechanisms); Capability Growth prevents dependency (maintaining institutional vitality); Calibrated Honesty prevents manipulation (information integrity); Reversibility prevents lock-in (the capacity to correct and override). Just as Madison designed the Constitution so that "ambition counteracts ambition," CED-AH designs its imperatives so that each one reinforces the others and compensates for the failure modes the others cannot prevent alone.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's accountability requirements through Hamilton's unity-of-executive argument. Hamilton's insistence that effective governance requires clear accountability — that authority must be traceable to identifiable human decision-makers — provides the political-philosophical foundation for CED-AH's claim that "the AI recommended it" is never a sufficient justification. If no identifiable human can explain and defend a decision, the decision lacks legitimate authority. CED-AH's Authority Retention imperative operationalizes this: every consequential decision must have a human author who can be held accountable.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's concern about "factions" in AI development through Federalist No. 10. Madison's analysis of factions — groups united by shared interest that may conflict with the common good — applies to the AI development community. Companies, research labs, and advocacy groups in AI each constitute factions with particular interests (market share, publication, ideological commitment) that may or may not align with human flourishing. CED-AH's framework is, among other things, a structural remedy for the problem of AI factions: it provides principles and measurement criteria that can evaluate any AI system regardless of which faction produced it, just as Madison's extended republic provides structural remedies for faction regardless of which faction is dominant.

**Qualifies** CED-AH by demonstrating the limits of structural design. The American constitutional system, despite its sophisticated separation of powers and checks and balances, has not prevented failures of governance — including slavery, civil war, corruption, and institutional decay. Structure is necessary but not sufficient; it must be supplemented by civic culture, institutional norms, and ongoing vigilance. CED-AH should not present its structural safeguards as self-executing guarantees. They require, as Madison acknowledged, the sustained commitment of the people who operate within them.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Moderate

**Justification:** The *Federalist Papers* are the most influential single work of constitutional design theory and provide a sophisticated philosophical account of how institutional structures can safeguard liberty and the common good against the realities of human imperfection. Madison's arguments about factions, separation of powers, and designing for imperfect agents are directly applicable to CED-AH's structural approach to AI governance. Hamilton's accountability argument directly supports Authority Retention. The rating is "Moderate" rather than "Strong" because: (1) the *Federalist Papers* address political institutions, not computational systems — the extension is analogical; (2) the American constitutional system they designed has experienced significant failures, complicating the claim that structural design is sufficient; (3) the 18th-century political context differs substantially from the 21st-century technological context; and (4) the essays are advocacy documents (written to persuade New Yorkers to ratify the Constitution), not detached philosophical analyses, which introduces potential rhetorical bias.

## Limitations

- **Advocacy documents, not philosophy.** The *Federalist Papers* were written to persuade, not merely to analyze. They present the proposed Constitution in its most favorable light and may understate its weaknesses. CED-AH should draw on their philosophical insights while acknowledging their rhetorical context.
- **Political institutions, not computational systems.** The *Federalist Papers* address the design of government among human citizens. The extension to human-AI systems requires substantial interpretive work. Whether "checks and balances" can be meaningfully implemented in computational architecture is a design question, not a settled principle.
- **Historical failures.** The constitutional system the *Federalist Papers* defended did not prevent slavery, civil war, Jim Crow, or numerous other institutional failures. Structural design is necessary but not sufficient for justice and flourishing. CED-AH should not overstate the power of structural safeguards.
- **Exclusionary context.** The Constitution the Federalists defended originally excluded women, enslaved people, and Native Americans from full citizenship. The political philosophy of the *Federalist Papers* was formulated within and partly in service of these exclusions. CED-AH's universalist framework implicitly corrects these exclusions but should acknowledge them.
- **The faction analogy has limits.** Madison's faction analysis assumes a political marketplace of competing interests within a shared constitutional framework. Whether the AI development ecosystem constitutes a "republic" in Madison's sense — with shared constitutional commitments and mechanisms for faction management — is doubtful. The faction analogy illuminates but may also mislead.
- **Scale and speed differences.** Constitutional checks and balances operate on the timescale of legislative sessions, judicial proceedings, and electoral cycles. Computational systems operate on the timescale of milliseconds. Whether structural safeguards designed for human-speed governance can function at computational speed is an engineering question that the *Federalist Papers* do not and cannot address.
