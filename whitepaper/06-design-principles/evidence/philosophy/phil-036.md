## Summary

Shannon Vallor's *Technology and the Virtues* is the most important work applying virtue ethics to the question of how emerging technologies — including AI, robotics, social media, and surveillance systems — affect human character and flourishing. Where most technology ethics focuses on consequences (will this technology cause harm?) or rules (should this technology be permitted or banned?), Vallor asks a different question: what kind of people are we becoming through our use of these technologies, and is the trajectory one we should want?

Vallor develops the concept of "technomoral virtues" — character traits that are both enabled by and necessary for living well with modern technology. Drawing on three major virtue traditions (Aristotelian, Confucian, and Buddhist), she identifies a set of technomoral virtues that converge across traditions: honesty, self-control, humility, justice, courage, empathy, care, civility, flexibility, perspective, magnanimity, and technomoral wisdom (the overarching capacity to discern what living well with technology requires in specific situations).

Central to Vallor's argument is the claim that technology is not merely a tool we use but a force that shapes who we are. Social media changes how we relate to others and to ourselves. AI assistants change how we think, decide, and remember. Surveillance systems change our experience of privacy, autonomy, and trust. These changes are not incidental side effects but constitutive transformations of human character and human community. A technology ethics that ignores these transformations misses the most important ethical dimension.

Vallor is particularly concerned with "moral deskilling" — the degradation of moral capabilities through technological substitution. When GPS navigates for us, our spatial reasoning atrophies. When AI recommends decisions for us, our judgment atrophies. When algorithms curate our information environment, our critical thinking atrophies. Vallor argues that the atrophy of moral capacities is a distinctive and underappreciated risk of technology: even technologies that produce good outcomes in the short term may be harmful if they degrade the human capabilities needed for long-term flourishing.

The book is notable for its cross-cultural depth (drawing seriously on Confucian and Buddhist as well as Aristotelian virtue traditions), its philosophical rigor, and its practical orientation (she provides specific guidance for technology designers, educators, and policymakers).

## Relevance to CED-AH

**Directly supports** CED-AH's Design Imperative 2 (Capability Growth: users become more capable over time, not dependent). Vallor's concept of "moral deskilling" is the philosophical articulation of the failure mode CED-AH's second imperative is designed to prevent. CED-AH requires that computational systems amplify human capability rather than substitute for it; Vallor provides the ethical argument for why substitution is harmful — it degrades the very capabilities (judgment, reasoning, moral discernment) that human flourishing requires.

**Directly supports** CED-AH's core distinction between amplification and replacement. Vallor's analysis of how technologies transform human character maps directly onto CED-AH's S4 (amplification vs. substitution). Technologies that amplify human capabilities (enabling better judgment, deeper understanding, more informed decisions) serve flourishing; technologies that substitute for human capabilities (making decisions for us, curating our world, replacing our judgment) undermine it. Vallor provides the virtue-theoretic grounding for this distinction.

**Supports by analogy** CED-AH's measurement framework. Vallor's technomoral virtues — honesty, self-control, humility, justice, courage, empathy — map onto the human flourishing dimensions CED-AH measures: understanding, capability, agency, and meaning. If computational systems degrade these virtues (as Vallor's moral deskilling thesis suggests they can), the Coherence Dashboard should register the degradation as a decline in human coherence.

**Qualifies** CED-AH's coherentist epistemology. Vallor's virtue ethics is not coherentist — it is grounded in an account of human nature and human flourishing that is teleological (what are humans *for*? what does a good human life look like?). This introduces a different philosophical register than CED-AH's coherentism. However, the two approaches may be compatible: CED-AH's S2 (flourishing requires agency, capability, understanding, meaning) is implicitly a teleological claim about what human beings need, and Vallor's virtue ethics provides a rich philosophical tradition for developing this claim.

## Strength Assessment

**Rating:** Strong

**Justification:** Vallor's work is the leading contribution to virtue ethics and technology, has been widely cited and influential in technology ethics, and directly addresses the human-capability concerns at the heart of CED-AH's design imperatives. The cross-cultural depth (three virtue traditions converging on similar conclusions) strengthens the argument by showing it does not depend on any single cultural framework.

## Limitations

- **Virtue ethics is contested.** Not all ethicists accept the virtue-theoretic framework. Consequentialists argue that character is less important than outcomes; deontologists argue that duties and rights are more fundamental than virtues. CED-AH's use of Vallor commits it to the importance of character and capability, which is contestable.
- **"Moral deskilling" is empirically underdetermined.** Vallor's claim that technology degrades moral capabilities is intuitively plausible but not empirically demonstrated with rigor. The evidence that GPS degrades spatial reasoning, or that AI assistants degrade judgment, is suggestive but not conclusive. CED-AH's measurement framework should be designed to test this claim, not merely assume it.
- **The teleological framework has limits.** Vallor's virtue ethics presupposes that human beings have a telos (a natural purpose or function) and that the good life consists in fulfilling that telos. This metaphysical commitment is not shared by all philosophical traditions. CED-AH should consider whether its flourishing claims can be stated without teleological assumptions.
- **No formal measurement framework.** Vallor provides philosophical guidance but not the operational metrics CED-AH's Coherence Dashboard requires. The gap between "cultivate technomoral wisdom" and "measure coherence on a five-dimensional scale" is significant.
- **Focus on individual character.** Vallor's analysis is primarily about how technology affects individual character and individual flourishing. CED-AH is also concerned with institutional, social, and civilizational coherence. The extension from individual virtue to systemic coherence requires bridging.
